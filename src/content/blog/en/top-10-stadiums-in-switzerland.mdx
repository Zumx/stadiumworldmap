Swiss football's stadium picture was redefined by UEFA Euro 2008, co-hosted with Austria. Basel, Bern, Geneva and Zurich each received a tournament-standard ground, and the Swiss Super League clubs have continued upgrading regional venues. The Swiss model differs from many European countries: compact, well-maintained grounds in the 20,000–40,000 range that serve a top division of just ten teams. Every stadium is on the [map](/map).

## 1. St. Jakob-Park, Basel

Capacity: 38,512. FC Basel's home, opened in 2001 and significantly expanded for Euro 2008. The Herzog & de Meuron-designed stadium — by the same Basel-based practice that built the Allianz Arena and Beijing Bird's Nest — combines residential apartments along the south side with the football bowl itself. The exterior fabric facade is lit from within. FC Basel has been Switzerland's dominant club of the past 20 years with 21 Super League titles. The ground hosted Euro 2008 group matches and a semi-final.

## 2. Stade de Suisse (Stadion Wankdorf), Bern

Capacity: 31,789 (Super League configuration). BSC Young Boys' home on the Wankdorf site, where Switzerland hosted the 1954 World Cup final (West Germany 3 Hungary 2 — the Miracle of Bern). The original Wankdorf was demolished in 2001 and the current building, opened in 2005, sits on the same plot. The roof is covered with solar panels — one of Europe's first stadiums to deliver electricity to the grid from its own roof. Young Boys have dominated the Swiss Super League since 2018 with multiple titles.

## 3. Stade de Genève, Geneva

Capacity: 30,084. Servette FC's home in the Lancy district of Geneva, opened in 2003 and used for Euro 2008. The stadium has been Servette's primary venue throughout the club's various financial struggles and renaissances. Servette won the Swiss championship in 1998-99 and has returned to consistent competitiveness in recent years. The ground hosts Geneva's main football fixtures and occasional Swiss national team matches.

## 4. Letzigrund, Zurich

Capacity: 26,000. Shared by Grasshopper Club Zürich and FC Zürich, completely rebuilt and reopened in 2007 for Euro 2008. The athletics track is retained — this is one of the few major European football grounds where the running track survived modernisation — because Letzigrund continues to host the annual Weltklasse Zürich athletics meeting, one of the most important Diamond League events. The Zurich derby between GC and FCZ is played here multiple times per season.

## 5. Tissot Arena, Biel/Bienne

Capacity: 6,000. Home of FC Biel/Bienne in the bilingual French-German Swiss city. The compact modern bowl, opened in 2015, represents the smaller end of Swiss club football. FC Biel/Bienne plays in the second division, but the ground regularly hosts cup fixtures and visiting teams.

## 6. Stockhorn Arena, Thun

Capacity: 10,000. FC Thun's home in the Bernese Oberland, opened in 2011. The location is among the most spectacular in European football — the Stockhorn mountain rises directly behind the south stand, with the Thunersee lake visible from the upper tiers. The ground is named after the Stockhorn peak. FC Thun reached the UEFA Champions League group stage in 2005-06, a remarkable achievement for a club from a town of 45,000.

## 7. Brügglifeld, Aarau

Capacity: 9,249. FC Aarau's compact historic ground, opened in 1924 and modernised in stages. Aarau won the Swiss championship in 1992-93 — the most recent title for a club outside the FC Basel / Young Boys / Zurich axis. The ground is among the most architecturally traditional in Swiss football, with original concrete tribunes still in use.

## 8. Schützenwiese, Winterthur

Capacity: 8,500. FC Winterthur's home, opened originally in 1903 and renovated multiple times. One of the oldest continuously used football grounds in Europe. FC Winterthur returned to the Swiss Super League in 2022 after decades in lower divisions; the modest ground reflects the club's traditional scale.

## 9. Stade de la Pontaise, Lausanne

Capacity: 12,544 (decommissioned 2020). Lausanne-Sport's historic home, in use from 1904 to 2020. Lausanne moved to the new Stade de la Tuilière in 2020. The Pontaise hosted the 1954 World Cup final's pre-tournament matches and is one of the most historically rich grounds in Swiss football. The new Tuilière ground is a step into the modern era.

## 10. Stade de la Tuilière, Lausanne

Capacity: 12,544. Lausanne-Sport's new home, opened in 2020 with a contemporary design featuring a thin metal-mesh facade and steep tribunes close to the pitch. The compact, modern bowl is a significant upgrade from the Pontaise and represents the current direction of Swiss club stadium development.

## Euro 2008 and the Swiss legacy

The St. Jakob-Park (Basel), Stade de Suisse (Bern), Stade de Genève (Geneva) and Letzigrund (Zurich) hosted Euro 2008 group matches and knockout fixtures. The Swiss tournament infrastructure has been used continuously since by Super League clubs, the Swiss national team, and Champions League and Europa League matches involving Swiss clubs. Unlike many countries' tournament hosting legacies, the Swiss grounds have all maintained regular high-level use.

## The Swiss Super League

The Swiss Super League is a ten-team competition with a quadruple round-robin (each team plays the others four times) producing 36 league matches per season. FC Basel and BSC Young Boys have dominated the past two decades, with FC Zürich, Grasshoppers, Lugano, St. Gallen, Servette and Lausanne-Sport rounding out the competitive ecosystem. The league produces Champions League and Europa League qualifiers regularly, with Swiss clubs occasionally progressing to knockout rounds.

## Swiss football culture

Swiss football culture varies between the German-speaking and French-speaking regions. Basel, Zurich, Bern and St. Gallen produce the most consistent crowds in the German-speaking areas, while Geneva, Lausanne and Lugano (Italian-speaking) carry the Latin Switzerland football tradition. The national team has been a regular World Cup and European Championship participant in the past 15 years, reaching the quarter-final of Euro 2020. Every venue listed is on the [map](/map).
