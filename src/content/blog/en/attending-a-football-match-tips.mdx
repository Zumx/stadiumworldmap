Going to a football match is one of sport's great experiences — the noise, the colour, the collective tension. It is also a logistical exercise that rewards preparation. Buy the wrong ticket from the wrong source, arrive at the wrong time, or carry the wrong bag and you will spend the first half queuing outside. This guide covers every step from purchase to the journey home.

## Where to buy your ticket

The safest starting point is always the club's official website or box office. Clubs sell direct, allocate you a valid barcode, and offer some protection if the match is postponed. Season-ticket holder resale portals operated by the clubs — such as Manchester United's official resale platform or FC Barcelona's licensed exchange — are the next-safest tier. Your name is usually registered against the ticket, which protects against duplicates.

Third-party resellers carry genuine risk. Viagogo, StubHub, and similar platforms operate legally in many countries but are not endorsed by most top clubs, and UEFA and national associations have explicitly warned fans against them. Tickets bought through these channels may be duplicates, may be in the wrong name for ID-checked venues, and often arrive at inflated prices. If you do use a resale platform, choose one that guarantees a refund if the ticket is invalid at the gate. For major European finals or Champions League knockouts, the counterfeit risk rises sharply.

Buying in person at the box office remains an underused option. Many grounds sell remaining tickets on matchday for league games, and the transaction is straightforward. Bring a credit card — cash windows have largely disappeared at top-flight venues.

## How early to arrive

For a Premier League fixture at a 50,000-seat ground, arriving 45 minutes before kickoff is comfortable. For a Champions League group stage match at Old Trafford, the Nou Camp, or the Bernabéu, plan on two hours early. Security queues at those venues are long even when they move efficiently, and any bag check, prohibited-item debate, or barcode problem compounds the delay. Champions League knockout matches and domestic cup finals at Wembley or the Stade de France regularly see gates clog 90 minutes before kickoff.

In Germany, Bundesliga grounds with large standing sections — Signal Iduna Park in Dortmund, the Allianz Arena in Munich — fill quickly because standing areas have no assigned seat to find. Arrive early, claim a spot on the Südtribüne or equivalent, and the pre-match atmosphere rewards you for it.

## ID requirements

Several countries now require photo ID for match entry, particularly in rivalry fixtures. In Italy, Serie A matches involving clubs with a history of supporter incidents require a Tessera del Tifoso (fan card) or equivalent document for home supporters, and many grounds request ID alongside tickets at the turnstile. In Spain, El Clásico and other high-risk fixtures operate under a named-ticket regime where the name on the ticket must match a government-issued document. Take your passport or national ID card to any match in Italy or Spain without exception.

In England, ID is not yet mandatory for most league matches, but alcohol purchase at stadiums requires proof of age, and Wembley has trialled named ticketing for selected events. The direction of travel across Europe is toward named tickets; keep your ID accessible.

## Prohibited items

The list of banned items has grown at most top European grounds and is worth checking on the relevant club's website before you leave home. Standard prohibitions across nearly all grounds include:

Selfie sticks and monopods are banned universally at UEFA-sanctioned matches and at most Premier League, La Liga, Bundesliga, and Serie A grounds. Stewards confiscate them at the gate without exception.

Large camera equipment — meaning detachable lenses, professional bodies, and anything that looks like broadcast equipment — requires a media accreditation that members of the public do not hold. A phone camera or a small compact is fine.

Large bags are subject to maximum-size rules that vary by ground but are typically stricter than airline cabin-baggage limits. Many top grounds now impose a policy of no bags larger than A4 (roughly 30 x 21 cm) or similar. If you need to carry more, use an official bag drop if one is available, or leave your bag at your hotel.

Alcohol, flares, pyrotechnics, and objects that could be used as projectiles are banned everywhere. Despite this, pyrotechnics appear regularly in ultras sections, particularly in Germany, Italy, and Turkey, where enforcement in the away end can be looser.

## Getting through the turnstile

Scan your ticket barcode before you reach the turnstile — have it open and brightness turned up on your phone, or hold a printed ticket at the ready. At older grounds with manual ticket checks, hand the ticket face-up. If your barcode fails to scan, do not force through; step to the side and speak to a steward immediately. Duplicate-ticket issues are resolved at the gate, not by pushing.

Once inside, familiarise yourself with the block and row layout at your seat. At grounds with standing sections, there is no assigned position. In seated areas, occupying a wrong seat causes friction, particularly in blocks where season-ticket holders have sat in the same seat for years.

## Away colours and visitor advice

Check the home club's policy on away colours before you travel. At most grounds in England, Germany, France, and Spain, wearing a visiting team's scarf or shirt is accepted in the designated away end and creates no problems. Wearing away colours in the home end is a different matter — stewards will typically relocate or eject you, and in derbies or high-tension fixtures the risk of confrontation with home supporters is real. In Istanbul derbies between Besiktas, Fenerbahce, and Galatasaray, away supporters are often not admitted at all.

## The post-match transport crush

Plan your exit before the final whistle. At grounds like Wembley (90,000 capacity), the post-match exodus onto the London Underground is a slow press of bodies that takes up to an hour to clear. Leaving three to five minutes before the match ends reduces the queue significantly without missing the result.

At Allianz Arena, special S-Bahn trains run to Munich city centre from the dedicated Froettmaning U-Bahn station. At the Stade de France, RER B and D lines run extra services but still fill quickly after a full house. At Old Trafford, walking to Oxford Road or Deansgate station is faster than waiting at the Trafford Park tram stop immediately post-match.

Rideshare apps operate near most grounds but drivers avoid the immediate post-match crush; arrange a pickup a few streets away from the stadium exit. Walking to a quieter nearby stop for public transport almost always beats waiting at the designated stadium station.
