Japan's football infrastructure was transformed by the 2002 FIFA World Cup, which the country co-hosted with South Korea and which prompted the construction or renovation of ten stadiums across the country. The 2020 Tokyo Olympics further added the new Japan National Stadium designed by Kengo Kuma. The result is a country with several world-class venues and a well-distributed network of modern club grounds. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Nissan Stadium (International Stadium Yokohama)

Capacity: 72,327. The largest stadium in Japan and the venue for the 2002 World Cup final (Brazil 2 Germany 0). Home to Yokohama F. Marinos in the J1 League, Japan's top division. The elliptical bowl design by Nikken Sekkei opened in 1998 and hosted the 2002 World Cup final and several quarter and semifinal matches. Japan national team fixtures are regularly held here. The ground is connected to Shin-Yokohama station by a short walk, making access straightforward despite its scale.

## 2. Saitama Stadium 2002

Capacity: 63,700. The J1 League's largest dedicated football stadium, home to Urawa Red Diamonds. Built specifically for the 2002 World Cup — the name reflects its purpose — Saitama Stadium held several group stage and knockout matches. Urawa's supporter culture is among the most intense in Japanese football, and the ground on a sold-out J1 or AFC Champions League night produces an atmosphere comparable to mid-table European top-division grounds. The stadium hosted football events at the Tokyo 2020 Olympics.

## 3. Japan National Stadium (New National Stadium), Tokyo

Capacity: 68,000. Designed by Kengo Kuma and completed in 2019 at a cost of approximately 157 billion yen, the Japan National Stadium replaced Kenzo Tange's 1964 Olympic stadium on the same site in Kasumigaoka. The design uses wood and greenery extensively — eaves clad in Japanese cedar and larch reference traditional Japanese architecture while reducing heat. The stadium hosted the opening and closing ceremonies and athletics for the Tokyo 2020 Olympics (held in 2021 due to COVID-19 postponement), as well as the Olympic football final. It serves as Japan's national stadium for football and rugby union.

## 4. Tokyo Stadium (Ajinomoto Stadium / Japan National Stadium Chofu)

Capacity: 49,970. FC Tokyo and Tokyo Verdy's home in Chofu, in western Tokyo. The stadium opened in 2001 and hosted 2002 World Cup matches. It is Japan's most active club ground in terms of multi-code use — FC Tokyo, Tokyo Verdy, and the Japan women's national team (Nadeshiko Japan) all use it regularly. AFC Champions League group stage matches have been held here. The bowl is functional and well-maintained.

## 5. Sapporo Dome

Capacity: 41,580 for football; 42,122 for baseball. The Sapporo Dome uses an unusual technical solution for its multi-sport use: the natural grass football pitch is grown outside the dome and rolled inside on an air-cushion system for matches, while baseball uses an artificial turf surface permanently installed inside. The pitch-rolling system — the Hovering Stage — takes approximately two and a half hours to complete. Consadole Sapporo plays football here and the Hokkaido Nippon-Ham Fighters played baseball here until 2023. The 2002 World Cup was hosted here and the dome's distinctive UFO-like profile makes it Sapporo's most recognisable building.

## 6. Toyota Stadium

Capacity: 45,000. Nagoya Grampus' home in Toyota City, opened in 2001 for the 2002 World Cup. The Toyota Motor Corporation's headquarters city funded the ground substantially. The roof and enclosed design make it one of Japan's loudest club venues. Nagoya Grampus won the J1 League in 2010 under former England manager Dragan Stojkovic.

## 7. Panasonic Stadium Suita (Gamba Osaka)

Capacity: 39,694. Gamba Osaka's purpose-built home in Suita, opened in 2016, is one of Japan's most modern club stadiums. The enclosed design, steep stands, and proximity to the pitch create an atmosphere well above average for Japanese club football. Gamba won the J1 League, Emperor's Cup, and FUJI XEROX Super Cup treble in 2014. The stadium was funded without public money, a model similar to Juventus' Allianz Stadium and rare in Japan.

## 8. Kashima Soccer Stadium

Capacity: 40,728. Home to Kashima Antlers — Japan's most successful J1 League club with eight titles — in the coastal city of Kashima in Ibaraki Prefecture. The stadium is relatively remote for a top-division ground, reflecting the J-League's original strategy of expanding football to non-metropolitan areas. Kashima Antlers are associated with Sumitomo Metal Industries and the company town origins of the club give it a specific corporate-community character. The 2002 World Cup group stage was held here.

## 9. Nack5 Stadium Omiya

Capacity: 15,500. Omiya Ardija's compact home in Saitama City is one of J1's smaller venues but consistently creates a strong atmosphere for its size. Omiya's yo-yo career between J1 and J2 has given the ground exposure at both levels.

## 10. Noevir Stadium Kobe

Capacity: 30,132. Vissel Kobe's home, renovated and expanded from an earlier Misaki Park Stadium structure. Vissel Kobe attracted Andres Iniesta as a player from 2018 to 2023, which brought international attention to the club and the ground. Kobe won the Emperor's Cup in 2019 and the J1 League in 2023 — their first league title. The stadium is centrally located in the Misaki Park complex.

## Japan's J-League stadium model

The J-League was founded in 1993 with the explicit goal of distributing football across Japan's regions, which meant many clubs were based in cities without the largest populations. The stadium network reflects this — Kashima, Albirex Niigata, Gamba Osaka — built in locations that prioritised geographic spread. The result is a league with genuinely diverse geography and a range of ground types from the largest 2002 World Cup venues down to compact city-centre grounds of around 15,000 capacity.

## The J-League's commercial development

The J-League's model — named shirt sponsorships on club kits from 1993, stadium naming rights partnerships, broadcast deals expanding to DAZN from 2017 — has produced one of Asia's most commercially sophisticated domestic football competitions. The DAZN streaming deal, worth approximately 210 billion yen over ten years from 2017, transformed the league's revenue basis. The AFC Champions League Elite (restructured for 2024-25) has given Japan's top clubs a more competitive East Asian club tournament framework, with Urawa Red Diamonds, Gamba Osaka, and Kashima Antlers competing regularly.

## Visiting Japanese grounds

Japan's combination of excellent public transport, high stadium safety standards, and visitor-friendly matchday culture makes it one of the easiest football environments in the world to attend a match. Tickets are available through the J-League official ticket platform and at convenience stores (Lawson, FamilyMart). Matchday atmosphere varies significantly by club: Urawa at Saitama Stadium produces genuine noise; other clubs in smaller cities are quieter but equally welcoming. The grounds on this list are located across the country — the [map](/map) shows the full distribution from Sapporo in the north to Kobe and Oita in the south, along with rail connections from Japan's Shinkansen network.
