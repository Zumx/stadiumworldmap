The Westfalenstadion, known under its naming-rights deal as Signal Iduna Park, is the home of Borussia Dortmund and the largest Bundesliga stadium by capacity. The figure most often quoted — 81,365 — is itself a partial summary of the ground's identity, because nearly a third of that crowd stands in one continuous block on the south terrace, the Südtribüne, universally known as the Gelbe Wand. The ground sits in the Dortmund suburb of Strobelallee, easily found on the [map](/map).

## A stadium built for a World Cup

The Westfalenstadion was constructed in 1971-1974 for the 1974 FIFA World Cup, replacing the older Stadion Rote Erde next door, which Dortmund had outgrown. The original design was simple and rectangular — four straight stands, each a single rake of terracing — and held just under 54,000. The closeness of the stands to the pitch was deliberate from the start, and that proximity has survived every redevelopment since.

## Expansion for the 2006 World Cup

For the 2006 FIFA World Cup, the Westfalenstadion was expanded in stages between 1995 and 2003 from 42,800 to 81,264, with corner sections filled in and a second tier added on the east, west, and north stands. Crucially the south stand was kept as a single tier — the largest standing terrace in European football — which preserved the ground's atmospheric character and gave the Yellow Wall its modern shape. The 2006 World Cup saw Germany play four matches at the stadium, including the semi-final against Italy.

## The Yellow Wall

The Südtribüne — 24,454 standing supporters under one roof — is the largest single-tier terrace in European football and the visual signature of German football. When Dortmund's supporters raise their scarves before kick-off and sing the club anthem, the entire south end becomes a wall of yellow and black, a sight that has been televised into a global emblem. For Champions League matches the standing terrace is converted to seats, reducing capacity to 65,829, but the Bundesliga configuration restores the wall.

## The choreographies

Dortmund's ultra groups, primarily The Unity and Desperados, produce some of European football's most ambitious tifo displays. Bundesliga matches against Bayern Munich, the Revierderby against Schalke, and Champions League knock-out nights have featured elaborate full-stand mosaics, descending banners, and pyrotechnic sequences (the latter often resulting in DFL fines that are absorbed as the cost of the choreography).

## The Revierderby

Although Schalke's relegation has interrupted the rhythm, the Revierderby between Dortmund and Gelsenkirchen-based Schalke 04 remains one of European football's most-charged fixtures. The two clubs are separated by 30 kilometres in the Ruhr industrial heartland, and matches at the Westfalenstadion involve months of choreography preparation, heightened policing, and the inevitable parsing of every result by both sets of supporters.

## Champions League nights

Dortmund's Champions League home record at the Westfalenstadion is one of the best in European football, with the noise level and visual intensity of the Yellow Wall regularly cited by visiting players and coaches as among the most difficult atmospheres in Europe. The 2012-13 run to the final at Wembley, the 2023-24 run to the final (again at Wembley), and the recurring knock-out ties against Spanish, Italian, and English opposition have made the stadium a regular venue for European football's biggest nights.

## The naming rights

The Signal Iduna naming-rights deal, signed in 2005 and repeatedly extended, attaches a Dortmund-based insurance group to the stadium name. Many Dortmund supporters continue to refer to the ground simply as the Westfalenstadion — partly out of preference, partly because UEFA's neutral-naming policy means European matches are played at the Westfalenstadion regardless of the commercial title.

## Stadium tours and the BVB Museum

The BORUSSEUM, the club's official museum, sits within the stadium complex and traces Dortmund's history from the 1909 founding through the European Cup of 1997 and the Klopp-era Bundesliga titles of 2011 and 2012. Stadium tours run daily and include the dressing rooms, the player tunnel, and the south stand itself — standing on the Yellow Wall when it is empty conveys something of the scale that match-day photographs cannot.
