Norwegian football is structured around modest-capacity grounds that produce some of the strongest matchday atmospheres in northern Europe. The Eliteserien runs from late March or April through November, similar to Sweden's, with most clubs operating in cities of relatively small population. The country's stadium investment has focused on quality and accessibility rather than scale. Every venue is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Ullevaal Stadion, Oslo

Capacity: 27,200. Norway's national stadium in the Sogn district of Oslo, opened in 1926 and progressively expanded and modernised since. The current configuration dates from a 1998 renovation, with further improvements over the years. Ullevaal hosts the Norwegian national team and the Norwegian Cup Final each year. Vålerenga used the ground as a temporary home for many years before building their own Intility Arena.

## 2. Lerkendal, Trondheim

Capacity: 21,166. Rosenborg BK's home in Trondheim, opened in 1947 and substantially modernised through the 1990s and 2000s. Lerkendal is one of Northern Europe's most historically rich football grounds, having hosted Rosenborg's golden era — the club won 13 consecutive Eliteserien titles between 1992 and 2004 — and many of their Champions League appearances. Rosenborg's qualification for the Champions League group stage in 1996-97 (reaching the quarter-finals) remains a high point of Norwegian club football.

## 3. Brann Stadion, Bergen

Capacity: 17,824. SK Brann's home in the Western Norwegian city of Bergen, opened in 1919 (the oldest continuously used Norwegian football ground) and modernised in stages. Brann's supporter culture is among the most intense in Scandinavia, with the standing Spissen section producing distinctive matchday atmospheres. Bergen's coastal location and the Fløyen hill rising behind the south stand make Brann Stadion one of the most scenically located grounds in Northern Europe.

## 4. Aker Stadion, Molde

Capacity: 11,167. Molde FK's home, opened in 1998 with private financing from local businessman Kjell Inge Røkke. Molde's emergence as a regular Eliteserien title contender in the 2010s — championships in 2011, 2012, 2014 and 2019 under Ole Gunnar Solskjær — has made Aker Stadion one of Norway's most consistently successful grounds. The compact rectangular bowl with steep tribunes produces good atmospheres.

## 5. SR-Bank Arena, Stavanger

Capacity: 16,300. Viking FK's home in the Western Norwegian oil city of Stavanger, opened in 2004 to replace the older Viking Stadion. Viking are one of Norway's traditionally strong clubs, with eight Eliteserien titles, although the title era has been more distant. The modern stadium represents the typical scale of Norwegian provincial top-flight football.

## 6. Color Line Stadion, Ålesund

Capacity: 10,778. Aalesunds FK's home on the western coast, opened in 2005. The stadium's position with the Atlantic and the surrounding mountains visible from the upper tiers gives it one of the most distinctive natural settings in Norwegian football. Ålesund have oscillated between the Eliteserien and the first division.

## 7. Aspmyra Stadion, Bodø

Capacity: 8,270. FK Bodø/Glimt's home in northern Norway, north of the Arctic Circle. Bodø/Glimt's emergence as a Eliteserien title-winning club in 2020, 2021 and 2023 has been one of European football's most surprising success stories. The compact stadium is one of the few European grounds where matches occur near the polar latitudes, with corresponding light conditions in midsummer (constant daylight) and midwinter (minimal sun).

## 8. Intility Arena, Oslo

Capacity: 16,555. Vålerenga's home, opened in 2017 in the Valle Hovin area of eastern Oslo, replacing the temporary ground at Ullevaal that the club had used for several years. The compact rectangular bowl with all-seater tribunes and a steep configuration produces strong matchday acoustics. Vålerenga have not won the Eliteserien since 2005 but maintain a strong supporter base.

## 9. Sør Arena, Kristiansand

Capacity: 14,448. IK Start's home in southern Norway, opened in 2007. The stadium replaced the older Kristiansand Stadion and represents one of the better modern Eliteserien stadium designs. IK Start have been a Eliteserien fixture in past decades, with the 1980 championship win their golden moment.

## 10. Sarpsborg Stadion (Sarpsborg 08 home)

Capacity: 4,966. Sarpsborg 08's home in southeastern Norway. The compact ground is one of the smallest in the Eliteserien but Sarpsborg 08 have been a consistent top-flight presence since their promotion in the early 2010s, including a UEFA Europa League group stage appearance.

## The Eliteserien

The Eliteserien has 16 teams and runs from late March or April through November. FK Bodø/Glimt's recent dominance — Eliteserien champions in 2020, 2021 and 2023 — has broken the traditional concentration around Rosenborg, Molde and Brann. The league has produced regular Europa League and Conference League qualifiers, with Bodø/Glimt's run to the 2024-25 Europa League semi-final being a particularly notable European achievement.

## Norwegian football culture

Norwegian supporter culture has grown substantially in recent decades, with strong tifo traditions at Brann, Vålerenga, Lillestrøm, Rosenborg and Bodø/Glimt. The cold-climate Eliteserien calendar — playing through winter conditions in October and November at northern grounds — produces distinctive matchday experiences not found in southern European football.

## Norwegian football success

Norway's modern football era has been marked by Rosenborg's Champions League runs of the late 1990s and early 2000s and the emergence of individual players like Erling Haaland (whose family roots are in Bryne), Martin Ødegaard, and the women's national team's deep cultural penetration — Norway have won World Cup (1995) and European Championship (1987, 1993) titles in women's football, making them one of the historically dominant nations.
