Australia's stadium landscape reflects the country's unusual sporting culture — cricket and Australian Rules Football share many of the same grounds, rugby league and union compete for venues in Queensland and New South Wales, and football (soccer) has gradually built its own purpose-built infrastructure. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Melbourne Cricket Ground (MCG), Melbourne

Capacity: 100,024. Home to Melbourne Cricket Club for cricket and multiple AFL clubs for Australian Rules Football. Opened 1853 in its first form, comprehensively developed through the twentieth century and rebuilt for the 1956 Olympics. The MCG hosts the AFL Grand Final annually — the defining day of Australia's most popular sport — and Test matches, One Day Internationals, and T20 internationals for cricket. It also hosted the 2006 Commonwealth Games athletics. No venue in the southern hemisphere comes close to its capacity or its range of sporting history. The National Sports Museum is embedded in the ground.

## 2. Stadium Australia (Accor Stadium), Sydney

Capacity: 83,500 for football, reduced from its Olympic capacity of 110,000 by infilling the athletics-track areas. Home to the Australian national football and rugby union teams and the New South Wales State of Origin rugby league fixture. Opened for the 2000 Sydney Olympics and has been in continuous multi-sport use since. The 2000 Olympics closing ceremony at this ground is one of the most fondly remembered in the modern Games' history. The stadium handles AFL Grand Final alternative-venue duties when the MCG is unavailable.

## 3. Adelaide Oval, Adelaide

Capacity: 53,583 for cricket, expanded to approximately 55,000 for football. One of Australia's most beautifully situated grounds, with the River Torrens and St Peter's Cathedral as its backdrop. Hosts both South Australian cricket and Adelaide Crows and Port Adelaide AFL clubs. The Adelaide Oval renovation completed in 2014 added a new northern stand while preserving the heritage scoreboard and surrounds. The combination of cricket and AFL under one management at a picturesque ground makes it the most commonly cited example of successful multi-sport venue management in Australia.

## 4. Optus Stadium, Perth

Capacity: 60,000. Opened in 2018 as one of the most modern sports venues in the world, replacing the defunct WACA ground as Perth's primary cricket and football venue. Designed by HKS Architects, the bowl is tight and steep, with sightlines considered among the best in Australia for both cricket and AFL. Hosts Western Australia cricket, Western Force Super Rugby, Perth Scorchers Big Bash, and AFL matches. The stadium is connected to the CBD by Matagarup Bridge, a pedestrian bridge that has become a city landmark in its own right.

## 5. Marvel Stadium, Melbourne

Capacity: 56,347 with the roof closed. Melbourne's retractable-roof stadium in Docklands has hosted AFL, rugby union, football, boxing, and concerts. The roof, which closes in approximately 10 minutes, makes it one of Australia's most weather-independent venues. Four AFL clubs use it as a home ground. The Docklands location, compared to the MCG's Domain position, gives it different urban connectivity — the surrounding development has grown significantly since the stadium opened in 2000.

## 6. Suncorp Stadium, Brisbane

Capacity: 52,500. Brisbane's primary rugby league and rugby union venue, home to the Queensland Maroons in State of Origin and the Queensland Reds in Super Rugby. Also used for A-League football and the Australian national team. Opened in 2003 on the site of the old Lang Park, Suncorp is consistently rated among the best atmospheres in Australian sport for rugby league. The enclosed design and proximity of the upper tiers to the playing surface are specific strengths.

## 7. Sydney Cricket Ground (SCG), Sydney

Capacity: approximately 48,000 for cricket, varying for other configurations. The SCG is one of Australia's most historically significant sporting venues, with Test cricket played on the ground since 1882. The heritage Members Stand and other Victorian-era structures are listed, and the ground is maintained with care for its historic character. Also hosts Sydney Swans AFL home matches. The juxtaposition of heritage stands and modern facilities gives the SCG a character that newer grounds cannot replicate.

## 8. Allianz Stadium, Sydney

Capacity: 45,500. Rebuilt and reopened in 2022 on the site of the old Sydney Football Stadium in Moore Park. The new Allianz Stadium is purpose-built for football, rugby league, and rugby union — tighter, steeper, and closer to the pitch than its predecessor. Hosts Sydney FC A-League, Sydney Roosters NRL, and New South Wales Waratahs Super Rugby. The reconstruction was controversial for the cost (approximately $830 million) and the demolition of the previous ground, but the finished building has been widely praised for sightline quality.

## 9. GMHBA Stadium, Geelong

Capacity: 36,000. Home to the Geelong Cats AFL club, GMHBA Stadium is one of the better regional AFL venues in Victoria. The Cats are one of the most successful clubs of the modern AFL era (premierships in 2007, 2009, 2011, and 2022), which has given the stadium a regular diet of significant football. Geelong, 75 kilometres southwest of Melbourne, is large enough to sustain a genuine home-ground atmosphere.

## 10. Bellerive Oval (Blundstone Arena), Hobart

Capacity: approximately 20,000. Hobart's primary cricket venue and the gateway to Tasmania's participation in Australian interstate and international cricket. Hosted its first Test match in 2011 (Australia versus South Africa). The ground sits on the Derwent River with views across to Mount Wellington, making it one of the most scenic cricket venues in Australia. Tasmania's ongoing campaign to establish a BBL franchise and full Sheffield Shield status has kept discussion of Bellerive's future capacity alive.

## The AFL Grand Final tradition

The AFL Grand Final at the MCG is one of Australian sport's central occasions. The tradition of playing it at the MCG goes back to 1902 (with interruptions), and the 100,000-capacity bowl on Grand Final day produces one of the southern hemisphere's most impressive sporting spectacles. During COVID-19 in 2020 and 2021, the match was played at the Gabba in Brisbane and Optus Stadium in Perth respectively — the only interruptions to MCG hosting in the modern era.

## Football's growing footprint

Association football (soccer) has historically occupied a smaller share of Australian stadium time than cricket and AFL, but the A-League's growth and a series of high-profile international fixtures — including Australia's 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup matches — have pushed football into a larger role at several venues. Stadium Australia hosted the Women's World Cup final between Spain and England in 2023, which drew 75,784 spectators, the largest crowd for a women's sporting event in Australia's history. The Allianz Stadium rebuild in Sydney reflects football's claim for dedicated infrastructure alongside the rugby codes that have traditionally dominated New South Wales' sporting calendar.

## The 2032 Brisbane Olympics

Australia won the bid to host the 2032 Summer Olympics in Brisbane. The games will be centred on existing venues — the Gabba, Suncorp Stadium, the Queensland Tennis Centre — with some new construction. The MCG will feature in the cricket programme and possibly athletics. For stadium followers, 2032 offers an opportunity to see Brisbane's existing sports infrastructure used at Olympic capacity, and several of the venues listed in this article will host events across the fortnight.

## Planning a ground visit

Australia's stadiums are spread across a continent, so a multi-stadium visit requires either long domestic flights or an acceptance that you are picking a city and staying there. Melbourne — with the MCG, Marvel Stadium, and GMHBA Stadium in Geelong an hour by train — is the densest cluster. Sydney's Accor Stadium, Allianz Stadium, and the SCG are all in Moore Park or Homebush, reachable from Sydney CBD in 20 to 40 minutes.
