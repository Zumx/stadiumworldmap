Finland's stadium landscape is shaped by ice hockey's commercial dominance — the country's largest indoor arenas dwarf its football grounds — and by the historical centrality of athletics. The Helsinki Olympic Stadium remains Finland's most famous outdoor venue, and the country has begun to upgrade its football-specific grounds as the Veikkausliiga (Finnish top division) attracts more sustained attention. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Helsinki Olympic Stadium, Helsinki

Capacity: 36,251 (post-renovation). Built for the cancelled 1940 Olympics and used for the 1952 Helsinki Olympics, the Olympiastadion in the Töölö district is Finland's national stadium and a defining piece of Nordic modernist architecture. The 72-metre tower remains one of Helsinki's most recognisable landmarks. The 2016-2020 renovation modernised the interior while preserving the original Yrjö Lindegren and Toivo Jäntti exterior. The athletics track keeps it from being a football-specific ground, but it hosts Finland national-team football matches and large concerts.

## 2. Bolt Arena (Töölö Football Stadium), Helsinki

Capacity: 10,770. The principal football-specific stadium in Helsinki, opened in 2000 as Finnair Stadium and renamed several times. The home of HJK Helsinki — Finland's most successful club — and a regular European-qualifying venue. The bowl is compact and rectangular, providing the modern football geometry the Olympic Stadium lacks.

## 3. Tammela Stadium, Tampere

Capacity: 8,000. Tampere's main football venue, opened in 2023 as a replacement for the older Tammela ground. The new build serves Ilves Tampere and Tampereen Pallo-Veikot, two of Finland's most consistent Veikkausliiga clubs.

## 4. Ratina Stadium, Tampere

Capacity: 16,800. Tampere's larger multipurpose stadium, with a running track that limits its football functionality but provides Finland's second city with a substantial outdoor venue.

## 5. Veritas Stadium, Turku

Capacity: 9,000. Turku's principal football ground, opened in 2003. The home of TPS Turku (one of the most historic Finnish clubs) and Inter Turku.

## 6. OmaSp Stadion, Seinäjoki

Capacity: 6,000. Seinäjoki Jalkapallokerho (SJK)'s home in the Ostrobothnia region. SJK won the Veikkausliiga in 2015 and the small stadium has hosted European qualifying games.

## 7. Hietalahti Stadium, Vaasa

Capacity: 6,005. Vaasan Palloseura's home on Finland's west coast. The ground has hosted Veikkausliiga fixtures and is one of the more historic Finnish football venues, dating back to 1922.

## 8. Lahden Stadion, Lahti

Capacity: 7,400. FC Lahti's home in the central Finnish city of Lahti. The ground has been used for Veikkausliiga fixtures and is integrated into the larger Lahti sports park.

## 9. Bjørneborg Centralplan, Pori (Karhuhalli)

Capacity: approximately 5,000. The football ground in Pori, hosting FC Jazz and other regional Finnish clubs.

## 10. Kupittaa Stadium, Turku

Capacity: 9,373. The older Turku athletics stadium that has hosted Finnish athletic championships and Finland's national football team during periods when Helsinki was unavailable.

## Veikkausliiga and the Finnish football pyramid

The Veikkausliiga has 12 clubs and runs an April-to-November summer calendar — like most Nordic leagues, Finland plays its season aligned with summer to avoid the harsh winter. HJK Helsinki has dominated the modern era, with KuPS Kuopio, FC Lahti, SJK Seinäjoki, and Inter Turku as the most consistent challengers. The league has produced increasing European-qualifying success, with HJK Helsinki regularly reaching the Conference League group stage and occasional Europa League appearances.

## Finland's emerging national team

Finland's football decline was finally reversed when the national team qualified for Euro 2020 (delayed to 2021), the country's first major tournament. Stars like Teemu Pukki (Norwich City) and Lukáš Hrádecký (Bayer Leverkusen) anchored a generation that finally broke through. The Olympic Stadium has hosted the national team for the biggest fixtures, while the Bolt Arena and Veritas Stadium have served as smaller-game venues. The success has driven investment in football-specific stadiums, with several mid-size grounds in the pipeline. All stadiums are marked on the [map](/map).
