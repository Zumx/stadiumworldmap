Iceland's football story is one of the modern game's most disproportionate success stories. A nation of 380,000 people qualified for Euro 2016 (reaching the quarterfinals after beating England) and the 2018 World Cup, sustained by a network of indoor football halls that allowed year-round development despite the Arctic climate. The stadium picture reflects this — modest in size but functional. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Laugardalsvollur, Reykjavik

Capacity: approximately 9,800. Iceland's national stadium has been the home of the Iceland national team since 1957. The compact open ground in central Reykjavik has hosted the country's most famous matches — including the 2-1 win over England at Euro 2016 (played at the Allianz Riviera in Nice) and the home qualifying matches that built the team. The size reflects Iceland's population.

## 2. Fjolskyldu og Husdyragardurinn (Vikingsvollur), Reykjavik

Capacity: 6,000. The home of Vikingur Reykjavik, the modern dominant club in Icelandic football. Vikingur's UEFA Conference League and Europa League appearances in 2022 and 2023 brought the ground European competition for the first time in modern memory.

## 3. Hlidarendi, Reykjavik

Capacity: approximately 5,500. The home of Valur, one of Iceland's traditional powerhouse clubs. Valur have won the Icelandic championship many times in their history and remain consistent at the top of the Urvalsdeild.

## 4. Vodafonevollurinn, Reykjavik

Capacity: approximately 5,000. The Reykjavik home of KR (Knattspyrnufelag Reykjavikur), Iceland's oldest and most successful club by historical league titles. The ground is in the western Reykjavik district close to Faxa Bay.

## 5. Kopavogsvollurinn, Kopavogur

Capacity: approximately 5,500. The Kopavogur municipal ground used by HK and the Kopavogur clubs. The Reykjavik metropolitan area contains a cluster of clubs in close proximity given the country's population concentration.

## 6. Akureyrarvollur, Akureyri

Capacity: approximately 6,000. The main ground of northern Iceland's largest town. KA Akureyri and Thor Akureyri use the ground, and the northern position gives the venue a distinct geographic identity in Icelandic football.

## 7. Vestmannaeyjavollur, Vestmannaeyjar

Capacity: approximately 4,000. The football ground on the Vestmannaeyjar islands off Iceland's south coast. IBV Vestmannaeyjar use the ground, and the island setting — a 30-minute ferry from Iceland's south coast — gives the club a uniquely insular character.

## 8. Akranesvollur, Akranes

Capacity: approximately 6,000. IA Akranes' home in the small town of Akranes, west of Reykjavik. Akranes have won multiple Icelandic championships and represent one of the country's most consistent football traditions outside the capital.

## 9. Selfossvollur, Selfoss

Capacity: approximately 2,000. The southern Iceland ground used by Selfoss FC and the smaller clubs of the south coast. Selfoss is a 50-minute drive from Reykjavik.

## 10. Egilshollin (indoor venue), Reykjavik

Capacity: 1,500 spectators. The full-size indoor football hall in Reykjavik that allowed Iceland to develop its national-team generation during winter months when outdoor pitches are unusable. The indoor hall network across the country — Reykjavik, Akureyri, Reykjanesbaer — is one of the most-cited explanations for the rise of Icelandic football.

## The indoor football revolution

Iceland's indoor football hall programme, begun in the 1990s, gave the country a competitive advantage that disproportionately small populations could not normally achieve. The halls allowed coaches to deliver year-round structured training to children at every level, regardless of Iceland's nine-month winter. The 2016 Euro qualification was attributed in part to this generation of players who had trained indoors as children.

## Urvalsdeild

The Urvalsdeild — Iceland's top division — has 12 clubs and runs from May to October, the only window when outdoor football is reliably playable. The compressed season produces short, intense competitions in which a small number of consistent clubs alternate the title. Vikingur, KR, Valur, IA Akranes, and FH Hafnarfjordur are the most consistent winners.

## The Iceland national team

The Iceland national team's Euro 2016 quarterfinal run — beating England 2-1 in the round of 16 before losing 5-2 to France in the quarter-final — is the modern peak. The 2018 World Cup, where Iceland drew with Argentina 1-1 in the group stage, was the first appearance for a country of Iceland's size at a World Cup. The post-2018 cycle has been quieter as the generation that produced those results moves through its later years.

## Visiting Icelandic grounds

Reykjavik is the entry point for any Icelandic football visit, with the national stadium and several major club grounds within the city. Hire a car for further visits — Akureyri, Akranes, and Selfoss are all 60-90 minute drives from Reykjavik on Iceland's Ring Road. The compressed Urvalsdeild calendar means visits should be planned for May through October. All grounds are on the [map](/map).
