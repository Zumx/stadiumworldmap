Polish football infrastructure was transformed by the run-up to UEFA Euro 2012, which Poland co-hosted with Ukraine. The four host venues — Warsaw, Gdańsk, Wrocław, and Poznań — received either new construction or comprehensive renovation, and the trickle-down effect over the following decade has rebuilt Ekstraklasa grounds across the country. Atmospheres in Poland remain genuinely intense, in the central European tradition shared with the Czechs and Slovaks. Every ground below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. PGE Narodowy, Warsaw

Capacity: 58,580. Poland's national stadium, opened in 2011 for Euro 2012 on the site of the demolished Stadion Dziesięciolecia. The exterior is wrapped in a striking red-and-white facade pattern referencing the Polish flag. The retractable roof — a central tensile structure that closes in approximately 20 minutes — was the first of its kind built in central Europe. The bowl hosted the 2012 European Championship opening match (Poland vs Greece) and the 2015 UEFA Europa League final. National-team fixtures remain its primary use.

## 2. Stadion Śląski (Silesian Stadium), Chorzów

Capacity: 54,378. The historic Polish national stadium in the Silesian conurbation, originally opened in 1956 and known for the legendary Cauldron — the steeply-banked terraces that produced the country's most intimidating home-fixture environment for decades. The 2009-17 renovation rebuilt the stadium completely, retaining the original shape and naming but with a modern roof, seats throughout, and full UEFA compliance. Major Polish national-team fixtures and athletics events use the ground.

## 3. Polsat Plus Arena Gdańsk

Capacity: 41,620. Gdańsk's Euro 2012 stadium, designed by RKW with a distinctive amber-coloured exterior referencing the Baltic Sea's amber trade — a defining cultural element of Pomeranian Poland. The translucent facade glows from within when the stadium is illuminated. Lechia Gdańsk play their Ekstraklasa home matches here. The ground hosted the 2021 UEFA Europa League final.

## 4. Stadion Wrocław

Capacity: 42,771. Wrocław's Euro 2012 stadium, designed by JSK Architects with a perforated white facade that lights up at night. The bowl is single-tier on three sides with a double-tier east stand, producing the asymmetry that distinguishes it from Poland's other Euro 2012 grounds. Śląsk Wrocław use the stadium for their Ekstraklasa matches. The 2017 World Games used Wrocław as host city, with the stadium central to the programme.

## 5. Stadion Poznań

Capacity: 42,837. Lech Poznań's home, originally opened in 1980 and comprehensively rebuilt for Euro 2012 by Modern Construction Systems. The Kocioł Czarownic (Witches' Cauldron) — Lech's ultras section — generates atmospheres that have made the ground one of the most consistently noisy in Polish football. The Poznań chant — supporters turning their backs and jumping in unison — originated here in the 1960s and has since been adopted by clubs worldwide, most famously Manchester City.

## 6. Stadion Wojska Polskiego, Warsaw

Capacity: 31,103. Legia Warsaw's home in the Łazienkowska district of central Warsaw, redeveloped in 2010 to UEFA standards. Legia's Żyleta ultras section is one of the most active in central European football. The compact, four-stand layout and the relatively small capacity by international standards produce sustained matchday noise that has prompted multiple UEFA disciplinary cases over the years.

## 7. Stadion Cracovia, Kraków

Capacity: 15,114. Cracovia's home in central Kraków, opened in 2010 to replace the historic but capacity-constrained old ground. The compact bowl sits within the city centre — a setting Polish football has largely lost as new grounds have moved to peripheral sites. The historic Holy War rivalry between Cracovia and Wisła Kraków produces some of Polish football's most volatile derby matchdays.

## 8. Stadion Miejski Wisła Kraków

Capacity: 33,326. Wisła Kraków's home, opened in 2010. The bowl is steep, modern, and acoustically competent. Wisła's recent relegation to the second division (with subsequent promotion attempts) has reduced the stadium's top-flight use, but the matchdays against Cracovia remain among the most intense in Polish football.

## 9. Arena Lublin

Capacity: 15,124. Motor Lublin's home, opened in 2014 as part of the wider Polish stadium renewal beyond the Euro 2012 cities. The compact modern bowl is a good example of how the post-2012 infrastructure programme extended to second-tier cities and second-division clubs.

## 10. Stadion GKS Tychy

Capacity: 15,150. GKS Tychy's home in upper Silesia, opened in 2015. The compact stadium has hosted both Ekstraklasa and Polish second-division matches and is part of the new generation of Silesian grounds modernised after Euro 2012.

## The Euro 2012 legacy

Few national stadium programmes have transformed a country's football infrastructure as completely as Poland's Euro 2012 build-out. The four host city grounds — Warsaw, Gdańsk, Wrocław, and Poznań — established a new baseline that has rippled outward. Ekstraklasa attendances in 2025 average above European mid-tier levels, and the matchday atmospheres at Poznań, Warsaw, and Chorzów rival those of any league outside the very largest. For a country whose football infrastructure in 2005 was widely considered inadequate, the transformation in two decades has been substantial.
