The Estadio Santiago Bernabéu, Real Madrid's home since 1947, has been radically transformed in a renovation completed in 2024. The new exterior wraps the existing bowl in a steel-and-glass facade illuminated by 256 LED panels, while a retractable pitch system allows the ground to convert between football and other event configurations. The stadium is on the [map](/map).

## Construction and naming (1944-1947)

Construction began in 1944 on a site in central Madrid — what is now the Castellana — under the direction of architects Manuel Muñoz Monasterio and Luis Alemany Soler. The ground opened on 14 December 1947 with a match between Real Madrid and Belenenses (Portugal). Original capacity was approximately 75,145. The stadium was renamed in 1955 to honour Santiago Bernabéu, the visionary Real Madrid president who drove the construction project and who built the club into a continental power.

## The Di Stéfano era and the first five European Cups

Real Madrid's five consecutive European Cup wins (1956-1960) under Alfredo Di Stéfano established the Bernabéu as the central venue of early European club football. The 1957 final at the Bernabéu — Real Madrid 2-0 Fiorentina — confirmed the stadium's status. Di Stéfano, Ferenc Puskás, Francisco Gento, Raymond Kopa, and José Santamaría formed the foundational team of European club football's early competitive era, and the Bernabéu was their stage.

## The 1982 World Cup

The Bernabéu hosted the 1982 FIFA World Cup Final between Italy and West Germany (Italy won 3-1). The match — the third World Cup final to be held in Spain at that point — confirmed the Bernabéu's permanent place in international football alongside Camp Nou.

## The Galácticos and the modern era

Florentino Pérez's first Galácticos era (2000-2006) — Zidane, Ronaldo, Beckham, Figo — brought the Bernabéu into the modern global football era. Zidane's volley to win the 2002 Champions League final against Bayer Leverkusen at Hampden Park (a goal at Hampden but defining Real Madrid's modern identity at the Bernabéu) is one of European football's defining moments.

The 2014-2018 four-Champions-League run under Carlo Ancelotti and Zinedine Zidane — anchored by Cristiano Ronaldo, Sergio Ramos, Luka Modrić — produced the most dominant period of any club in Champions League history. The Bernabéu was the home base for this era; the 2018 win at Kyiv against Liverpool (3-1) was followed by the celebration of a fourth Champions League in five years.

## The 2019-2024 renovation

The renovation, designed by GMP Architekten with L35 and Ribas & Ribas Arquitectos, completely transformed the Bernabéu. The new exterior, with its 256-LED steel-and-glass facade, is one of the most visually distinctive elements of any modern stadium. The retractable pitch system allows the natural grass surface to be moved beneath the south stand, exposing a multi-purpose floor that can be used for concerts, NFL games, NBA events, and other configurations.

The new roof is fully retractable, transforming the ground from open to enclosed in approximately 15 minutes. Capacity has been reduced to 78,297 from the previous 81,044 to provide modern hospitality areas and the technical infrastructure for the new configuration. The total project cost has been reported at approximately €1.7 billion.

## The retractable pitch

The Bernabéu's retractable pitch is one of the most ambitious technological undertakings in modern stadium construction. The natural grass surface is stored in subterranean racks beneath the south end of the ground when not in use, and a system of trays moves the grass back into position for football fixtures. The conversion takes approximately 17 hours. The replacement floor below the pitch allows the stadium to be configured for concerts (more than 25,000 audience at floor level) and other events. The first concert at the new Bernabéu was Taylor Swift in 2024.

## The trophy room and the museum

The Bernabéu Museum is one of the most visited sports museums in Europe, with the 15 European Cup / Champions League trophies, 36 La Liga titles, and a complete history of Real Madrid's stars from Di Stéfano to the present. The trophy room — the Sala de las Copas — is the museum's centrepiece, with the European Cups displayed in their original silver finishes.

## The location

The Bernabéu sits on the Paseo de la Castellana in central Madrid, easily accessible by metro (Bernabéu station). The location places it among the city's commercial heart and gives it a distinct urban character — Real Madrid is a club of central Madrid, not a suburban venue like many modern football grounds. The renovated exterior has become a Madrid landmark in its own right, with the LED facade illuminated in club colours and themes throughout the year. See the location on the [map](/map).
