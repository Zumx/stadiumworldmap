Croatia punches far above its weight in international football — World Cup finalists in 2018, third place in 2022 — and its stadium landscape reflects a country where Dinamo Zagreb and Hajduk Split sustain two of Europe's most passionate club fan bases. The grounds themselves tend to be older athletic venues that have been incrementally adapted to modern football; new builds are scarce. Every venue below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Stadion Maksimir, Zagreb

Capacity: 35,123. Dinamo Zagreb's home in the Maksimir district of the Croatian capital, built in 1912 and renovated several times. Maksimir hosts Croatia national team matches and Dinamo's Champions League fixtures. The Bad Blue Boys, Dinamo's organised supporters, occupy the north stand and produce some of Croatia's most consistently sustained atmospheres. The grounds will likely undergo a complete reconstruction; a new stadium has been planned for years.

## 2. Stadion Poljud, Split

Capacity: 34,198. Hajduk Split's home in the Adriatic coastal city, built for the 1979 Mediterranean Games and one of the most architecturally distinctive stadiums in Europe. The shell-shaped roof — designed by Croatian architect Boris Magaš — gives Poljud its silhouette. Torcida Split, founded in 1950 and considered the oldest organised supporters group in Europe outside Italy, occupies the north stand and provides Hajduk's defining atmosphere.

## 3. Stadion Rujevica, Rijeka

Capacity: 8,279. HNK Rijeka's home, opened in 2015 to replace the older Stadion Kantrida. The relatively new ground is fully covered and rectangular, providing modern football geometry that the older Croatian stadia lack. Rijeka's strongest seasons have included Champions League and Europa League appearances.

## 4. Opus Arena, Osijek

Capacity: 13,005. NK Osijek's home in eastern Croatia, opened in 2023 as a modern replacement for the older Gradski Vrt stadium. The fresh build is one of the few twenty-first-century football-specific stadiums in Croatia.

## 5. Stadion Kantrida, Rijeka (former)

Capacity: 10,275 (when active). The former home of HNK Rijeka, located on a cliffside above the Adriatic — one of the most dramatically situated football grounds in Europe. Use was discontinued for first-team fixtures after Rujevica opened in 2015, but the ground remains in use for lower-tier games.

## 6. Stadion Aldo Drosina, Pula

Capacity: 9,800. Istria 1961's home in the Istrian peninsula, located in Pula. The stadium provides Croatia's Istria region with its sole top-flight football venue.

## 7. Gradski stadion, Koprivnica

Capacity: 12,000. Slaven Belupo's home in northern Croatia, hosting Croatian first division football.

## 8. Stadion HNK Cibalia, Vinkovci

Capacity: 10,000. Eastern Croatian football ground that has hosted Croatian First League matches and remains a regional venue.

## 9. Stadion Velika Gorica, Velika Gorica

Capacity: 5,228. HNK Gorica's home just south of Zagreb. The compact ground provides a Croatian First League venue close to the capital.

## 10. Stadion Šubićevac, Šibenik

Capacity: 8,000. HNK Šibenik's home on the Adriatic coast. The setting between the historic old town and the sea is among the most picturesque in Croatian football.

## Croatia's HNL

The Croatian First Football League (Prva HNL) has 10 clubs and runs an autumn-spring calendar. Dinamo Zagreb has dominated the modern era, with Hajduk Split, HNK Rijeka and NK Osijek as the most consistent challengers. The league produces Croatia's European representatives — Dinamo's regular Champions League group-stage qualifying being the standard against which other Croatian clubs measure themselves.

## The Eternal Derby and Torcida

The Eternal Derby (Vječni derbi) between Dinamo Zagreb and Hajduk Split is one of European football's most intense fixtures, with the two clubs reflecting the Zagreb-Split capital-coastal divide of Croatian society. Torcida, founded at Hajduk in 1950, is widely recognised as the first organised football supporters group in Europe (preceding the formation of the Italian ultras movement). The Bad Blue Boys at Dinamo, founded in 1986, mirror Torcida's matchday role. Both sides have a documented history of choreography, pyrotechnics, and the occasional disorder that has marked European football's most charged rivalries.
