New Zealand's stadium landscape is shaped by the dominance of rugby union — the All Blacks' calendar is the year's structural sporting event — combined with cricket, association football, and the regional rugby league franchises. The country's main grounds are multi-sport in design, with rugby and cricket sharing capacity at most senior venues. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Eden Park, Auckland

Capacity: 50,000. New Zealand's largest stadium and the spiritual home of All Blacks rugby. Eden Park has hosted Rugby World Cup finals in 1987 and 2011, multiple Cricket World Cup matches, and All Whites football fixtures. The Suburban Auckland location and the heritage status of the ground make any redevelopment politically complicated; the main stand and the South Stand were upgraded in the 2010s.

## 2. Sky Stadium (Wellington Regional Stadium), Wellington

Capacity: 34,500. The Cake Tin in central Wellington, opened in 2000, is one of New Zealand's most architecturally distinctive sports venues. The compact rectangular ground hosts Hurricanes rugby, Wellington Phoenix A-League football, and Wellington cricket. The exposed harbour-side location can make matches challenging for both supporters and players due to wind.

## 3. Forsyth Barr Stadium, Dunedin

Capacity: 30,748. The only fully enclosed indoor multi-sport stadium in New Zealand, opened in 2011 in Dunedin on the South Island. The ETFE-foil roof closes the bowl completely, making rugby and cricket possible regardless of Dunedin's challenging weather. Highlanders rugby and Otago cricket use the ground.

## 4. Westpac Stadium, Christchurch (rebuilt)

Capacity: variable. Christchurch's main rugby ground was significantly damaged by the 2011 earthquakes and the city has been in the process of building a replacement venue (Te Kaha) ever since. The temporary venues that filled the gap in the post-earthquake decade have themselves been re-named and re-purposed.

## 5. Mount Smart Stadium, Auckland

Capacity: 25,000. The Auckland rugby league ground, used by the New Zealand Warriors NRL franchise and historically for various other codes. The location in Penrose, south of central Auckland, is reachable by train.

## 6. Waikato Stadium, Hamilton

Capacity: 25,800. The Waikato region's primary stadium, used by Hamilton-based rugby, football, and cricket. The Chiefs Super Rugby franchise use it as their home ground.

## 7. McLean Park, Napier

Capacity: 22,000. The Hawke's Bay ground in Napier, primarily a cricket venue but with rugby and football use. The ground hosted matches during the 2011 Rugby World Cup.

## 8. Yarrow Stadium, New Plymouth

Capacity: 25,000. New Plymouth's main rugby stadium on the west coast of the North Island. Taranaki rugby and the wider New Plymouth sporting community use the ground.

## 9. North Harbour Stadium, Albany

Capacity: 25,000. The Auckland North Shore stadium in Albany has hosted various rugby, football, and concert events. It served as the home ground for North Harbour rugby and for football's North Shore United.

## 10. Trafalgar Park, Nelson

Capacity: 18,000. The Nelson regional ground at the top of the South Island, used by Tasman Makos rugby and Nelson cricket. The scenic Nelson location places the ground in one of New Zealand's most picturesque sporting settings.

## The Rugby World Cup legacy

New Zealand's hosting of the 1987 and 2011 Rugby World Cups produced two cycles of stadium investment. The 2011 cycle in particular upgraded Eden Park, Sky Stadium, and several regional grounds to international broadcast standard. The All Blacks' continued global commercial weight sustains these venues at a level that the country's population (5 million) would not otherwise support.

## Football's growing footprint

Association football in New Zealand has grown significantly through Wellington Phoenix's A-League participation since 2007 and the women's national team (Football Ferns) consistent World Cup qualification including hosting the 2023 FIFA Women's World Cup. The hosting of the 2023 Women's World Cup brought eight cities and four stadiums into international football's primary calendar — Eden Park, Sky Stadium, Forsyth Barr Stadium, and Waikato Stadium were the primary New Zealand venues.

## The All Blacks at home

New Zealand's national rugby team plays its home matches across multiple grounds, with Eden Park as the primary venue (the All Blacks have not lost a Test at Eden Park since 1994 — one of sport's longest unbeaten home runs at a single venue). The Bledisloe Cup matches against Australia and the Rugby Championship fixtures generate the year's largest attendances at Eden Park.

## Visiting New Zealand grounds

Eden Park is a 15-minute train journey from Auckland CBD. Sky Stadium is walking distance from Wellington's CBD. Forsyth Barr Stadium in Dunedin is reachable by a 90-minute domestic flight from Auckland followed by a short taxi journey. The North-South Island geography of New Zealand makes multi-stadium visits practical with domestic flights between Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch, and Dunedin.
