Romanian football's stadium picture has been shaped by a generation of new builds across major cities — Bucharest, Cluj-Napoca, Craiova, Timișoara — replacing the ageing infrastructure left over from the Communist era. The Arena Națională in Bucharest, opened in 2011, set the architectural standard, and several regional grounds have followed. Each stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Arena Națională, Bucharest

Capacity: 55,634. Romania's national stadium, opened in 2011 on the site of the demolished Stadionul Național de Fotbal "Lia Manoliu". Designed by GMP Architekten with a retractable roof, the arena hosted the 2012 UEFA Europa League final (Atlético Madrid 3 Athletic Bilbao 0) and four matches of UEFA Euro 2020 (delayed to 2021). It is the home ground of the Romanian national team and used for club Champions League matches and major domestic cup finals.

## 2. Stadionul Steaua, Bucharest

Capacity: 31,254. Steaua București's home in the Ghencea district, rebuilt and reopened in 2021 on the site of the historic Stadionul Ghencea. The original Ghencea ground hosted Steaua's 1986 European Cup-winning campaign — the only Eastern European European Cup win between the Soviet bloc's split-up and the Champions League era. The reconstruction maintained the historic location while delivering modern infrastructure. The club's ownership and naming history is complicated — FCSB are the legal successor, while CSA Steaua, the army-affiliated club, also plays here.

## 3. Cluj Arena, Cluj-Napoca

Capacity: 30,201. The largest stadium in the Transylvania region, opened in 2011 on the site of the former Stadionul Ion Moina. Universitatea Cluj is the primary tenant in recent years, with CFR Cluj using the smaller Stadionul Dr. Constantin Rădulescu nearby. Cluj Arena hosts the Romanian national team's matches in the Transylvania region and major concerts. The modern facade with its diagonal cable structure is one of the most recognisable new stadium designs in Eastern Europe.

## 4. Stadionul Giulești, Bucharest

Capacity: 14,047. Rapid București's home in the Giulești district of Bucharest, comprehensively rebuilt and reopened in 2022 after the demolition of the historic original. The Giulești area has been associated with Rapid since the club's foundation in 1923 — the working-class railway-workers' club identity remains central to Rapid's character. The new ground retains the historic location while modernising the facilities.

## 5. Stadionul Dr. Constantin Rădulescu, Cluj-Napoca

Capacity: 23,500. CFR Cluj's home, opened in 1973 and modernised in several stages. CFR have been Romania's dominant club of the past decade, winning seven SuperLiga titles between 2008 and 2022 and qualifying for the Champions League group stage. The stadium's name honours a former club doctor — one of European football's more unusual naming traditions.

## 6. Stadionul Ion Oblemenco, Craiova

Capacity: 30,983. Universitatea Craiova's home in the southern Romanian city, opened in 2017 on the site of the historic Stadionul Ion Oblemenco. The Craiova football tradition runs deep — the original Universitatea Craiova reached the European Cup semi-final in 1982-83 before the team's dispersal. The current Universitatea Craiova plays in the SuperLiga and the modern ground is among Romania's best new builds.

## 7. Stadionul Francisc von Neuman, Arad

Capacity: 12,500. UTA Arad's home in western Romania near the Hungarian border. UTA were one of the dominant Romanian clubs of the post-war era, with multiple Romanian championships in the 1940s and 1960s. The modern stadium reopened in 2020 on the site of the historic Stadionul Francisc Neuman. The compact new bowl is among the most architecturally distinctive in Romanian regional football.

## 8. Stadionul Municipal, Pitești

Capacity: 15,000. FC Argeș Pitești's home, modernised over multiple phases. FC Argeș has a strong historic tradition in Romanian football and the city of Pitești — population approximately 155,000 — has produced footballers disproportionate to its size. The stadium's regional importance has been recognised through the national team using it for friendly matches periodically.

## 9. Stadionul Municipal Sibiu (Sibiu Sports Complex)

Capacity: 12,500. FC Hermannstadt's home in the Transylvanian city of Sibiu. The city's historic German name (Hermannstadt) — reflecting the Saxon population that built the medieval town — gives the modern football club its naming heritage. The compact stadium opened in 2018 and represents the new generation of regional Romanian football venues.

## 10. Stadionul Dan Păltinișanu, Timișoara

Capacity: 32,019. Timișoara's main football venue, opened in 1963 and partially renovated. The stadium hosted the Romanian national team and several clubs over the decades, although the city's club football has fluctuated significantly. Politehnica Timișoara, the historic club, was dissolved in 2012; multiple successor clubs have used the ground.

## The Romanian SuperLiga

Romanian top-flight football has been competitive in recent years, with CFR Cluj's seven-year championship streak from 2017 to 2022 broken by Farul Constanța in 2022-23 under coach Gheorghe Hagi. The SuperLiga's structure has shifted multiple times in the past decade — currently a 16-team league with promotion/relegation playoffs and a top-six championship round. The level of investment has gradually risen, but the gap to Western European leagues remains significant for player retention.

## Romanian football's golden generation

The 1990s saw Romania's football peak. The 1994 World Cup quarter-final run (eliminating Argentina in the round of 16) and the Euro 2000 quarter-final, with players like Gheorghe Hagi, Dan Petrescu, Adrian Mutu, Gică Popescu and Ilie Dumitrescu, are the country's most consistently cited historical reference points. Steaua's 1986 European Cup win remains the only European trophy by a Romanian club — and one of only a handful by any Eastern European club in the European Cup era.

## Visiting Romanian grounds

Romanian football is increasingly accessible for visiting supporters. Bucharest's stadiums are reachable by metro and bus; regional grounds via train. Tickets are inexpensive by Western European standards. The Arena Națională and the new Steaua Stadium are among the most architecturally interesting builds of the past 15 years in Eastern Europe.
