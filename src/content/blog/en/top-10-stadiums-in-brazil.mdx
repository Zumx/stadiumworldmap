Brazilian football is played in a range of venues that reflect the country's scale and diversity — from the iconic to the crumbling, from the UNESCO-recognised to the purpose-built modern arena. The 2014 World Cup accelerated a generation of stadium construction, some of it immediately successful and some still searching for a sustainable post-tournament role. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Maracana, Rio de Janeiro

Capacity: 78,838 (post-2014 renovation). Opened for the 1950 World Cup — the only tournament Brazil has hosted until 2014 — the Maracana held an estimated 199,854 for the 1950 final, which Uruguay won in what became known as the Maracanazo. It hosted the 2014 World Cup final (Germany 1 Argentina 0) and the 2016 Olympic football final. The renovation for 2014 reduced capacity significantly by replacing the standing areas with seats and installing a new roof, while retaining the overall structure. Flamengo and Fluminense use it as home ground. It remains the single most historically resonant football venue in South America.

## 2. Mineirao, Belo Horizonte

Capacity: 61,846 after renovation for the 2014 World Cup. Home to Cruzeiro and Atletico Mineiro. The ground in Belo Horizonte's Pampulha neighbourhood was built in 1965 and renovated comprehensively for 2014. It is famous — or infamous — as the venue for Brazil's 7-1 defeat to Germany in the 2014 semifinal, one of the most shocking results in World Cup history. The match has been referred to as the Mineirazo. Despite the association, the stadium is one of the best-maintained in Brazil.

## 3. Estadio do Morumbi, Sao Paulo

Capacity: 72,000 (unrenovated sections restrict regular use to approximately 67,000). Sao Paulo FC's home, built through the 1960s and 70s and named for the neighbourhood it occupies. One of the largest privately owned stadiums in the world. Morumbi was not selected as a 2014 World Cup venue because it did not meet FIFA's infrastructure requirements, which was a political and financial controversy at the time. It remains in regular use for Sao Paulo home matches and the Brazilian national team.

## 4. Allianz Parque, Sao Paulo

Capacity: 43,713. Palmeiras' purpose-built home, opened in 2014, replaced the old Palestra Italia ground. Designed by Hok, the all-seater arena is one of Brazil's most modern venues and was the first in the country built primarily with private investment on a model close to European club stadium ownership. The naming rights deal with Allianz was the first major stadium naming-rights agreement in Brazilian football.

## 5. Arena Corinthians (Neo Quimica Arena), Sao Paulo

Capacity: 49,205. Sport Club Corinthians Paulista's home in the Itaquera district, built as the opening venue for the 2014 World Cup. The World Cup opener — Brazil versus Croatia — was played here. Post-tournament, it became Corinthians' permanent home. With Corinthians being one of Brazil's most widely supported clubs, the ground regularly sells out for Sao Paulo state championship and Brasileirao matches.

## 6. Estadio Beira-Rio, Porto Alegre

Capacity: 51,300 after 2014 renovation. Sport Club Internacional's home on Ilha do Pavao on the shores of the Guaiba River, opened in 1969. The riverside location and the renovated bowl make it one of Brazil's most visually distinctive grounds. Internacional and Gremio are Porto Alegre's two major clubs; Beira-Rio hosted 2014 World Cup group stage and round of 16 matches.

## 7. Arena do Gremio, Porto Alegre

Capacity: 55,662. Gremio Foot-Ball Porto Alegrense's purpose-built home, opened in 2012 to replace the old Olimpico Monumental. The Arena is consistently rated among Brazil's best in terms of design and supporter experience — tight, steep, well-facilitated. Gremio's Copa Libertadores wins in 1983, 1995, and 2017 make it one of South America's most successful clubs, and the Arena has hosted Libertadores knockout rounds.

## 8. Estadio Mane Garrincha, Brasilia

Capacity: 72,788 (one of the largest in Brazil after renovation). The national capital's stadium was rebuilt entirely for the 2014 World Cup at a cost of approximately R$1.4 billion — the most expensive stadium in the tournament and a figure that attracted sustained criticism given that Brasilia lacks a top-division club. Post-tournament use has been limited to occasional national team fixtures and concerts. It is regularly cited alongside Montreal 1976 and Athens 2004 as a cautionary example of stadium investment without sporting legacy.

## 9. Estadio do Pacaembu, Sao Paulo

Capacity: approximately 40,199. Built in 1940 in the Pacaembu neighbourhood, this listed municipal stadium has been closed for renovation and privatisation since 2020. The ground's art deco design is one of Brazilian sport architecture's most significant examples, and the renovation, managed by a private consortium, has been expected to restore it to operational status. Several Sao Paulo clubs have used it as a temporary home at various points in its history.

## 10. Vila Belmiro, Santos

Capacity: 16,798. Santos FC's compact home on the coast at Santos is remarkable not for its size but for its association — it is where Pele played from 1956 to 1974, scoring the majority of his club career goals. The ground has never been significantly expanded due to its position within a dense urban neighbourhood. Santos' run to the Copa Libertadores final in 2011, with Neymar, revived international attention on the ground.

## Brazil's 2014 World Cup legacy debate

The twelve stadiums built or renovated for 2014 represented an investment of approximately $3.5 billion, and the debate about whether that investment was justified continues. Allianz Parque and Arena do Gremio are the clearest successes — purpose-built for strong clubs with large supports. Mane Garrincha in Brasilia and Arena Amazonia in Manaus (not in the top ten but relevant to the legacy discussion) are the most commonly cited failures. The story of Brazilian stadium investment after 2014 is the tension between those two outcomes.

## The Brasileirao and Copa do Brasil

Brazilian club football runs on two parallel axes: the Brasileirao Serie A (the national league) and the Copa do Brasil (the national cup), alongside state championships at the beginning of each calendar year. The Brasileirao operates April to December on a 20-team league format similar to the Premier League. Flamengo — Brazil's most widely supported club — have been the Brasileirao's dominant force since 2019, using the Maracana as their home and winning the Copa Libertadores in 2019 and 2022.

The state championships — most significantly the Campeonato Paulista in Sao Paulo and the Campeonato Carioca in Rio de Janeiro — fill the calendar from January to March and produce derbies (Flamengo versus Fluminense, Corinthians versus Palmeiras) that use the largest stadiums at near-full capacity before the national competition begins.

## Visiting Brazilian grounds

Brazilian matches require advance research by visiting supporters. The major clubs — Flamengo, Corinthians, Palmeiras, Fluminense — operate official ticket systems. Security arrangements at Maracana and Arena Corinthians are thorough but the queue systems can be slow. Portuguese language skills are useful; translation apps cover most practical needs at ticket windows. All grounds in this list are marked on the [map](/map).
