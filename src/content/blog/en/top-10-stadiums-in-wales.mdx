Welsh stadium culture revolves around the unique balance between rugby union — the country's traditional national game — and association football, which has been on a sustained upswing since the national team's Euro 2016 semi-final run. The Principality Stadium in central Cardiff dominates the landscape; the rest of the country plays in smaller, often historic grounds tied tightly to community identity. Every venue below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Principality Stadium, Cardiff

Capacity: 73,931. The home of the Wales national rugby union team in the heart of Cardiff, completed in 1999 for the Rugby World Cup. The retractable roof — one of the few stadium roofs in the UK that genuinely closes the bowl — produces an indoor atmosphere unlike anything else in international rugby when shut. The stadium has hosted FA Cup finals (during the Wembley reconstruction 2001-2006), Champions League finals (2017), and European Rugby Cup finals. Cardiff city centre wraps around it on three sides, making matchday in the streets surrounding the ground unlike any other UK venue.

## 2. Cardiff City Stadium, Cardiff

Capacity: 33,280. Home of Cardiff City FC and the Wales national football team, opened in 2009 to replace the old Ninian Park. The ground sits in the Leckwith area, adjacent to the original Ninian Park site (now demolished). Wales hosted the Republic of Ireland here in the 2018 UEFA Nations League and continues to use the stadium for non-Wembley-replacement-era home games when Principality is unavailable.

## 3. Swansea.com Stadium (formerly Liberty Stadium), Swansea

Capacity: 21,088. Opened in 2005, the Swansea ground is shared by Swansea City FC and the Ospreys rugby union region. The dual-tenant arrangement saved Swansea from the rural-shed alternative many smaller UK clubs face. Naming rights have changed several times.

## 4. Rodney Parade, Newport

Capacity: 7,850. Newport County AFC's home, shared with the Dragons rugby region and Newport RFC. Rodney Parade is one of the most historically packed sports grounds in Wales — multiple sports, multiple eras, one site — and the football end of its calendar fills the small stand network for League Two and EFL Trophy fixtures.

## 5. SToK Cae Ras (Wrexham Racecourse), Wrexham

Capacity: 13,341 (expanding). The oldest international football ground in the world still in use — Wrexham's Racecourse hosted Wales-Scotland in 1877. The Ryan Reynolds and Rob McElhenney ownership of Wrexham AFC, combined with the documentary Welcome to Wrexham, has driven a complete capacity rebuild. The stand expansion is ongoing.

## 6. Parc y Scarlets, Llanelli

Capacity: 14,870. Home of the Scarlets rugby union region, opened in 2008 to replace the old Stradey Park. The Llanelli ground has hosted multiple Heineken Cup matches and Wales rugby fixtures when Principality is unavailable.

## 7. Brewery Field, Bridgend

Capacity: 7,000. Historic ground of the former Bridgend Ravens, now used by Bridgend RFC. The site has been a fixture in Welsh club rugby for over a century and retains its traditional character despite the modern stadium era.

## 8. Eirias Park (Parc Eirias), Colwyn Bay

Capacity: 7,500. North Wales's principal regional sports venue, hosting Wales rugby league and Wales football fixtures when north Wales needs the matches. The Colwyn Bay ground is the closest large stadium to Liverpool for travelling Welsh fans.

## 9. Latham Park, Newtown

Capacity: 5,000. Newtown AFC's home and one of the most photogenic football grounds in Wales, the small terraced stand with white painted timber framing the pitch. The Mid Wales setting is one of the most distinctive in Cymru Premier (Welsh Premier League) football.

## 10. Park Avenue, Aberystwyth

Capacity: 5,000. Aberystwyth Town FC's home on the West Wales coast. The Cymru Premier ground has hosted European qualifying fixtures and remains the western anchor of the Welsh football pyramid.

## Welsh rugby and the Principality Stadium

Wales rugby's connection to the Principality Stadium runs deeper than the simple home-and-away tradition of international sport. The stadium occupies the site of the original Cardiff Arms Park, where Welsh rugby established its identity in the late nineteenth century. When the roof is closed for a Six Nations fixture, the acoustic intensity inside has been measured at levels comparable to a covered NFL dome. Welsh anthem-singing — Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau — at the start of an international is one of sport's most reliable visceral experiences.

## Welsh football's modern rise

Wales football reached the Euro 2016 semi-final under Chris Coleman, the World Cup 2022 finals under Robert Page, and the country's player generation including Gareth Bale, Aaron Ramsey and Joe Allen brought Welsh football to a sustained competitive level not seen since 1958. The Cardiff City Stadium has been the home throughout this rise. Welsh football historians point to the 2015 Wales-Belgium qualifier — won 1-0 — as the night when the modern era of Welsh international football began. All grounds are marked on the [map](/map).
