Paraguay's football is densely concentrated in Asunción, where most of the country's biggest clubs share the capital. The grounds tend to be older South American football venues with tight rectangular geometries and atmospheres that have produced Copa Libertadores semi-finalists and finalists across the decades. Olimpia and Cerro Porteño remain the dominant clubs, with their derby — the Superclásico — among South America's most intense. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Estadio Defensores del Chaco, Asunción

Capacity: 36,000. Paraguay's national stadium in the Sajonia district of Asunción, named after the Defenders of the Chaco — soldiers from the 1932-1935 Chaco War with Bolivia. The ground hosts the Paraguay national team's biggest fixtures and major Paraguayan football events. It has hosted the Copa América and remains the country's principal large-stadium venue.

## 2. Estadio General Pablo Rojas (La Nueva Olla), Asunción

Capacity: 45,000. Cerro Porteño's home in the Barrio Obrero neighbourhood of Asunción, opened in 2017 as a complete rebuild. La Nueva Olla — The New Pot — is one of South America's largest club-owned stadiums and produces atmospheres at the level of the continent's biggest grounds when Cerro plays Copa Libertadores knockout-round fixtures.

## 3. Estadio Manuel Ferreira, Asunción

Capacity: 25,000. Olimpia's home in the Mariscal Estigarribia neighbourhood, named after the club's former president. Olimpia is Paraguay's most successful club historically, with three Copa Libertadores titles (1979, 1990, 2002). The stadium's tight rectangular geometry produces concentrated atmospheres.

## 4. Estadio Antonio Aranda, Ciudad del Este

Capacity: 25,000. Located in Ciudad del Este near the Brazilian border, this stadium hosts Sportivo 2 de Mayo and serves as the eastern Paraguay football centre.

## 5. Estadio Feliciano Cáceres, Luque

Capacity: 25,000. Sportivo Luqueño's home in Luque, just east of Asunción. The Luqueño grounds have hosted Copa Libertadores group-stage fixtures.

## 6. Estadio Roberto Bettega, Asunción (Tigo Club Sol de América)

Capacity: 8,000. Club Sol de América's home in the Villa Elisa area of greater Asunción. A modest ground for a club with consistent first-division presence.

## 7. Estadio Erico Galeano Segovia, Asunción

Capacity: 4,500. Smaller ground in Asunción hosting lower-division and youth football, named after a Paraguayan football administrator.

## 8. Estadio Ka'arendy, Asunción

Capacity: 10,000. A modern ground in the Ka'arendy district, hosting various Paraguayan first division clubs.

## 9. Estadio Tomas Beggan Correa, Coronel Oviedo

Capacity: 12,000. Coronel Oviedo's principal ground in central Paraguay. The setting near the Caaguazú department centre is one of the more regional venues for Paraguayan top-flight football.

## 10. Estadio Río Parapití, Pedro Juan Caballero

Capacity: 12,000. Sportivo Trinidense's home in the northeastern border city of Pedro Juan Caballero, near the Brazilian frontier.

## Primera División Paraguaya

Paraguay's Primera División has 12 clubs and runs an Apertura-Clausura split-season format common to many South American leagues. Olimpia and Cerro Porteño dominate historically, with Libertad, Guaraní, and Nacional as the most consistent challengers. The league's overall standard is competitive within CONMEBOL, with Paraguayan clubs reaching the Copa Libertadores knockout rounds regularly.

## The Superclásico

The Superclásico Paraguayo between Olimpia and Cerro Porteño is one of South America's most intense rivalries. The two clubs share Asunción between them, with Olimpia's traditional middle-class base contrasting Cerro Porteño's working-class identity in Barrio Obrero. Matches are split between the Manuel Ferreira and La Nueva Olla, with the visitor's stand reserved for the away team — a separation enforced by security operations on derby days. The fixture has produced numerous incidents over the years, mirroring the intensity of Argentine and Brazilian classics.

## The Paraguay national team

The Paraguay national team have qualified for eight FIFA World Cups, with their best finish being the quarter-final in 2010 (when they were eliminated by eventual champions Spain). Stars like Roque Santa Cruz (Bayern Munich, Manchester City) and Salvador Cabañas brought Paraguayan football to European audiences. The team uses the Defensores del Chaco for major fixtures and has occasionally rotated home games to Cerro's La Nueva Olla for larger crowds. All stadiums are marked on the [map](/map).
