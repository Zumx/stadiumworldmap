The Tottenham Hotspur Stadium, on the site of the old White Hart Lane in north London, has been the home of Tottenham Hotspur since April 2019. Designed by Populous and built at an estimated cost of £1.2 billion, the 62,850-seat ground is the most expensive stadium ever constructed in the United Kingdom and one of the most technically ambitious purpose-built football grounds in the world. Its core innovation — a fully retractable grass pitch that slides aside to reveal a permanent NFL-grade artificial surface beneath — has made the stadium the European base of the NFL's London Games. ## The site and the move

White Hart Lane, Tottenham's home from 1899 to 2017, had reached the limit of expansion possible on its constrained north-London site. The replacement project, announced in 2008 and developed over a decade, ultimately demolished the old ground and built the new stadium on the same plot plus expanded adjacent land. Tottenham played the 2017-18 and most of the 2018-19 seasons at Wembley as their temporary home; the first competitive match at the new ground was on 3 April 2019 against Crystal Palace (won 2-0 by Spurs).

## The dual-pitch system

The signature engineering feature of the stadium is the retractable grass pitch. The natural-grass football pitch sits on three movable trays that slide apart and into storage beneath the south stand, revealing a permanent NFL-grade artificial surface laid in the cavity below. The transition between configurations takes approximately 25 minutes. The system allows the stadium to host Premier League football and NFL games in the same week without compromising either surface — and to monetise rugby, concerts, and other non-football events while protecting the natural grass.

## The South Stand single-tier home end

The South Stand — 17,500 seats in a single, steep, continuous rake — is the largest single-tier home end in England, modelled on the Yellow Wall at Dortmund's Westfalenstadion. The tier rises at a 35-degree pitch close to the maximum permitted by UK safety regulations, placing the most-vocal Tottenham supporters in a single block where chants spread quickly and the visual effect of scarves and flags is maximised. Below the South Stand sits the Tunnel Club, a hospitality area with views into the player tunnel.

## The Sky Walk and the architectural detail

The exterior is animated by the visible Sky Walk — a glass-walled walkway around the stadium roof that opens for paying visitors as a high-altitude tour experience. Other architectural details include the LED-lit Spurs cockerel atop the south stand, the brass plaque memorial to Bill Nicholson (the manager who guided Spurs to the 1961 Double), and the integrated lower-bowl supporter mural. The exterior cladding combines glass and warm metal panels, with the Tottenham High Road frontage designed to be a continuous public space rather than a closed perimeter wall.

## The NFL partnership

The Tottenham Hotspur Stadium is the official European base of the NFL, with a minimum of two NFL London Games scheduled at the ground each season under a long-term partnership signed in 2015 and re-extended in 2024. The artificial surface is laid out to NFL specifications including end zones, hash marks, and a goal-post system that retracts entirely when not in use. The dual-pitch system was specifically designed around the dual NFL/football requirement.

## The microbrewery and matchday offer

The stadium includes the longest bar in Europe (the 65-metre Goal Line Bar in the West Stand) and an on-site microbrewery (Beavertown Brewery's match-day taproom) producing beer brewed specifically for matchday consumption. The catering operation is structured around point-of-sale technology designed to minimise queue times during the half-time and pre-match peaks, a chronic problem at older grounds. Matchday catering revenue per attendee is among the highest in European football.

## The Champions League nights

Tottenham's Champions League run to the 2018-19 final (lost to Liverpool in Madrid) was begun at Wembley but ended at the new stadium, with the second leg of the semi-final against Ajax — won 3-2 with Lucas Moura's hat-trick — among the defining nights of the early stadium era. The April 2019 quarter-final first leg against Manchester City, the first European match at the new ground, was won 1-0 with Heung-min Son's goal.

## Visiting

The stadium offers a range of tour options including a standard stadium tour (dressing rooms, tunnel, dugouts), the Sky Walk (a 46-metre-high external roof walkway), and a dedicated Tunnel Club hospitality experience. Match-day access is via the White Hart Lane station on the Overground network or Northumberland Park station, with Seven Sisters and Tottenham Hale on the Victoria Line a short bus ride away.
