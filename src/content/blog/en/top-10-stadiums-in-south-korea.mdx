South Korea co-hosted the 2002 FIFA World Cup with Japan, and the build-out for that tournament produced ten new or comprehensively renovated stadiums across the country. Two decades later, the post-2002 venues remain the backbone of Korean football's stadium portfolio. K League football has consolidated around clubs based at these grounds, and the women's WK League and national-team fixtures add to the matchday load. Every ground below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Seoul World Cup Stadium

Capacity: 66,704. Opened in 2001 for the 2002 World Cup, designed by Ryu Choon-Soo with a roof structure resembling a traditional Korean kite. The bowl has hosted FC Seoul K League matches since opening and is the primary venue for the South Korea national team. The architectural concept of integrating Korean traditional motifs with modern stadium engineering is widely cited as a successful synthesis of cultural reference and structural function.

## 2. Busan Asiad Main Stadium

Capacity: 53,769. Opened in 2001 for the 2002 World Cup and the 2002 Asian Games. The bowl is in the Yeonje district of Busan and features a roof structure shaped to evoke the Korean dolmen — the prehistoric burial monument. The stadium hosted multiple 2002 World Cup matches including Brazil's group-stage games. Post-tournament use has been mixed, with the K League's local Busan IPark playing some matches at the venue.

## 3. Daegu World Cup Stadium

Capacity: 66,422. Opened in 2001 for the 2002 World Cup in the Suseong district of Daegu. The bowl is one of the most acoustically distinct of the 2002 grounds, with a complete roof that produces strong matchday noise. Daegu FC's K League matches are now held at the smaller DGB Daegu Bank Park, leaving the World Cup Stadium for major fixtures and concerts.

## 4. Suwon World Cup Stadium

Capacity: 43,959. The Suwon bowl, opened in 2001, is the home of Suwon Samsung Bluewings in the K League. The roof structure uses cables and posts to suspend the canopy, producing one of the lighter visual signatures among 2002 venues. Suwon's traditional rivalry with FC Seoul produces some of the K League's most-attended matches.

## 5. Incheon Munhak Stadium

Capacity: 44,460. The Incheon bowl, opened in 2001 for the 2002 World Cup. The home of Incheon United in the K League. The stadium hosted matches during the 2014 Asian Games, providing a second-tier mega-event reuse that has kept the ground in regular major-fixture use.

## 6. Jeju World Cup Stadium

Capacity: 35,657. The Jeju Island venue from 2002, sited in Seogwipo on the southern coast. The exterior facade references the Jeju traditional volcanic stone wall pattern. Jeju United play their K League matches here, and the geographic isolation of the island setting makes the ground one of the more distinctive 2002 World Cup legacy venues.

## 7. Ulsan Munsu Football Stadium

Capacity: 44,102. Ulsan Hyundai's home in the industrial city of Ulsan, opened in 2001. The bowl is football-specific (no athletics track), which was relatively unusual for major Korean 2002 venues, and produces excellent sightlines and acoustics. Ulsan's recent K League title dominance has kept the ground in regular continental-level focus.

## 8. Gwangyang Football Stadium

Capacity: 13,496. The home of Jeonnam Dragons in the K League, opened in 1993 as one of South Korea's earliest football-specific (no track) stadiums. The compact, intimate ground predates the 2002 build-out and offers a different model of Korean stadium architecture — closer in spirit to European football-specific grounds than to the larger multi-purpose 2002 venues.

## 9. Seoul Olympic Stadium

Capacity: 69,950. The athletics-track stadium from the 1988 Seoul Olympics, sited in the Jamsil district. The bowl hosted the 1988 Olympic opening and closing ceremonies, football final, and athletics programme. Post-Olympic use has been mixed, with concerts and occasional football fixtures. The 1988 Games are considered a pivotal moment in South Korea's modernisation, and the stadium carries that historical weight.

## 10. Goyang Stadium

Capacity: 41,311. A 2002-era venue in Goyang, just outside Seoul. Used for K League matches by smaller clubs and for major national-team fixtures when Seoul World Cup Stadium is unavailable. Goyang represents the broader Seoul metropolitan area's distributed stadium infrastructure.

## The 2002 legacy and Korean football culture

South Korea's 2002 World Cup stadium portfolio has been more successful than many similar mega-event legacies. K League use has been sustained at most major 2002 grounds, the national team has consistently used the Seoul, Busan, Daegu, and Suwon venues, and the architectural ambition of the original designs (Korean cultural motifs integrated with modern stadium engineering) has aged well. The Korean stadium landscape in 2025 reflects two decades of consolidation around the 2002 build-out, with newer football-specific grounds at Daegu (DGB Bank Park) and Pohang representing a second-generation evolution.
