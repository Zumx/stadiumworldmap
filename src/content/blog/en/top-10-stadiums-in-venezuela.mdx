Venezuelan football has historically lived in the shadow of baseball, the country's dominant sport, but the football infrastructure has improved significantly since the early 2000s. La Vinotinto, the Venezuela national team, has reached respectability at the South American level despite never qualifying for a World Cup, and the club game has developed in parallel. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Estadio Monumental de Maturin

Capacity: 51,796. Venezuela's largest stadium, opened in 2007 for the Copa America. Located in Maturin in the eastern Monagas region, the ground hosts the Venezuela national team for major fixtures and Monagas Sport Club, who have won multiple Venezuelan Primera Division titles. The vast bowl is one of South America's larger grounds outside Argentina and Brazil.

## 2. Estadio Olimpico de la UCV, Caracas

Capacity: 24,000. The Caracas municipal stadium at the Universidad Central de Venezuela campus is part of the UNESCO-recognised Carlos Raul Villanueva-designed university complex. The ground hosts the Venezuela national team in Caracas and Caracas FC.

## 3. Estadio Brigido Iriarte, Caracas

Capacity: approximately 12,000. Caracas FC's compact home in central Caracas. Caracas are Venezuela's most successful club historically, with 12 Primera Division titles, and the ground has hosted Copa Libertadores group stage matches.

## 4. Estadio Metropolitano de Lara

Capacity: 41,000. Located near Barquisimeto in the Lara state, the Metropolitano was built for the 2007 Copa America. It hosts the local club Deportivo Lara, but the capacity is large relative to typical match attendance, which has been a long-standing operational issue for the venue.

## 5. Estadio Pueblo Nuevo, San Cristobal

Capacity: 38,000. The Pueblo Nuevo stadium in San Cristobal (Tachira state) is the home of Deportivo Tachira, one of Venezuela's most consistent clubs. The Andean location gives the ground a different climatic context, and Tachira's altitude provides some home advantage in Copa Libertadores group stages.

## 6. Estadio Jose Antonio Anzoategui, Puerto La Cruz

Capacity: 37,485. Puerto La Cruz on Venezuela's Caribbean coast hosts a 2007 Copa America-era ground used by Deportivo Anzoategui. The coastal setting and the Caribbean climate give the stadium a tropical character.

## 7. Estadio Cachamay, Puerto Ordaz

Capacity: 41,600. The Cachamay stadium in Puerto Ordaz, near the Guyana border, was rebuilt for the 2007 Copa America. Mineros de Guayana use the ground; the Guayana region's mining and steel industry has historically supported the local football economy.

## 8. Estadio Polideportivo Cachamay, Puerto Ordaz

Capacity: limited. A secondary Puerto Ordaz venue used for multi-sport events including basketball and volleyball at international level.

## 9. Estadio Misael Delgado, Valencia

Capacity: 12,000. Valencia (Carabobo state) hosts Carabobo FC at this compact ground. Valencia is one of Venezuela's largest cities and a longstanding football centre.

## 10. Estadio Olimpico Hermanos Ghersi, Maracay

Capacity: 22,400. Maracay's municipal stadium, used by Aragua FC. The Aragua region's industrial concentration has supported Aragua FC as a recurrent Primera Division presence.

## The 2007 Copa America investment

Venezuela's hosting of the 2007 Copa America produced the country's first major stadium investment cycle. New or significantly renovated grounds in Maturin, Barquisimeto, Puerto La Cruz, and Puerto Ordaz were the result. The challenge since 2007 has been sustaining utilisation of these large venues at Primera Division club level — many of the 2007 grounds have run at attendance well below capacity.

## Primera Division Venezolana

Venezuela's top division has 20 clubs and operates with Apertura and Clausura tournaments. Caracas FC, Deportivo Tachira, and Estudiantes de Merida have been historically dominant; Monagas SC and Zamora have emerged as more recent powers. The Venezuelan league produces a steady flow of talent for South American and European clubs.

## La Vinotinto

Venezuela's national team has reached the Copa America semi-finals (2011) and has consistently competed at South American qualifying level despite never reaching a World Cup. The 2026 expanded format provides the most realistic World Cup opportunity for Venezuela in modern times. La Vinotinto's home matches are split between Caracas (Estadio Olimpico de la UCV) and Maturin (Monumental).

## Visiting Venezuelan grounds

Venezuela's domestic political and economic situation has affected international football tourism significantly. Caracas remains the entry point, but visits to provincial stadiums require careful planning. Domestic flights connect Caracas with Maracaibo, Maturin, and Puerto La Cruz, though service has been disrupted at various points. All grounds are on the [map](/map) for orientation.
