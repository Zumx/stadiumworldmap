Bolivian football is shaped by the country's extreme altitude. La Paz sits at over 3,500 metres above sea level, and Bolivian home matches against unacclimatised opponents have been controversial in CONMEBOL competition for decades. The result is a national-team venue that no visiting side can take lightly, and a club structure dominated by La Paz and Santa Cruz. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Estadio Hernando Siles, La Paz

Capacity: 41,143. Bolivia's primary national stadium, at 3,640 metres altitude in central La Paz. The Bolivia national team has produced some of CONMEBOL's most surprising results here, including a 6-1 win over Argentina in 2009 World Cup qualifying. FIFA briefly attempted to ban international matches above 2,500 metres on health grounds — the ruling was reversed after CONMEBOL protests — and the Estadio Hernando Siles remains at the centre of that ongoing debate.

## 2. Estadio Tahuichi Aguilera, Santa Cruz

Capacity: 38,000. Santa Cruz de la Sierra's main football venue, named after a former coach who founded the youth football academy Tahuichi that produced multiple Bolivian internationals. Santa Cruz sits at low altitude (about 400 metres) and is Bolivia's economic and population centre, making the ground the country's primary lowland football base.

## 3. Estadio Felix Capriles, Cochabamba

Capacity: 32,000. Cochabamba's municipal stadium, used by Aurora, Wilstermann, and Sport Boys Warnes for matches as needed. Cochabamba sits at moderate altitude (2,558 metres) and is one of Bolivia's three primary football cities alongside La Paz and Santa Cruz.

## 4. Estadio Ramon Tahuichi Aguilera Costas, Santa Cruz

Capacity: limited. A secondary Santa Cruz venue named after the Tahuichi figure. The Santa Cruz football scene includes Oriente Petrolero, Blooming, and Royal Pari as major clubs.

## 5. Estadio Hugo Sanchez Patino, El Alto

Capacity: approximately 25,000. El Alto sits at over 4,000 metres above La Paz and is one of the highest cities in the world. Always Ready, the El Alto-based club, plays at the highest altitude in CONMEBOL competition. The ground has hosted Copa Libertadores group stage matches where visiting teams have struggled visibly with the thin air.

## 6. Estadio Jesus Bermudez, Oruro

Capacity: approximately 30,000. The municipal stadium of Oruro, used by San Jose. Oruro is in the high-altitude Andean plateau at 3,706 metres and shares the altitude advantage with La Paz.

## 7. Estadio Patria, Sucre

Capacity: 30,000. Sucre's main football venue. Sucre, Bolivia's constitutional capital, is at 2,810 metres and hosts Universitario de Sucre. The ground is well-maintained for a regional Bolivian stadium.

## 8. Estadio Olimpico de Tarija

Capacity: approximately 18,000. Tarija's municipal stadium in southern Bolivia, near the Argentine border. The lower altitude and southern geography give Tarija football a different character from the La Paz altiplano.

## 9. Estadio Real Potosi

Capacity: approximately 32,000. Potosi's ground, at 4,067 metres altitude — even higher than La Paz. Real Potosi compete in the Bolivian Liga Profesional and the altitude makes the ground notably difficult for visiting teams.

## 10. Estadio Edgar Pena Gutierrez, Trinidad

Capacity: 18,000. The municipal stadium of Trinidad in the Beni region, in Bolivia's Amazon basin. The low-altitude, hot, humid setting is the opposite extreme from the altiplano grounds.

## The altitude question

Bolivian football's relationship to altitude is the country's defining sporting feature. Home advantage in La Paz, El Alto, Potosi, and Oruro is enormous — visiting teams from sea level cities (Buenos Aires, Sao Paulo, Rio de Janeiro) regularly suffer headaches, breathing difficulty, and reduced performance. CONMEBOL has resisted any move to restrict altitude football, and Bolivia's home qualifying record reflects the advantage: while Bolivia rarely qualify for World Cups, their home matches produce results that ranking position would not predict.

## The Liga Profesional

The Bolivian Liga Profesional has 16 clubs and operates on a calendar-year basis with Apertura and Clausura tournaments. The Strongest (La Paz), Bolivar (La Paz), Wilstermann (Cochabamba), Oriente Petrolero (Santa Cruz), and Always Ready (El Alto) have been the dominant clubs in recent decades. Bolivian clubs participate in Copa Libertadores and Copa Sudamericana annually.

## The Bolivian national team

Bolivia have appeared at three World Cups (1930, 1950, 1994) and won Copa America in 1963 — both Cup wins came from playing at home with altitude advantage. The 1994 World Cup appearance under Xabier Azkargorta was the high point of modern Bolivian football. Qualifying success has been limited since.

## Visiting Bolivian grounds

La Paz requires acclimatisation — visitors should plan two to three days of light activity before attending a match at altitude. The El Alto international airport sits at 4,061 metres, and even healthy visitors feel the altitude on arrival. Santa Cruz is the easier base for South American football tourists due to its low altitude and warmer climate. Domestic flights between La Paz, Santa Cruz, and Cochabamba take under an hour.
