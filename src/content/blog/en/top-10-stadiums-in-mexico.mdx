Mexico's football infrastructure ranges from the historically monumental to the recently ultramodern. The Estadio Azteca stands alone among the world's football venues for the number of occasions it has hosted — and will host, in 2026, its third World Cup final venue role. The Liga MX's commercial strength has driven a generation of new stadium construction in Monterrey, Guadalajara, and elsewhere. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Estadio Azteca, Mexico City

Capacity: 87,523. The only stadium in the world to have hosted two World Cup finals — Mexico 1970 (Brazil 4 Italy 1) and Mexico 1986 (Argentina 3 West Germany 2, the Maradona tournament) — and the only stadium scheduled to host a third, having been selected as one of the venues for the 2026 World Cup. Club America's home in the Coyoacan borough of Mexico City, the Azteca opened in 1966. At altitude — the Mexico City plateau sits at approximately 2,240 metres above sea level — the ground presents specific challenges for visiting teams unacclimatised to the thin air. The ground is currently undergoing renovation ahead of 2026. The Maradona 1986 Goals of the Century (the Hand of God and the dribbling goal against England) were both scored at the Azteca.

## 2. Estadio Olimpico Universitario, Mexico City

Capacity: approximately 72,000. The national university's stadium, built in 1952, is one of Latin America's most architecturally significant sports buildings. The exterior murals by Diego Rivera — a 10,000 square metre mosaic depicting the history of Mexican sport and culture — are a UNESCO-recognised artwork. UNAM Pumas use it as their home ground. The stadium hosted 1968 Olympic Games football events and 1986 World Cup matches.

## 3. Estadio BBVA (Estadio BBVA Bancomer), Monterrey

Capacity: 53,500. CF Monterrey's purpose-built home, opened in 2015 in Guadalupe, east of Monterrey. The mountain backdrop — the Cerro de la Silla peak is visible directly behind the south stand — is one of the most dramatic natural settings for any football stadium in the world. Designed by Populous, the BBVA Estadio is considered the best modern football ground in Mexico and among the best in Latin America. Monterrey are one of Mexico's most successful clubs of recent decades, with multiple Liga MX titles and CONCACAF Champions League victories.

## 4. Estadio Akron (previously Estadio Chivas), Guadalajara

Capacity: 49,850. Chivas de Guadalajara's purpose-built home, opened in 2010 in Zapopan, on the western edge of Guadalajara. The distinctive blue and red exterior LED facade makes it visible at night from surrounding roads. Chivas are Mexico's most passionately supported club for domestic reasons — they have a policy of fielding only Mexican nationals, which aligns the club with a nationalist identity — and the Akron regularly fills for Liga MX fixtures and CONCACAF Champions League ties. It hosted 2011 Copa América matches and will host 2026 World Cup games.

## 5. Estadio Jalisco, Guadalajara

Capacity: 56,310. Guadalajara's older stadium, still in use by Atlas FC and occasionally by Chivas for major matches. It hosted the 1970 and 1986 World Cups and remains one of Mexico's most historically significant grounds despite its age. The Clasico Tapatio — Atlas versus Chivas — is played here, giving the old ground continued relevance in the Guadalajara football calendar.

## 6. Estadio Cuauhtemoc, Puebla

Capacity: 51,736. Club Puebla's home in the city of Puebla, southeast of Mexico City. Opened in 1968 for the Olympics and used for 1970 and 1986 World Cup matches. Puebla are one of Liga MX's historically established clubs, and the Cuauhtemoc — named after the last Aztec emperor — is one of provincial Mexico's most important football grounds.

## 7. Estadio Hidalgo, Pachuca

Capacity: 30,000. Club de Futbol Pachuca's home in the silver-mining city of Pachuca, north of Mexico City. Pachuca are one of Mexico's most successful clubs with six Liga MX titles and five CONCACAF Champions League wins — a record in the region's club competition. The Hidalgo, at altitude (approximately 2,400 metres), is one of the more difficult places for visiting teams to adapt to in the region.

## 8. Estadio Caliente, Tijuana

Capacity: 33,612. Club Tijuana (Xoloitzcuintles) home in Tijuana, directly on the US-Mexico border. The Xolos were promoted to Liga MX in 2011 and the Caliente has hosted top-division football since. The border location gives the club a specific cross-cultural identity — they attract supporters from both the Tijuana side and the San Diego, California side of the border.

## 9. Estadio TSM Corona, Torreon

Capacity: 30,944. Santos Laguna's purpose-built home in the Lagunera region of northern Mexico. Santos are one of Liga MX's more successful clubs since their founding in 1983, and the TSM Corona is considered one of the better smaller stadiums in Mexico for its modern design and proximity of stands to the pitch.

## 10. Estadio Universitario, Monterrey

Capacity: 42,000. UANL Tigres' home at the Universidad Autonoma de Nuevo Leon campus in Monterrey. Tigres are one of Mexico's most successful modern clubs — Liga MX titles in 2011, 2015, 2016, 2017, and 2019, and CONCACAF Champions League wins — and the Universitario on a Clasico Regio night against Monterrey is one of the most intense derbies in Mexican football.

## The 2026 World Cup's Mexican chapter

The 2026 World Cup will be co-hosted by the United States, Mexico, and Canada. Mexico's venues — the Azteca, the Akron, the BBVA Estadio, and the Estadio Guadalajara (Akron) — will host group stage and a limited number of knockout round matches. The Azteca's selection for a third World Cup puts it in a category of one in terms of the competition's history, and its ongoing renovation ahead of 2026 is one of the more closely watched stadium projects in world football.

## Liga MX and CONCACAF Champions Cup

Liga MX is consistently ranked among the top leagues in the Americas by FIFA's club competition performance metrics. The CONCACAF Champions Cup (rebranded and reformatted in 2023-24) pits Mexican clubs against MLS clubs, and Mexican sides have historically dominated — Club America, Cruz Azul, Tigres, Monterrey, and others have all won the competition. The 2023-24 reformatting added a league phase and expanded entry, bringing more Central American clubs into earlier rounds.

The Clasico Nacional between Club America and Chivas de Guadalajara is the most-watched domestic fixture in Mexican football. When played at the Azteca, its television audience across Mexico and the United States Latino market is among the largest in North American domestic sport. When played at the Estadio Akron, the Guadalajara crowd generates a significantly different but equally passionate atmosphere.

## Altitude and the Mexican football calendar

Several Mexican grounds sit at or above 2,000 metres altitude — the Azteca, Estadio Olimpico Universitario, and Estadio Cuauhtemoc are all on the central plateau. This matters for visiting teams and for supporters who are not acclimatised. The Liga MX season runs across two short tournaments (Apertura in July to December, Clausura in January to May) rather than a single long season, which concentrates the competitive pressure and makes each tournament's knockout phase feel more intense.
