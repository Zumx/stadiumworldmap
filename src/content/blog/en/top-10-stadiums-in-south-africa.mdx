South Africa's stadium portfolio was transformed by the 2010 FIFA World Cup, the first held on African soil. The build-out produced ten match venues across the country, several of which have since become genuine multi-use legacies; others are studies in the difficulty of sustaining major sports venues without continuous top-level football. South African rugby and cricket grounds add depth to the national stadium landscape. Every venue below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. FNB Stadium (Soccer City), Johannesburg

Capacity: 94,736. Africa's largest stadium and host of the 2010 World Cup final (Spain 1-0 Netherlands after extra time). The Boogertman and Partners renovation that wrapped the existing bowl in a calabash-inspired terracotta facade produced one of the most distinctive stadium exteriors in world football. FNB Stadium now hosts Kaizer Chiefs Premier Soccer League matches and Bafana Bafana fixtures. The Nelson Mandela connection — his first major public appearance after release from prison was here in 1990 — adds historical weight.

## 2. Loftus Versfeld Stadium, Pretoria

Capacity: 51,762. The home of the Bulls (Super Rugby) and Mamelodi Sundowns (PSL). Loftus has hosted rugby since 1906 and is one of the oldest continuously-used sports grounds in South Africa. The renovation for the 2010 World Cup modernised the facilities while retaining the four-stand layout. The Bulls' three-time Super 14 / Super Rugby championships (2007, 2009, 2010) all featured Loftus as the home leg venue.

## 3. Moses Mabhida Stadium, Durban

Capacity: 56,000 (expandable). The Durban World Cup venue, defined by its 350-metre arch — visible across the city skyline — that rises 106 metres above the bowl and includes a SkyCar funicular to the top for visitors. The arch combines structural function (supporting half the roof load) with tourist amenity (the viewing deck at the apex). The mixed-use design has been more successful than many 2010 venues at sustaining post-tournament use through events and tourism.

## 4. Newlands Stadium, Cape Town

Capacity: 25,000 (rugby) / 51,900 (former cricket). The historic home of the Stormers (Super Rugby) and Western Province rugby. The cricket ground at Newlands, an adjacent but separate facility, is one of cricket's most picturesque venues with Table Mountain as backdrop. The rugby stadium is one of the oldest continuously-used rugby grounds in the world, hosting matches since 1890. A major renovation programme is reshaping the precinct.

## 5. Cape Town Stadium, Green Point

Capacity: 55,000. The Cape Town World Cup venue, sited in Green Point with views of Table Mountain and the Atlantic. The HOK and Pierre Atelier design uses translucent fibreglass cladding that diffuses ocean light. Post-2010 use has been mixed — concerts, rugby internationals, occasional football — making it one of the venues most cited in debates about World Cup stadium legacies.

## 6. Ellis Park Stadium, Johannesburg

Capacity: 62,567. The historic Johannesburg ground that hosted the 1995 Rugby World Cup final — Nelson Mandela presenting the trophy to Francois Pienaar in the Springbok jersey, an image that became part of the post-apartheid national narrative. Now known as Emirates Airline Park, the ground hosts the Lions (Super Rugby) and Orlando Pirates (PSL).

## 7. Mbombela Stadium, Mbombela

Capacity: 40,929. The Nelspruit 2010 World Cup venue, defined by its giraffe-leg-shaped steel supports that hold up the roof — one of the most visually distinctive World Cup stadium exteriors. Post-tournament use has been limited, and Mbombela is among the venues that have struggled to find a sustainable tenant programme. The stadium remains structurally and visually impressive.

## 8. Peter Mokaba Stadium, Polokwane

Capacity: 41,733. The Polokwane World Cup venue, named after the youth ANC leader. The design uses motifs of the indigenous baobab tree on its facade panels. Like Mbombela, post-tournament use has been intermittent, with the stadium relying on national-team fixtures and concerts.

## 9. Royal Bafokeng Stadium, Rustenburg

Capacity: 44,530. Owned by the Royal Bafokeng Nation — one of the few stadiums in the world owned by a traditional African kingdom. The ground hosted 2010 World Cup matches and continues to be used for football and the occasional international fixture. The traditional kingship that owns it adds a governance structure unlike any other major World Cup venue.

## 10. SuperSport Park, Centurion

Capacity: 22,000. South Africa's cricket headquarters, sited in Centurion north of Pretoria. The grass embankment behind the eastern boundary — where supporters sit on the grass rather than on seats — is part of the ground's appeal and the Proteas' Test fixtures here are among the most-attended cricket matches in the country.

## The legacy of 2010

The South African stadium portfolio remains one of the most interesting case studies in the difficulty of sustaining World Cup infrastructure after the tournament. FNB, Loftus, Moses Mabhida, and Cape Town have achieved varying degrees of post-tournament sustainability; Mbombela and Polokwane have struggled. The grounds, viewed collectively, also represent South Africa's three major sporting traditions — football, rugby, and cricket — sharing the national stadium landscape in a way few other countries match.
