Peruvian football is concentrated heavily in Lima, where the country's two biggest clubs — Universitario and Alianza Lima — share the capital. The Estadio Monumental, Universitario's home, is one of South America's largest stadiums. Outside Lima, the high-altitude grounds of Cusco and Cajamarca provide the kind of conditions that have produced famous Peru national team home wins. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Estadio Monumental de Lima, Lima

Capacity: 80,093. Universitario de Deportes' home in the Ate district of Lima, the largest stadium in Peru and one of the largest club-owned grounds in South America. The Monumental is regularly used by the Peru national team for the biggest fixtures and has hosted Copa Libertadores knockout rounds.

## 2. Estadio Nacional, Lima

Capacity: 50,000. Peru's national stadium in central Lima, originally built in 1952 and modernised for the 2004 Copa América. The ground hosts Peru national team matches, the Peruvian Cup final, and other major fixtures. The site has hosted football since 1923.

## 3. Estadio Alejandro Villanueva (Matute), Lima

Capacity: 33,931. Alianza Lima's home in the La Victoria district of central Lima, in the heart of the historic Matute neighbourhood. The ground is named after one of Peru's most famous historical players, who died in the 1987 Alianza Lima Fokker air crash. Alianza's atmosphere at Matute, particularly for derbies against Universitario, is one of South America's most intense.

## 4. Estadio Monumental de la UNSA, Arequipa

Capacity: 60,370. The very large stadium in Arequipa, built for the 2004 Copa América and hosting Universidad San Agustín FC. The high-altitude conditions at 2,335 metres provide a competitive advantage similar to other Peruvian highland venues.

## 5. Estadio Mansiche, Trujillo

Capacity: 25,000. Trujillo's principal football ground in northern Peru. Has hosted Universidad César Vallejo matches and Peru national team fixtures.

## 6. Estadio Inca Garcilaso de la Vega, Cusco

Capacity: 42,056. Cusco's principal football ground at 3,399 metres altitude — one of the highest stadiums in international football. The conditions create such a pronounced advantage that international fixtures here have caused FIFA to debate altitude limits. Cusco's clubs Cienciano (Copa Sudamericana champions 2003) and Cusco FC use the ground.

## 7. Estadio Heraclio Tapia, Huánuco

Capacity: 14,000. Huánuco's central Peru ground, at 1,900 metres altitude. Has hosted León de Huánuco and other regional Peruvian clubs.

## 8. Estadio Iván Elías Moreno, Lima (Villa El Salvador)

Capacity: 13,000. Modern Lima ground in the Villa El Salvador district, hosting various Peruvian first-division clubs.

## 9. Estadio Miguel Grau, Callao

Capacity: 17,000. The principal football ground in Callao, the Pacific port city adjacent to Lima. Named after the Peruvian admiral Miguel Grau, hero of the War of the Pacific. Sport Boys, the historic Callao club, plays here.

## 10. Estadio Mariscal Cáceres, Tacna

Capacity: 19,850. Southern Peru's principal stadium in Tacna, near the Chilean border. Hosts Peruvian football's southernmost matches and provides one of the region's larger venues.

## Peruvian Liga 1

Peru's Liga 1 has 19 clubs and runs an Apertura-Clausura format. Universitario, Alianza Lima, and Sporting Cristal — together known as the Big Three — dominate the modern era. The 2003 Copa Sudamericana win by Cienciano (Cusco) remains the only South American club trophy won by a Peruvian side outside the Big Three. The league's quality is competitive, though Peruvian clubs traditionally underperform their Argentine and Brazilian counterparts in the Copa Libertadores.

## The Lima Clásico

The Clásico Peruano (or Clásico del Fútbol Peruano) between Universitario and Alianza Lima is one of South America's most intense rivalries. The two clubs represent contrasting Lima identities — Universitario's middle-class and university base versus Alianza's working-class roots in La Victoria. The matches alternate between the Monumental and Matute, with security operations comparable to those for the Buenos Aires Superclásico.

## The Peru national team

Peru qualified for the 2018 FIFA World Cup, ending a 36-year absence. The team's home record at altitude — particularly when hosting matches in Cusco — has been a competitive lever for World Cup qualifying. Stars including Paolo Guerrero, Jefferson Farfán, and Christian Cueva have brought Peruvian football to European audiences. The Estadio Nacional in Lima and the Monumental are the two main national team venues, with occasional altitude matches in Cusco. All stadiums are marked on the [map](/map).
