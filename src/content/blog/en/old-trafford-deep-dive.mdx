Old Trafford in Greater Manchester has been Manchester United's home since 1910 and remains the largest football club ground in England, with a capacity of 74,310. The stadium — known to United supporters as the Theatre of Dreams — has hosted FA Cup Finals, Champions League knockout fixtures, England national team matches, and rugby league finals. The current debate over its future, with proposals for a complete redevelopment, has placed the historic ground at the centre of an ongoing club discussion. Old Trafford is on the [map](/map).

## Construction and opening (1909-1910)

Old Trafford was designed by Scottish architect Archibald Leitch — the dominant football-ground architect of the early twentieth century — and built between 1909 and 1910. The stadium opened on 19 February 1910 with a Manchester United v Liverpool match (a 3-4 home defeat). The site at Old Trafford in Stretford was chosen for the proximity to industrial Manchester and the railway network. The original capacity was approximately 80,000.

## World War II and the rebuild

In March 1941, German Luftwaffe bombs hit Old Trafford, destroying much of the main stand and rendering the ground unusable. Manchester United played at Manchester City's Maine Road from 1941 to 1949 while the rebuild was completed. The post-war reconstruction, funded in part by the War Damage Commission, brought Old Trafford back into use in 1949.

## The Munich air disaster and the Munich Tunnel

The 1958 Munich air disaster killed eight Manchester United players (including Roger Byrne, Tommy Taylor, and Duncan Edwards) and 15 others when the plane carrying the Busby Babes home from a European Cup match crashed at Munich-Riem Airport. The Old Trafford ground commemorates the disaster with the Munich Tunnel — a tunnel through the East Stand that displays photographs of the players who died — and with the Munich Memorial plaque outside the main stand. The Sir Matt Busby Way alongside the stadium also references the disaster's central figure.

## Bobby Charlton, George Best, and the 1968 European Cup

Manchester United won the 1968 European Cup — the first English club to do so — under Matt Busby, with Bobby Charlton, George Best, and Denis Law as the team's central figures. The semi-final 3-3 draw at Real Madrid (after a 1-0 home win) and the 4-1 extra-time final win over Benfica at Wembley remain definitive moments in English club football. Charlton, who survived the Munich disaster, captained the side, and the European Cup was dedicated to the players who died at Munich. The Bobby Charlton Stand (the South Stand, renamed in 2016) at Old Trafford honours his career.

## The Sir Alex Ferguson era

Sir Alex Ferguson's 26-year tenure at Manchester United (1986-2013) produced the most decorated period of any English club. 13 Premier League titles, two Champions League trophies (1999 and 2008), and a global supporter base that made Manchester United the most-followed football club in the world. Old Trafford was the home base for this era; the Stretford End — the traditional Manchester United supporters' end — was the section that drove the atmosphere through the 1990s and 2000s.

## The Stretford End

The Stretford End at Old Trafford is the traditional home of Manchester United's most vocal supporters. Originally a standing terrace, it became all-seater following the 1989 Hillsborough disaster and the Taylor Report. Despite the conversion, the section remains the loudest part of Old Trafford, with the United supporters who occupy it sustaining the atmospherics through full match cycles.

## Capacity and configuration

Old Trafford's current capacity of 74,310 makes it England's largest club ground. The four stands — North Stand (Sir Alex Ferguson Stand), East Stand (Stretford Paddock area), South Stand (Sir Bobby Charlton Stand), and West Stand (Stretford End) — have been incrementally rebuilt over decades. The North Stand's 2000 reconstruction added a third tier, taking the stand to a single 26,000-seat capacity — one of the largest in English football.

## The proposed redevelopment

The Ratcliffe ownership era of Manchester United (2024-) has produced proposals for a complete redevelopment of Old Trafford or for a new stadium on an adjacent site. The current ground's age (115 years), maintenance demands, and the physical limits of further expansion have placed the question of what to do with Old Trafford at the centre of the club's strategy. Any redevelopment will be politically contested given the stadium's heritage status in English football, and the timetable remains uncertain.

## Stadium tours and the museum

The Manchester United Museum at Old Trafford is one of England's most-visited sports museums, with the European Cups, FA Cups, and Premier League trophies, plus exhibits on the Munich disaster, the Busby Babes, and the Ferguson era. Stadium tours run daily when matches are not in progress and include the dressing rooms, the player tunnel, and a guided experience of the Sir Alex Ferguson Stand. The ground is at Sir Matt Busby Way, accessible from Manchester city centre by tram or by the Old Trafford Metrolink station. See the location on the [map](/map).
