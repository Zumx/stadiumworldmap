Bulgarian football lives mostly in two cities — Sofia and Plovdiv — with a scatter of smaller club grounds across the Black Sea coast and the interior. Most stadiums are publicly owned, often dating from the post-war period, and the modernisation cycle that swept neighbouring countries has barely begun here. The result is a stadium stock that is historically textured but visibly in need of investment. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Vasil Levski National Stadium, Sofia

Capacity: approximately 43,230. Bulgaria's national stadium, built in 1953 and renovated several times, sits in Borisova Gradina park near central Sofia. It hosts the Bulgaria national team, the Bulgarian Cup final, and major Sofia derbies when capacity at the club grounds is insufficient. The stadium has a running track and an open athletics-stadium character that places the front rows at some distance from the pitch.

## 2. Bulgarska Armiya Stadium, Sofia

Capacity: approximately 22,995 (currently restricted during reconstruction). CSKA Sofia's traditional home in Borisova Gradina park. The stadium has been the centre of a long-running reconstruction project; CSKA played some recent matches at the Vasil Levski while building works progressed. CSKA Sofia were European Cup semi-finalists in 1967 and 1982, and the club is one of Bulgaria's two historical giants.

## 3. Georgi Asparuhov Stadium, Sofia

Capacity: 28,940. Levski Sofia's home, named after a club legend who died young in 1971. The ground is in the Poduyane district and is one of Bulgaria's better-maintained club stadiums. The Sofia derby between Levski and CSKA — known locally as the Eternal Derby — is played at one of these two grounds depending on the configuration.

## 4. Lazur Stadium, Burgas

Capacity: 18,037. The seaside city of Burgas hosts a municipal ground used by PFC Chernomorets Burgas and Neftohimik. The Black Sea coast position gives the ground an unusual context for Bulgarian football, and Burgas has produced a stream of national-team players relative to its population size.

## 5. Hristo Botev Stadium, Plovdiv

Capacity: approximately 18,728. Botev Plovdiv's home in Bulgaria's second city. Plovdiv has two major clubs — Botev and Lokomotiv — and the local derby is one of the country's most contested. The Hristo Botev ground has been progressively renovated and remains in regular Parva Liga use.

## 6. Lokomotiv Plovdiv Stadium

Capacity: approximately 12,000. Lokomotiv Plovdiv's home, opened in its current form in 2020 as one of the few significant new stadium constructions in modern Bulgarian football. The compact, modern bowl is closer to European-standard new builds than any other Bulgarian first-division ground.

## 7. Beroe Stadium, Stara Zagora

Capacity: 13,138. PFC Beroe Stara Zagora's home in central Bulgaria. The city of Stara Zagora is one of Bulgaria's historically important football centres, and Beroe's 1986 Bulgarian Cup win remains one of the club's most cited achievements.

## 8. Hadzhi Dimitar Stadium, Sliven

Capacity: approximately 12,000. Sliven's municipal ground, used by PFC Sliven and historically associated with the Sliven football tradition. The Balkan setting gives the ground a different geographic context from the Sofia and Plovdiv venues.

## 9. Stadion Naroden Sport, Plovdiv (Park 1900)

Capacity: variable. Several Plovdiv-area municipal pitches and training grounds carry historical weight in Bulgarian football. Plovdiv's football culture is among the country's most deeply rooted.

## 10. Stadion Dunav, Ruse

Capacity: approximately 13,000. PFC Dunav Ruse's home on the Danube. Ruse is northern Bulgaria's largest city and produces a regular flow of first-division players. The riverside location and the Romanian border proximity give Ruse football a specific cross-border character.

## Parva Liga and the structural challenge

Bulgaria's top division — Parva Liga or Efbet Liga depending on the sponsor — has 14 to 16 clubs depending on the season. CSKA Sofia, Levski Sofia, Ludogorets Razgrad, Botev Plovdiv, and Lokomotiv Plovdiv have been the most consistent presences. Ludogorets, based in the small town of Razgrad in northeast Bulgaria, transformed Bulgarian football by winning eleven consecutive titles from 2012 to 2022 — a dominance that highlighted both the club's investment and the relative weakness of the established Sofia clubs.

## The Eternal Derby

The Sofia derby between Levski and CSKA is one of southeastern Europe's most charged fixtures. Levski's working-class associations and CSKA's military origins (Central Sports Club of the Army) divide Sofia along lines that predate the current generation of supporters. Match days carry the visible weight of a rivalry that has been continuous since the late 1940s.

## Visiting Bulgarian grounds

Sofia's centre is a 20-minute walk from the Vasil Levski National Stadium, with the Asparuhov and Armiya grounds short tram or metro rides away. Plovdiv requires a separate visit — the train from Sofia takes around two hours and is the standard route. Tickets for Parva Liga matches are widely available on matchday and rarely sell out outside the Sofia and Plovdiv derbies. All grounds are on the [map](/map) for planning purposes.
