Danish football's stadium picture is dominated by Copenhagen's Parken — the national stadium and home of FC Copenhagen — and by a network of regional grounds in cities like Brøndby, Herning, Aarhus and Aalborg. The Danish Superliga is structured around clubs in cities of modest population by international standards, which produces modest stadium capacities but consistently high attendance percentages. ## 1. Parken, Copenhagen

Capacity: 38,065. Denmark's national stadium in the Østerbro district of Copenhagen, opened in 1992 on the site of the demolished Idrætsparken. Parken is the home of FC Copenhagen and hosts the Danish national team. The stadium has a partial retractable roof — one of the earliest retractable systems in European football, although it covers only part of the bowl. Parken hosted three matches of UEFA Euro 2020 (delayed to 2021). FC Copenhagen are Denmark's most successful modern club with 15 Superliga titles since 1992.

## 2. Brøndby Stadium, Brøndby

Capacity: 28,000. Home of Brøndby IF in the Copenhagen suburb of Brøndby, opened in 1965 and renovated multiple times. The South Stand contains Denmark's largest standing supporter section and produces some of Scandinavian football's loudest atmospheres. Brøndby are the historical rivals of FC Copenhagen, and the New Firm derby — formally known as the Battle of Copenhagen — divides the city. Brøndby's golden era of the late 1980s and 1990s produced UEFA Cup semi-final runs.

## 3. MCH Arena, Herning

Capacity: 11,800. Home of FC Midtjylland in the central Jutland town of Herning. Opened in 2004 and significantly expanded after Midtjylland's emergence as a Superliga title contender. Midtjylland have won the Superliga in 2014-15, 2017-18, 2019-20 and 2022-23, becoming the most consistent challenger to the Copenhagen clubs. The club's data-driven approach to recruitment and tactics has made Midtjylland one of Europe's most-studied small-market football operations.

## 4. Aalborg Portland Park, Aalborg

Capacity: 14,072. Home of AaB (Aalborg Boldspilklub) in North Jutland. Originally opened in 1960 and renovated multiple times, most recently in 2008. AaB have won the Superliga title three times since the league's founding — 1994-95, 1998-99, 2007-08 — and the ground has hosted European competition group matches regularly.

## 5. Ceres Park, Aarhus

Capacity: 19,433. Home of AGF (Aarhus Gymnastikforening), Denmark's second-largest city's main club. Originally opened in 1920 and renovated several times. AGF were Danish champions in 1986 and have been a consistent Superliga presence since. The ground's history extends through the club's golden era of the 1950s and 1960s and into the modern competitive era.

## 6. Telia Parken (alternative name for Parken)

Capacity: 38,065. The same ground as Parken above, listed here only because the naming sponsorship rotation has produced multiple names over the years (Telia Parken until 2022, then back to Parken). The historic name "Idrætsparken" referred to the original 1911 stadium.

## 7. Right to Dream Park, Farum

Capacity: 10,500. FC Nordsjælland's home in the Copenhagen suburb of Farum, opened in 2002 and expanded. FC Nordsjælland's emergence as a Superliga title-winning club in 2011-12 was a remarkable underdog story. The club's Right to Dream academy partnership (with Ghana) has produced a steady stream of African talent that has transferred to top European leagues.

## 8. Vejle Stadion, Vejle

Capacity: 10,418. Home of Vejle BK in southern Jutland. The current ground, opened in 1957 and renovated, replaces older venues that hosted Vejle's golden era in the 1970s when they won multiple Danish championships. Vejle BK's contemporary status oscillates between the Superliga and the first division.

## 9. Lyngby Stadion, Lyngby

Capacity: 12,000. Home of Lyngby BK in the Copenhagen suburb of Lyngby. The compact ground has been the venue for multiple Lyngby Superliga campaigns since the 1990s. Lyngby's modest scale reflects the club's traditional position as one of the smaller capital-area sides relative to FC Copenhagen and Brøndby.

## 10. Casa Arena, Horsens

Capacity: 10,400. Home of AC Horsens in eastern Jutland. The compact stadium represents the typical Danish second-tier or recently promoted Superliga venue — modest capacity, well-built, accessible to local supporters. Horsens have spent recent seasons oscillating between the Superliga and the first division.

## The Danish Superliga

The Danish Superliga has 12 teams and uses a split-season format with a championship round of six and a relegation round of six after the regular phase. FC Copenhagen, Brøndby, Midtjylland, Nordsjælland and AaB have been the most consistently competitive clubs of the past decade. The league's average attendance has grown substantially in the past 15 years, with the FC Copenhagen / Midtjylland competitive duopoly producing high-quality matches for European broadcast.

## The New Firm derby

The Copenhagen derby between FC Copenhagen and Brøndby — known as the New Firm or as Battle of Copenhagen — is Danish football's biggest match. FC Copenhagen, the city centre club founded only in 1992 (following a merger), and Brøndby, the suburban club with deeper working-class roots, divide the capital geographically and culturally. The fixture has produced some of Scandinavia's most charged matchday atmospheres.

## Danish football culture

Danish football culture is distinct from the bigger European leagues — modest stadium sizes, high attendance percentages, strong supporter ownership traditions (Brøndby is a publicly listed company, FC Nordsjælland has community ownership elements), and a national league competitive enough to produce regular UEFA group stage qualifiers. The national team's 1992 European Championship — won as a late-call replacement for Yugoslavia — remains the country's defining football moment.
