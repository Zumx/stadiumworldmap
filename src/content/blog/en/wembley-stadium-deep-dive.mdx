Wembley Stadium in north-west London is the home of English football. The current ground, opened in 2007 on the site of the original 1923 Wembley, hosts the England national team's biggest matches, the FA Cup Final, the Championship play-off finals, NFL London Games, and the country's largest concerts. The 133-metre arch over the north stand is one of London's most recognisable structures. The stadium is on the [map](/map).

## The original Wembley (1923-2003)

The first Wembley was built in 1922-1923 for the British Empire Exhibition, with the twin towers at the south end becoming the stadium's defining feature for eight decades. The opening event was the 1923 FA Cup Final between Bolton Wanderers and West Ham — the so-called "White Horse Final," at which an estimated 200,000-300,000 fans broke into the ground, far exceeding the planned 127,000 capacity. PC George Scorey on his white horse Billy helped clear the pitch for the match, which Bolton won 2-0.

The original Wembley hosted the 1948 London Olympics' opening ceremony and athletics, the 1966 World Cup Final (England 4-2 West Germany), Live Aid in 1985, and countless FA Cup Finals. The decision to demolish it for a complete rebuild was politically and emotionally contested but eventually decided by the structural and modern-amenity limitations of the original bowl.

## The new Wembley (2007-)

Norman Foster and Partners, working with HOK Sport (now Populous), designed the new Wembley as a 90,000-seat bowl with a single 133-metre steel arch carrying the roof structure over the north end. The arch is the longest single-span roof structure in the world and is visible from much of London on clear days. The construction project, by Multiplex, ran from 2002 to 2007 and was famously beset by delays and cost overruns — the final bill of approximately £798 million made Wembley the most expensive stadium ever built in the UK at that time.

The new ground replaces the twin towers with the arch as the stadium's signature element. The interior was designed to address every shortcoming of the original Wembley: better sightlines, modern concourses able to handle 90,000 people moving simultaneously, full hospitality offerings, and excellent acoustic properties.

## The FA Cup Final and the road to Wembley

The FA Cup Final has been held at Wembley since 1923 (with brief exceptions during the rebuild, when matches were played at the Millennium Stadium in Cardiff). The "Road to Wembley" — the ritual progression of FA Cup teams through the rounds — is one of football's most cherished traditions, with non-league sides occasionally reaching the third round and earning ties against Premier League opposition. The Cup Final itself, traditionally held on a Saturday in May, remains an event of national consequence even as the financial dominance of the Premier League has reduced the trophy's relative status.

## England fixtures and Euro 2020

The England national team plays its principal home fixtures at Wembley. Major tournament matches hosted include the 1966 World Cup Final, the 1996 European Championship Final, and the 2020 European Championship Final (delayed to 2021), which Italy won 3-2 on penalties after a 1-1 draw against England. The Euro 2020 final at Wembley was marred by crowd-management problems and supporter behaviour outside the ground that led to a UEFA investigation and a behind-closed-doors sanction for a future fixture.

## Champions League finals

Wembley has hosted the UEFA Champions League Final five times (1963, 1968, 1971, 1992, 1978, 2011, 2013, 2024 if counting the recent additions). The 2011 final — Barcelona's 3-1 win over Manchester United — was widely cited as one of the great club performances. The 2013 all-German final (Bayern 2-1 Borussia Dortmund), the 2024 final (Real Madrid 2-0 Borussia Dortmund), and Wembley's selection for the 2024 final reaffirmed the stadium's place in UEFA's premier rotation.

## NFL London and other sports

Wembley has hosted NFL London Games since 2007, with up to four matches per season in some years. The stadium also hosts rugby league Challenge Cup Finals, Olympic football (during London 2012), and other major sporting events. Mention is made of every event because the ground's calendar reflects English sport's broader commercial pressures: monetising 90,000-seat capacity demands a far broader event mix than football alone could provide.

## The arch

The Norman Foster arch is the structural and visual element that defines new Wembley. At 133 metres tall and spanning 315 metres across the stadium, it carries the entire weight of the north side of the roof while allowing the south half to be cable-suspended, free of any columns that would obstruct the stands. The arch is illuminated at night and changes colour to mark club competitions, national events, and tributes — when England wins a major fixture, it lights white; for women's events, often red and blue; for memorial occasions, single colours depending on the cause.

## Visiting

Wembley is open for stadium tours that run most days when not preparing for an event. The tour includes the dressing rooms, the players' tunnel walk, the trophy display area, and the iconic 107-step walk up the trophy steps. The Wembley Park area surrounding the stadium has been heavily redeveloped, with the Boxpark Wembley restaurant complex providing a matchday experience adjacent to the ground.
