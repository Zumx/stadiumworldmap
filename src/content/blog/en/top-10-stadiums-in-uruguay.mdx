Uruguay's football culture is among the deepest in world football relative to the country's small population. Montevideo's two giant rivals — Peñarol and Nacional — produce one of the most intense city derbies in South America, and the Estadio Centenario is one of the most historically significant football grounds in existence. The 1930 World Cup, the first in history, was played and won by Uruguay on home soil; the Centenario was built for it. Every venue below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Estadio Centenario, Montevideo

Capacity: 60,235. The first FIFA World Cup stadium, opened in July 1930 for the inaugural World Cup. Uruguay won the 1930 final at the Centenario, beating Argentina 4-2. The ground has been continuously used since and remains the national team's primary venue. FIFA designated the Centenario as a "Monument of World Football" — the only stadium so honoured. The Olímpico stand and the four-side bowl have been preserved largely as they were in 1930.

## 2. Estadio Campeón del Siglo, Montevideo

Capacity: 40,000. Peñarol's modern home, opened in 2016 in the outskirts of Montevideo. The new ground replaced decades of Peñarol playing home matches at the shared Centenario. The compact bowl with steep stands produces excellent atmospheres for Peñarol's Primera División matches and Copa Libertadores fixtures. The four-stand layout, with the South stand (La Norte for ultras-style support) generating most matchday noise.

## 3. Gran Parque Central, Montevideo

Capacity: 34,000. Nacional's traditional ground, opened in 1900 — the oldest continuously-used football stadium in South America. The ground hosted the inaugural match of the 1930 World Cup (USA vs Belgium) before the Centenario was completed. The historical depth of Gran Parque Central matches that of any club football venue worldwide, and Nacional's matchday culture here is one of the most consistently passionate in South America.

## 4. Estadio Domingo Burgueño Miguel, Maldonado

Capacity: 22,000. The largest stadium outside Montevideo, in the resort city of Maldonado. The ground hosts Uruguay national-team matches when Montevideo venues are unavailable and has been a Copa América venue. The Atlantic coastal location distinguishes the stadium from the Montevideo-centric Uruguayan football geography.

## 5. Estadio Atilio Paiva Olivera, Rivera

Capacity: 13,500. The main ground in Rivera on the Brazilian border. Uruguay-Brazil cross-border football has long made this one of the more interesting regional grounds. The stadium hosts Uruguayan Primera División matches and the occasional regional fixture.

## 6. Estadio Centenario (Salto Department)

Capacity: 12,000. The main stadium in Salto, the third-largest Uruguayan city. The ground hosts Uruguayan Primera División matches and represents the northern provincial Uruguay infrastructure. The shared name with Montevideo's Centenario can confuse first-time researchers.

## 7. Estadio Luis Tróccoli, Montevideo

Capacity: 18,000. Cerro's home in the Cerro district of Montevideo, named for the former club president. Cerro's traditional working-class identity in Montevideo's Atlantic-coast neighbourhood makes the Tróccoli a representative example of the country's working-class football culture.

## 8. Estadio Centenario (Florida Department)

Capacity: 8,000. A regional ground in central Uruguay. Multiple stadiums in Uruguay carry the Centenario name; this is one of the smaller examples. Florida's Primera División-level Uruguayan football has occasionally seen Copa Sudamericana qualification fixtures here.

## 9. Estadio Belvedere, Montevideo

Capacity: 12,500. Liverpool FC Montevideo's home in the Belvedere district of the capital. The ground predates most other Uruguayan football venues and represents the early-20th-century era of stadium construction in the country.

## 10. Estadio Charrúa, Montevideo

Capacity: 14,000. A multi-purpose Montevideo ground used by Uruguay's women's national team, the rugby Los Teros, and the Uruguayan Primera División. The renovation of the early 2010s modernised the facilities while keeping the compact urban siting.

## Uruguay's small country, deep football culture

The disproportion between Uruguay's tiny population (approximately 3.5 million) and its football culture's depth is one of world football's defining anomalies. The country has won the World Cup twice (1930, 1950), reached multiple semifinals, and produced generations of major players. The Centenario remains the symbol of this football culture, and the Peñarol-Nacional rivalry at the Campeón del Siglo and Gran Parque Central continues to generate domestic intensity. Few small countries have produced a stadium portfolio as historically significant as Uruguay's.
