German football infrastructure is among the most consistently high-quality in the world. The 2006 World Cup prompted a generation of renovation and new construction, and the Bundesliga's sustained commercial success has allowed clubs to maintain and upgrade their grounds at a level few other leagues match. The 50+1 rule — which requires clubs to retain majority voting rights and prevents the kind of sole-investor ownership common in England — shapes stadium economics in ways that favour long-term investment over speculative development. ## 1. Allianz Arena, Munich

Capacity: 75,024 for Bundesliga; approximately 66,000 for UEFA competitions. FC Bayern Munich's home, opened in 2005 in Freimann, north Munich, designed by Herzog and de Meuron. The ETFE foil cushion exterior — 2,874 panels individually lit — changes colour between red (Bayern), white (Germany national team), and previously blue (TSV 1860 Munich). The arena has no running track and the lower tier puts supporters very close to the pitch. Bayern have won multiple Bundesliga titles and Champions League trophies since moving here from the Olympiastadion. The ground hosted Euro 2024 matches including a semifinal.

## 2. Signal Iduna Park, Dortmund

Capacity: 81,365 for Bundesliga; approximately 66,000 for UEFA competitions. Borussia Dortmund's home holds the largest terrace in world football: the Sudtribune (Yellow Wall), accommodating 25,000 standing supporters in a single enclosed end. On matchdays, the Wall generates a noise level that visiting players and managers regularly identify as the most intimidating environment in European club football. The ground opened in 1974 and has been expanded several times. Dortmund's 2023 Champions League final run and their consistent Bundesliga competition with Bayern have kept the ground in regular high-level European focus.

## 3. Olympiastadion Berlin

Capacity: 74,475. Built for the 1936 Berlin Olympics and used by the Nazi regime as a propaganda stage. Renovated between 2000 and 2004 for the 2006 World Cup, the Olympiastadion now hosts Hertha BSC's Bundesliga home matches and the DFB-Pokal (German Cup) final annually. The renovation preserved the original limestone exterior while rebuilding the roof and interior. It also hosted the 2006 World Cup final (Italy 1 France 1, Italy won on penalties). The combination of historical weight and continued active use makes it one of the most significant sports buildings in the world.

## 4. Olympiastadion Munchen

Capacity: approximately 69,250 (now primarily for concerts and events). The 1972 Munich Olympics stadium, designed by Frei Otto and Gunter Behnisch, introduced the acrylic-glass tent-roof structure that defined 1970s architectural ambition. FC Bayern played here from 1972 until their move to the Allianz Arena in 2005. The stadium is now used for concerts, the Bayern Amateure, and occasional events — it hosted the first-ever Bundesliga game in 1963 at a predecessor ground. Its listed-building status precludes significant modification.

## 5. Veltins-Arena, Gelsenkirchen

Capacity: 62,271 (62,000 domestic; 54,000 UEFA). Schalke 04's home opened in 2001 with a retractable roof and a pitch that rolls out of the arena on rails for maintenance. The tunnel under the north stand brings coaches directly to pitch level. Schalke's large and passionate supporter base makes the Veltins-Arena one of the Bundesliga's most consistently sold-out venues regardless of the club's league position. The arena hosted Euro 2024 group stage and round of 16 matches.

## 6. MHPArena, Stuttgart

Capacity: 60,449. VfB Stuttgart's home underwent comprehensive renovation ahead of Euro 2024. The ground opened in 1933 and has been rebuilt and expanded multiple times, with the most recent renovation closing the east stand and adding capacity. Stuttgart's rollercoaster history — Bundesliga title in 2007, relegation in 2016 and 2019, return to the top flight — means the MHPArena has seen both extremes. Hosted Euro 2024 group stage and a quarterfinal.

## 7. Deutsche Bank Park, Frankfurt

Capacity: 58,000. Eintracht Frankfurt's home in the Sachsenhausen area underwent a roof closure programme completed in 2005 that converted an open bowl to a fully enclosed venue. Frankfurt won the Europa League in 2022, their first European trophy since 1980, in a campaign that attracted 30,000 Frankfurt supporters to the final in Seville — an unusual logistical feat. The enclosed design makes the stadium very loud on big European nights. Hosted Euro 2024 group stage and round of 16 matches.

## 8. Rhein Energie Stadion, Cologne

Capacity: 50,000. FC Cologne's home, rebuilt for the 2006 World Cup. The enclosed bowl with the distinctive ribbed roof panels is one of the Bundesliga's most architecturally consistent venues. Cologne's passionate supporter culture — they attract large home attendances even in difficult seasons — makes the Rhein Energie one of the Bundesliga's reliable atmospheres. Hosted Euro 2024 group stage matches.

## 9. Volksparkstadion (Volksparkstadion Hamburg)

Capacity: 57,000. Hamburger SV's home has been in continuous use since 1953, predating all other current Bundesliga grounds. Hamburg were famously relegated from the Bundesliga in 2018, ending a 55-year unbroken run in the top division, and the Volksparkstadion hosted 2. Bundesliga football until HSV returned to the top flight in 2024. The ground hosted Euro 2024 group stage and a semifinal.

## 10. Volkswagen Arena, Wolfsburg

Capacity: 30,000. VfL Wolfsburg's purpose-built home, opened in 2002 and funded substantially by Volkswagen AG, whose headquarters dominate the company town of Wolfsburg. Small by top-division standards but well-designed, compact, and consistently used. Wolfsburg's 2009 Bundesliga title remains one of the division's most surprising, won at a ground that reflects the industrial patronage model of German club football in its purest form.

## Germany's Euro 2024 investment

Germany hosted Euro 2024 using ten stadiums across the country. The tournament prompted significant renovation spending — Stuttgart, Hamburg, Frankfurt, Cologne, and others all received upgrades. Unlike the 2006 World Cup, where new construction was substantial, 2024 was primarily a renovation and infrastructure cycle. The result is a Bundesliga with some of the best-maintained stadium infrastructure in European football.

## Standing and ticket prices

The Bundesliga is routinely cited as offering the best value in top European football. A standing Bundesliga ticket — at Signal Iduna Park, the Allianz Arena, or the Volksparkstadion — costs significantly less than an equivalent Premier League ticket, reflecting the 50+1 rule's structural effect on club economics. Bayern Munich standing tickets in Block 108 (Nordkurve) sell for approximately 15 to 17 euros per match for season-ticket holders. Single-match prices are higher but remain far below English equivalents.

The standing culture and the price differential together explain why Bundesliga attendances are so consistently high relative to stadium capacity. Signal Iduna Park, with its 81,365 capacity, regularly records 100 percent attendance across a full Bundesliga season. No club in the English Premier League comes close to that figure despite significantly higher average ticket prices.

## Getting to German stadiums

Germany's rail network makes multi-city stadium visits practical. Munich's Allianz Arena is on U-Bahn line U6 (Freimann station). Dortmund's Signal Iduna Park is directly adjacent to the Stadion S-Bahn station. Frankfurt's Deutsche Bank Park is a short U-Bahn journey from Frankfurt Hauptbahnhof. Berlin's Olympiastadion is on S-Bahn line S3 and S9. All grounds are on the [map](/map) and their proximity to city centres can be judged directly from the satellite view.
