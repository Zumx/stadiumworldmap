Dutch football infrastructure reflects a country with a strong club tradition and relatively compact geography. The Eredivisie's top clubs are served by modern or well-maintained grounds that punch above the league's population size, and the country has been at the forefront of supporter-focused stadium design in several cases. ## 1. Johan Cruijff Arena, Amsterdam

Capacity: 54,990. AFC Ajax's home in the Amsterdam ArenA district (the stadium gave its name to the development zone), opened in 1996 as the first major stadium in Europe with a retractable roof. Renamed Johan Cruijff Arena in 2018 in honour of the club's most celebrated player and manager. The roof closes in approximately 18 minutes and has been used for concerts, making the arena one of Europe's busiest multi-use venues. Ajax are the Netherlands' most successful European club, with four European Cup and Champions League titles. The arena hosted Euro 2020 group stage matches. Amsterdam's excellent public transport connects the stadium directly to Amsterdam Centraal via the metro.

## 2. De Kuip (Stadion Feijenoord), Rotterdam

Capacity: 47,500. Feyenoord Rotterdam's home since 1937, De Kuip (the Tub) is one of the Netherlands' most historically significant football grounds. The elliptical bowl design by Leendert van der Vlugt is a modernist landmark, and the ground was renovated in 1994. Feyenoord have pursued plans for a new stadium — New Feyenoord Stadion, with a planned capacity of 63,000 — for years, with the project in development as of 2024. The Rotterdam derby against Sparta Rotterdam makes De Kuip one of the most intense atmospheres in Dutch football. Feyenoord's 2022 Europa League final run gave the ground its most prominent recent European exposure.

## 3. Philips Stadion, Eindhoven

Capacity: 35,000. PSV Eindhoven's home, named after the Philips corporation whose Eindhoven headquarters have been linked to the club since its founding. The compact bowl is enclosed and produces a concentration of noise unusual for its capacity. PSV have been the Netherlands' most consistently successful club of recent decades, winning the Eredivisie title in multiple consecutive seasons. The stadium hosted Euro 2020 group stage matches and has hosted Champions League football regularly.

## 4. Grolsch Veste, Enschede

Capacity: 30,205. FC Twente's home in Enschede, near the German border, is one of the Netherlands' more modern and well-designed club grounds. The angular roof and enclosed corners create a good atmosphere for a regional club. Twente's 2010 Eredivisie title was their first and only league championship, and the Grolsch Veste hosted a brief period of Champions League football in 2010-11.

## 5. Stadion Galgenwaard, Utrecht

Capacity: 24,500. FC Utrecht's home, rebuilt in 1982 and expanded progressively since. The location in the southern Utrecht suburb of Lunetten is well-connected to the city centre by public transport. Utrecht are a reliable Eredivisie presence and the Galgenwaard has hosted UEFA Cup and Europa League preliminary round matches on several occasions.

## 6. AFAS Stadion, Alkmaar

Capacity: 17,023. AZ Alkmaar's compact home has hosted Champions League group stage football — AZ's 2009-10 Champions League campaign and their 2024-25 campaign both used the ground. The small capacity makes tickets scarce for European nights. AZ are one of the Netherlands' most consistently well-run clubs at a size below the Ajax-PSV-Feyenoord tier.

## 7. GelreDome, Arnhem

Capacity: 26,600 (21,000 for football). Vitesse Arnhem's retractable-roof stadium opened in 1998, predating the widespread adoption of roof technology in European football. The artificial pitch and retractable-field system make it one of the Netherlands' more technically complex venues. Vitesse are associated through their history with Chelsea FC's loan player system, which sent dozens of young Chelsea players to Arnhem for first-team experience over more than a decade.

## 8. Cars Jeans Stadion, The Hague

Capacity: 15,000. ADO Den Haag's compact urban ground in the Duindorp area of The Hague is one of the older Eredivisie venues still in active use. Den Haag's relatively modest history at the top of Dutch football means the ground has operated primarily at Eredivisie and Eerste Divisie (second division) level. The intimate scale makes it loud for its size.

## 9. Euroborg, Groningen

Capacity: 22,530. FC Groningen's purpose-built home opened in 2006 in the Europapark district of Groningen, the northernmost major city in the Netherlands. The enclosed bowl and proximity of stands to the pitch give the Euroborg a better atmosphere than its modest capacity might suggest. Groningen are a reliable Eredivisie presence representing the Netherlands' largest northern city.

## 10. MAC3PARK Stadion, Zwolle

Capacity: 12,500. PEC Zwolle's compact ground serves a club that has twice won the KNVB Cup (2014 and 2015) while playing in one of the Eredivisie's smaller venues. Zwolle's cup success came as a genuine upset — defeating Ajax in the 2014 final — and the celebrations in the small ground were among Dutch football's more joyful recent moments.

## The Netherlands and European football culture

The Dutch football tradition — Total Football, the Ajax academy, the coach export network — is disproportionate to the country's size. The stadiums at the top of this list have hosted some of European football's landmark games: Ajax's 1995 Champions League final win over Milan was prepared at the old De Meer Stadion (replaced by the Cruijff Arena), and PSV's 1988 European Cup win was won by a club from a city of 230,000 people. The grounds in the Netherlands are reachable from Amsterdam Centraal in under three hours; the country is compact enough that a multi-ground visit is practically straightforward.

## Ajax, PSV, Feyenoord: the big three

Dutch football's structure is defined by three clubs whose resources significantly exceed the rest. Ajax's youth academy — the famed Ajax Jeugdopleiding — has produced more players for Europe's top clubs than almost any other development system in the world: Johan Cruyff, Marco van Basten, Patrick Kluivert, Clarence Seedorf, Zlatan Ibrahimovic, and many others were developed here. The Johan Cruijff Arena reflects that ambition in its size and modernity. Feyenoord's De Kuip is the stadium of Rotterdam's working port identity — the ground and the club are bound together by the city's industrial character in the same way that the Dortmund Sudtribune reflects the Ruhr. PSV's Philips Stadion is the most compact of the three but has produced a higher proportion of recent domestic titles. Outside the big three, AZ Alkmaar and Twente have both demonstrated that smaller Eredivisie clubs can compete in European group stages with the right coaching structure and stadium investment.

## Planning a Dutch stadium visit

The Johan Cruijff Arena is on the Amsterdam Metro line 50 (Amstelveen to Isolatorweg), stopping at Amsterdam ArenA/Bijlmer. De Kuip in Rotterdam is accessible from Rotterdam Centraal by tram and bus. Philips Stadion in Eindhoven is a 20-minute walk from Eindhoven Centraal station. The compact geography of the Netherlands — Amsterdam, Rotterdam, The Hague, Utrecht, and Eindhoven form a triangle reachable by intercity train in under an hour — means that combining two grounds in a weekend requires no overnight travel between cities.
