Argentine football is one of the most passionate and architecturally varied in the world. The country's top grounds range from the intimate and intimidating to the enormous and historic, concentrated in Buenos Aires but spread across Córdoba, La Plata, and Mar del Plata. Every stadium below is marked on the [map](/map).

## 1. La Bombonera, Buenos Aires

Home to Boca Juniors in the working-class La Boca neighbourhood, the Estadio Alberto J. Armando — universally known as La Bombonera — holds approximately 54,000 and is one of world football's most atmospheric grounds. The three-tiered stands on three sides and a single straight stand on the fourth create a sound dynamic that produces an extraordinary volume. Opened in 1940, renovated progressively, the ground sits on a cramped city block that prevents expansion. Boca's supporter organisation, La 12, fills the terrace behind the goal for every home match. Copa Libertadores nights here are considered unmissable by serious football travellers.

## 2. El Monumental (Estadio Antonio Vespucio Liberti), Buenos Aires

River Plate's Estadio Antonio Vespucio Liberti, known as El Monumental, holds approximately 84,567 after recent renovations, making it the largest stadium in South America. Located in the Nunez neighbourhood, it hosted the 1978 World Cup final in which Argentina defeated the Netherlands 3-1 in extra time. The renovated third tier completed in the 2010s added capacity and improved sightlines. River's Copa Libertadores back-and-forth with Boca during the late 2010s gave El Monumental some of the most intense South American nights in recent memory.

## 3. Estadio Mario Alberto Kempes, Córdoba

Named after the striker who was Argentina's hero at the 1978 World Cup, the Estadio Mario Alberto Kempes holds approximately 57,000 and is the largest stadium outside Buenos Aires. It was one of the venues for the 1978 World Cup and hosted matches at the 1987 Copa América. Talleres and Belgrano share the ground for major matches. The city of Córdoba uses it for national team fixtures and international events.

## 4. Estadio Unico de La Plata (Ciudad de La Plata)

Opened in 2003, the Estadio Unico de La Plata holds approximately 53,000 and was purpose-built as a national-team-standard ground for the city of La Plata, 60 kilometres south of Buenos Aires. Gimnasia y Estudiantes — the two local clubs — use it for major fixtures. The rounded oval design and open upper tier give it a different character from Buenos Aires' enclosed club grounds. It hosted 2011 Copa América group stage matches.

## 5. Estadio Jose Amalfitani, Buenos Aires

Vélez Sarsfield's home in the Liniers neighbourhood holds approximately 49,540. Opened in 1943 and developed progressively, it is one of Argentina's best-maintained club grounds. Vélez won the 1994 Copa Libertadores and the Intercontinental Cup in 1994, and the stadium saw both those campaigns. The Liniers location, slightly removed from the main Buenos Aires football geography, gives the ground a self-contained atmosphere.

## 6. Estadio Tomas Adolfo Duco, Buenos Aires

Huracán's home in the Parque Patricios neighbourhood holds approximately 48,000. Known informally as El Palacio, it is a three-tiered Buenos Aires club ground of the traditional Argentine style. Huracán's supporter culture is intense for a club of its size, and the compact urban site intensifies the noise inside the bowl.

## 7. Estadio Diego Armando Maradona, Buenos Aires

Renamed in honour of Diego Maradona after his death in November 2020, Argentinos Juniors' ground in the Villa del Parque neighbourhood holds approximately 26,000. It is small by Argentine first-division standards but significant: it was the club where Maradona developed as a player in the late 1970s before his move to Boca Juniors. The renaming was immediate and universal.

## 8. Estadio Pedro Bidegain, Buenos Aires

San Lorenzo's home — known as El Nuevo Gasometro — in the Bajo Flores neighbourhood holds approximately 47,964. San Lorenzo won the 2014 Copa Libertadores and are one of Argentina's most successful clubs of recent decades. The ground was rebuilt in the early 1990s after San Lorenzo spent over a decade without their own stadium following the compulsory purchase and demolition of their original ground under the Videla military government.

## 9. Estadio Jose Maria Minella, Mar del Plata

The Estadio Jose Maria Minella in Mar del Plata, on the Atlantic coast, holds approximately 35,000 and serves as the primary stadium for the coastal city's football. It hosted matches during the 1978 World Cup. Mar del Plata's clubs — Aldosivi and Alvarado — use it for major fixtures, and the national team has played there for internationals.

## 10. Estadio Julio Cesar Villagra (formerly named), Córdoba

Several smaller Córdoba provincial grounds round out Argentina's stadium geography. The clubs outside Buenos Aires and Córdoba — including those in Rosario (Estadio Marcelo Bielsa of Newell's Old Boys, Estadio Gigante de Arroyito of Rosario Central) — have significant grounds of their own. Rosario's grounds in particular are worth noting: the Gigante de Arroyito holds approximately 42,000 for Rosario Central, while Newell's Old Boys play in a compact 42,000-seat ground associated with Lionel Messi's boyhood club.

## Argentine stadium culture

Argentine grounds are unusual by European standards in their relationship to the neighbourhood. Most Buenos Aires clubs are embedded in specific barrios, and the ground is an extension of that local identity. The peñas (supporter clubs) that fund and organise the barra brava terrace sections operate in a complex ecosystem of club politics and neighbourhood loyalty that is unlike anything in European football. Going to a match in Argentina as a visitor requires research, reasonable Spanish, and ideally a local contact — but the reward is an atmosphere that is genuinely different from anything else in world football.

## The Copa Libertadores dimension

Argentina has produced more Copa Libertadores winners than any other country — Boca Juniors, River Plate, Independiente, Estudiantes, San Lorenzo, Racing Club, Vélez Sarsfield, and Estudiantes collectively hold the majority of CONMEBOL's flagship club trophies. The stadiums listed here have all hosted Libertadores group stage or knockout matches at some point, and several have hosted finals. La Bombonera and El Monumental in particular are considered among the most intimidating venues in the competition by visiting clubs. The altitude of Buenos Aires (a mere 25 metres above sea level) is not a factor, but the noise, the density of passionate support, and the tactical familiarity that Argentine clubs bring to their own grounds more than compensate.

## Getting to the grounds

Buenos Aires' football geography is navigable by public transport and taxi for most grounds. La Bombonera, El Nuevo Gasometro, and El Palacio are all in inner Buenos Aires and reachable by Subte (metro) or bus. The Estadio Jose Amalfitani in Liniers is served by the Sarmiento suburban rail line. El Monumental in Nunez is in the north of the city and is best reached by Subte D or river-district bus. Security arrangements around Argentine matches are extensive: police escorts for away supporters are common, and ticket allocation between home and away sections is managed carefully for high-risk fixtures.
