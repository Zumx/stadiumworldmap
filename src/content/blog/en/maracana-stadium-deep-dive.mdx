The Estádio Jornalista Mário Filho, universally known as the Maracanã, occupies a place in Brazilian sporting consciousness that no other ground in the country comes close to matching. Built for the 1950 World Cup in the Maracanã neighbourhood of Rio de Janeiro and named in tribute to the river that flows through the area, the stadium is the site of three of football's most-discussed matches and the only ground in the world to have hosted two FIFA World Cup finals. ## The 1950 World Cup and the Maracanazo

The Maracanã was constructed at extraordinary speed in 1948-1950 specifically for the 1950 FIFA World Cup, with Brazil expecting to use the tournament — and the new stadium — to confirm its arrival as a footballing power. The decisive match against Uruguay on 16 July 1950 drew an official attendance of 173,850, with unofficial estimates as high as 199,854 — the largest crowd in football history. Brazil needed only a draw to win the tournament; instead, Uruguay won 2-1.

The defeat, known in Brazilian football mythology as the Maracanazo, is regularly described as the most traumatic single event in the country's sporting history. The stadium itself has carried the weight of that match for 75 years. Goalkeeper Moacir Barbosa, who conceded both Uruguay goals, was treated by Brazilian fans for the rest of his life as the man responsible — a treatment historians have analysed as both unfair and revealing of the depth of the 1950 wound.

## The 2014 World Cup final

The Maracanã underwent a complete renovation between 2010 and 2013 ahead of the 2014 FIFA World Cup. Original capacity of 200,000-plus was reduced to a modern all-seater configuration of 78,838. The 2014 final — Germany 1-0 Argentina, after extra time, with Mario Götze's winner — restored the Maracanã to World Cup final history. The renovation preserved the original bowl shape while modernising every element of the seating, concourses, and roof structure.

## Flamengo and Fluminense — and the Fla-Flu

The Maracanã hosts Flamengo and Fluminense — Brazil's most-followed and one of its most historic clubs respectively. The Fla-Flu derby is one of South America's most-attended fixtures, regularly drawing crowds above 60,000. Flamengo's supporter base, the largest of any football club in the world by some measures, fills the Maracanã for Brasileirão matches and Copa Libertadores nights.

Flamengo's 1981 Copa Libertadores final at the Maracanã against Cobreloa is one of the most discussed club matches in South American football. The 2019 Libertadores final, played in Lima, was followed by Flamengo's return to Copa Libertadores glory — the trophy presented at the Maracanã to a city in collective celebration.

## The Pelé connection

Pelé scored his 1,000th career goal at the Maracanã on 19 November 1969, with Santos playing Vasco da Gama. The goal — a penalty in the 78th minute — was followed by a 90-second pause as Pelé took the ball, kissed it, and addressed the crowd. The moment is preserved in Brazilian football memory as one of the defining events of the Maracanã's history, alongside the 1950 disaster and the 2014 final.

## The stadium's name

The official name, Estádio Jornalista Mário Filho, honours the journalist who promoted the original construction project and who was instrumental in establishing the Maracanã as a national institution. The popular name, Maracanã, comes from the river and neighbourhood; the original Tupi-Guarani word means "rattle bird" or, in some interpretations, "snake-like" — referring to a bird native to the area or the winding course of the river.

## Architecture and engineering

The original Maracanã, designed by Brazilian architects Pedro Paulo Bernardes Bastos and Antônio Dias Carneiro, was a continuous oval bowl of reinforced concrete, the largest stadium structure in the world at the time of opening. The 2010-2013 renovation, led by Brazilian architects working with international consultants, preserved the original silhouette while completely replacing the seating, roof, and structural reinforcement. The new translucent membrane roof covers the entirety of the seating bowl, allowing natural light to reach the pitch while sheltering supporters from the tropical rain that defines Rio's climate.

## Concerts and beyond football

The Maracanã has hosted concerts by some of the largest international acts — the Rolling Stones, Frank Sinatra, Madonna, U2. Pope John Paul II celebrated Mass at the Maracanã during his 1980 Brazilian visit, with an attendance estimated at 150,000. The 2007 Pan American Games' opening and closing ceremonies were held at the stadium. The site has been both Brazil's sporting cathedral and one of its most-used national-event venues.

## Pilgrimage destination

The Maracanã is now one of Rio's principal tourist destinations alongside the Christ the Redeemer statue and Copacabana beach. Stadium tours run daily and include the dressing rooms, the pitch, and the journalist Mário Filho memorial. The ground sits at the centre of the Maracanã sports complex, which includes the Maracanãzinho (a smaller indoor arena), and is accessible from the Maracanã Metro Station.
