Canadian sport spreads across more codes than most countries — Canadian football (CFL), ice hockey (NHL, but arenas, not stadiums), baseball (MLB), Major League Soccer, and increasingly rugby. The stadium picture reflects that mix: a smaller number of large multi-use venues, several stadium-sized arenas for the CFL, and the gradual emergence of soccer-specific grounds following the European football model. ## 1. BC Place, Vancouver

Capacity: 54,500 (soccer), expandable to 63,000 (concerts). Originally opened in 1983 as the world's largest air-supported domed stadium, BC Place was reconstructed in 2011 with a retractable fabric roof supported by a cable structure — the largest single retractable cable-supported roof in the world. The Vancouver Whitecaps (MLS) and BC Lions (CFL) share the building. BC Place hosted the 2010 Winter Olympics opening and closing ceremonies and will host seven 2026 FIFA World Cup matches.

## 2. Rogers Centre, Toronto

Capacity: 49,282 (baseball); previously up to 53,506 for football events. Opened in 1989 as SkyDome — the first stadium with a fully retractable roof — Rogers Centre is the home of the Toronto Blue Jays (MLB). Its roof, comprising four panels that retract over each other, takes approximately 20 minutes to fully open or close. The stadium underwent a comprehensive renovation completed in 2023 that significantly improved the spectator experience. The CN Tower adjacent to the stadium provides the most recognisable visual context of any North American baseball ground.

## 3. Olympic Stadium (Stade Olympique), Montreal

Capacity: 56,040. The Big O — designed by French architect Roger Taillibert for the 1976 Summer Olympics — is one of the most architecturally ambitious stadiums ever built. The hyperboloid concrete frame and the inclined Tour de Montréal, the tallest inclined tower in the world at 165 metres, give the building a science-fictional appearance. The retractable roof has never functioned reliably, and the stadium has lacked a long-term sporting tenant since the Expos baseball franchise left in 2004. A major reconstruction announced in 2023 will replace the roof entirely by 2028.

## 4. Commonwealth Stadium, Edmonton

Capacity: 56,302. Edmonton's CFL home — the Edmonton Elks play here — is among the largest stadiums in Canada by capacity. Built for the 1978 Commonwealth Games and renovated for the 2001 World Athletics Championships and the 2015 FIFA Women's World Cup final, the open-air bowl has hosted some of the largest crowds in Canadian sport. The atmospheric difficulty of playing in Edmonton's cold and wind is part of the venue's CFL reputation.

## 5. McMahon Stadium, Calgary

Capacity: 35,400. Home of the Calgary Stampeders (CFL) since 1960 and the most historically resonant CFL ground. McMahon hosted the 1988 Winter Olympics opening and closing ceremonies and three Grey Cup finals. The Stampeders are one of the CFL's most successful franchises with eight Grey Cup wins. The stadium's relatively modest size by international standards reflects the Canadian football audience — focused, regional, deeply committed rather than mass-attended.

## 6. BMO Field, Toronto

Capacity: 30,991 (expandable to 40,000 with temporary seating). Home of Toronto FC (MLS) and the Toronto Argonauts (CFL) since 2007 (Toronto FC) and 2016 (Argonauts), built as Canada's first soccer-specific stadium. The bowl was expanded in 2016 and 2018, and is being further expanded to approximately 45,000 for 2026 World Cup matches. Toronto will host six 2026 World Cup matches at BMO. The lakeshore location with the downtown Toronto skyline behind the north stand is one of MLS's most recognisable visual settings.

## 7. TD Place Stadium, Ottawa

Capacity: 24,000 (expandable to 39,376). Home of the Ottawa Redblacks (CFL) and Atlético Ottawa (Canadian Premier League). The current stadium, opened in 2014 within the redeveloped Lansdowne Park district, replaced the historic Frank Clair Stadium. The bowl is unusual in being one of the few Canadian grounds with significant urban density immediately surrounding it — the Glebe neighbourhood touches the south stand directly.

## 8. Tim Hortons Field, Hamilton

Capacity: 23,218. Home of the Hamilton Tiger-Cats (CFL), opened in 2014 on the site of the historic Ivor Wynne Stadium. Designed by Cannon Design and BBB Architects with a deliberately compact, urban footprint that places the stands directly on the touchline — an unusual approach for a Canadian football stadium. The Tiger-Cats are the most historically successful CFL franchise from Eastern Canada.

## 9. Saputo Stadium, Montreal

Capacity: 19,619 (expandable to 25,500). Home of CF Montréal (MLS, formerly Montreal Impact) since 2008. The compact, soccer-specific design follows the MLS template developed at venues like Toyota Park in Chicago. The expansion to over 25,000 in 2012 used temporary seating in the configuration for important matches; the bowl can hold large crowds for Champions League and rivalry fixtures.

## 10. Mosaic Stadium, Regina

Capacity: 33,350. Home of the Saskatchewan Roughriders (CFL), opened in 2017 to replace the historic Taylor Field. The Roughriders are the CFL's most fanatically supported franchise — Saskatchewan's population of approximately 1.2 million produces the league's largest territorial fan base. Mosaic's compact, sleek modern design is a clear architectural step up from typical CFL infrastructure and has set the template for any future CFL stadium projects.

## The 2026 World Cup and Canadian stadiums

Canada will co-host the 2026 FIFA World Cup with the United States and Mexico, with thirteen matches played in Canadian venues. Two stadiums are confirmed: BC Place in Vancouver (seven matches including a round of 16) and BMO Field in Toronto (six matches including a group stage). Both venues are receiving infrastructure upgrades — the BMO Field expansion is significant, the BC Place pitch is being relaid with natural grass for tournament use. The Canadian World Cup investment is the smallest of the three co-hosts but represents a major moment for Canadian soccer.

## The CFL and Canadian football

Canadian football differs from American football in a series of rule details — three downs instead of four, a 110-yard field, twelve players per side rather than eleven. The Canadian Football League has nine teams and runs from June to November, with the Grey Cup final hosted by a different city each year. CFL crowds in Western Canada (Edmonton, Calgary, Saskatchewan, Winnipeg) average higher than in the East, with Saskatchewan particularly noted for its supporter intensity in a province with only one major-league sports franchise.

## Visiting Canadian stadiums

The Canadian stadium experience varies by region. Toronto and Montreal venues integrate with public transit (subway, métro), while Western Canadian stadiums typically require driving and parking. Winter games at Edmonton and Calgary in the late CFL season can be played in genuinely arctic conditions — temperatures below minus 20 Celsius are not unusual at the conference finals in November. Canadian stadium concessions are typically more diverse than American equivalents, reflecting the country's multicultural urban populations. All venues are visible on the [map](/map).
