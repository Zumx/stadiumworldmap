China's stadium portfolio has been shaped by two factors above all others: the 2008 Beijing Olympics, which gave the country a generation of world-class venues including the Bird's Nest; and the Chinese Super League's volatile 2010s, which produced both the world's most expensive transfer market for several years and ambitious stadium projects, some of which were left unfinished after the league's financial collapse in 2021-22. The result is one of the most uneven and interesting national stadium portfolios in world sport.

## 1. Beijing National Stadium (Bird's Nest)

Capacity: 80,000. The architectural icon of the 2008 Olympics, designed by Herzog and de Meuron with Ai Weiwei as artistic consultant. The lattice steel structure — 110,000 tonnes of structural steel — became one of the most photographed buildings of the 21st century before construction finished. The Bird's Nest also hosted the 2022 Winter Olympics opening and closing ceremonies, making it the first stadium to host both a Summer and Winter Olympics. Post-Olympic use has been modest; it serves as an event venue and tourist attraction.

## 2. Shanghai Stadium

Capacity: 56,000. The shell-shaped multipurpose stadium opened in 1997, used for Shanghai Shenhua Chinese Super League matches and major concerts. The roof is a translucent steel-cable structure that gives the bowl a distinctive interior light quality. The ground hosted the 2018 FIFA Club World Cup and remains one of the most-used venues in Chinese sport.

## 3. Universiade Sports Centre, Shenzhen

Capacity: 60,334. The flagship venue of the 2011 Summer Universiade, designed by gmp architects with a crystal-shaped exterior that combines structural and aesthetic functions. The stadium hosts Shenzhen FC matches and major football fixtures. The Universiade legacy has been more sustained than that of many other Chinese mega-event venues.

## 4. Wuhan Sports Center Stadium

Capacity: 60,000. The home of Wuhan Three Towns Chinese Super League. The stadium hosted matches during the 2007 FIFA Women's World Cup and continues to be one of the most-used venues in Chinese football's central region. Wuhan's pandemic-era closure and reopening became internationally symbolic; the stadium's matchday use has been progressively restored.

## 5. Tianjin Olympic Center Stadium

Capacity: 60,000. The "Water Drop" stadium, named for its distinctive drop-shaped exterior. Used during the 2008 Olympics for football matches and now the home of Tianjin Tigers in the Chinese Super League. The exterior facade glows blue at night, creating a visual effect unique to this stadium among Chinese sports venues.

## 6. Hangzhou Olympic Sports Center Stadium

Capacity: 80,000. The flagship venue of the 2022 Asian Games in Hangzhou (held in 2023 due to pandemic delays). The lotus-flower-shaped exterior referencing local cultural motifs gives the stadium its distinctive shape. The Asian Games legacy includes both football and athletics use, with the stadium continuing to host major events.

## 7. Workers' Stadium, Beijing

Capacity: 68,000 (post-renovation). The historic Beijing ground, originally opened in 1959 and comprehensively rebuilt between 2020 and 2023 with the original facade and aesthetic preserved. The home of Beijing Guoan in the Chinese Super League, the rebuilt Workers' Stadium combines historical continuity with modern infrastructure — a rare combination in the otherwise-renewal-focused Chinese stadium portfolio.

## 8. Guangdong Olympic Stadium, Guangzhou

Capacity: 80,012. Built for the 2001 National Games, the stadium is a multipurpose venue used for football (Guangzhou FC matches), athletics, and major concerts. The capacity makes it one of the largest stadiums in southern China.

## 9. Tianhe Stadium, Guangzhou

Capacity: 58,500. Guangzhou's traditional football home before the Guangzhou Evergrande era, where the city's senior football club has played for decades. The ground has hosted Chinese Super League title-decisive matches during Guangzhou Evergrande's eight-time championship run (2011-2019) and remains a fixture of southern Chinese football.

## 10. Suzhou Industrial Park Sports Center Stadium

Capacity: 45,000. A modern Suzhou venue, opened in 2018, representative of the second-tier city stadium build-out that paralleled the Chinese Super League's spending peak. The technical infrastructure is comparable to top-tier European grounds, though post-2021 league use has been reduced as Chinese football's financial structure has reset.

## The Chinese Super League legacy

China's stadium portfolio includes both the global landmark of the Bird's Nest and the half-finished or under-used projects from the CSL boom. The Guangzhou Evergrande Stadium — once intended as a 100,000-capacity lotus-shaped colossus that would have been the world's largest football stadium — was suspended unfinished after the parent company's financial collapse. The stadium portfolio in China in 2025 reflects both the country's capacity to build at unprecedented scale and the volatility of football economics that have shaped the past decade.
