Every Summer Olympics produces a main stadium that is expected to be its defining image. What happens to those stadiums after the closing ceremony is the more interesting story — some become the beating heart of city sport for decades, others decay within years, and a few stand as expensive monuments to the gap between promise and planning.

## Berlin 1936: the longest-serving Olympic stadium

The Olympiastadion Berlin, built for the 1936 Games under the Nazi regime and used by Adolf Hitler as a propaganda backdrop for the Jesse Owens-dominated athletics programme, is the oldest Olympic main stadium still in regular use. Renovated between 2000 and 2004, it now holds approximately 74,500 and hosts Hertha BSC's Bundesliga home matches as well as the German Cup final. The renovation preserved the original limestone facade while rebuilding the roof structure and interior. Its continued operation is as much a statement about confronting history as it is about stadium practicality.

## Helsinki 1952

The Helsinki Olympic Stadium was actually built in 1938, delayed by war, and hosted the 1952 Games. It remains in use as Finland's national stadium and underwent a comprehensive renovation completed in 2020 that brought capacity to around 36,000. It hosts the Finnish national football team and major concerts. A rare example of a mid-century Olympic stadium successfully modernised without demolition.

## Rome 1960: Stadio Olimpico

The Stadio Olimpico was already fourteen years old when Rome hosted the 1960 Olympics, having been built during the Mussolini era and expanded for the Games. It went on to host the 1968 European Championship final, the 1977 and 1984 European Cup finals, the 1990 World Cup final, and every AS Roma and SS Lazio home match for decades. At 70,634 capacity, it remains one of Europe's largest active football venues. Rome's longstanding failure to build a new dedicated stadium for its two clubs means the Olimpico continues in use well past the point where most comparable grounds would have been replaced.

## Tokyo 1964 and 2020

Japan's first postwar Olympics produced the Yoyogi National Gymnasium — Kenzo Tange's suspended-cable masterpiece — as a secondary venue, and the main stadium was later demolished and replaced for the 2020 Games. The new Japan National Stadium, designed by Kengo Kuma and opened in 2019 at a cost of approximately 157 billion yen, replaced Tange's original main stadium. It holds 68,000 and hosted the athletics, football final, and opening/closing ceremonies for the delayed 2020 Tokyo Olympics.

## Munich 1972: Olympiastadion

Frei Otto and Gunter Behnisch's iconic tent-roof structure at Munich's Olympiastadion was the design landmark of the 1970s Olympic movement. FC Bayern used it from 1972 until 2005, when they moved to the newly opened Allianz Arena. The Olympiastadion is now used primarily for concerts, the German Athletics Championships, and occasional football. Its listed status protects the tent-roof structure but limits renovation options. The legacy is architectural first and sporting second.

## Montreal 1976: the white elephant

Montreal's Olympic Stadium — the Big O — is the defining cautionary tale of Olympic construction. The retractable roof did not work properly for years after construction, the asymmetric tower was added after the Games, and the final construction bill was not paid off until 2006, thirty years after the Olympics. Seating up to 56,040, it now hosts events and concerts but has no regular sports tenant. It is the example cited in every discussion of Olympic facility legacy planning.

## Moscow 1980: Luzhniki

Luzhniki in Moscow was already the Soviet Union's primary national stadium when it hosted the 1980 Olympics. At a post-renovation capacity of 81,000, it is Russia's largest stadium. Luzhniki hosted the 2018 World Cup final, the 2018 Champions League final, and the opening and closing ceremonies of the boycott-affected 1980 Games. Unlike most Soviet-era sports infrastructure, it has been extensively rebuilt and remains genuinely operational.

## Los Angeles 1984: Memorial Coliseum

The Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum has hosted two Olympic Games — 1932 and 1984 — making it the only venue to hold that distinction. It is the home stadium of USC Trojans college football and was renovated in 2019. Los Angeles will use it again for the 2028 Olympics, giving it a third Summer Games, an entirely unique record.

## Seoul 1988

The Seoul Olympic Stadium held approximately 69,950 and served its Olympic purpose but has had limited use as a football venue since. South Korean football moved to newer, purpose-built grounds in subsequent decades. The main stadium is now used for concerts and occasional athletics.

## Barcelona 1992: Estadi Olimpic Lluis Companys

Barcelona's Olympic stadium — built in 1927, renovated twice, and used for the 1992 Olympics — has a complicated recent history. Named after Lluis Companys, the Catalan president executed in 1940, it sat relatively underused for years before becoming FC Barcelona's temporary home during the Camp Nou renovation that began in 2023. Capacity around 55,000. Its revival as a top-level football venue has extended its useful life unexpectedly.

## Sydney 2000: Stadium Australia / Accor Stadium

The Sydney Olympic Stadium — now known as Accor Stadium after multiple naming-rights changes — has been one of the more successfully converted Olympic venues. At 83,500 for football (reduced from an Olympic capacity above 100,000 by filling end-zone seating), it hosts NRL, football, and rugby union. Australia's willingness to use the venue actively for multiple major sports has kept it relevant.

## Athens 2004: decay and neglect

Athens' Olympic venues are the most cited example of post-Games neglect. The main Olympic Stadium (OAKA), designed by Santiago Calatrava with its distinctive roof arches, fell into disrepair following the 2004 Games. Several satellite venues were abandoned entirely. The full cost of the Athens Olympics, combined with post-Games maintenance failures, became a major political issue during the Greek debt crisis.

## Beijing 2008: the Bird's Nest

Herzog and de Meuron's National Stadium — the Bird's Nest — is one of the most architecturally admired sports buildings of the twenty-first century. Post-Games use has been limited: a handful of football matches and concerts annually. China hosted the 2022 Winter Olympics in part to repurpose some of the same infrastructure, and the Bird's Nest was used again for the opening ceremony.

## London 2012: West Ham's inheritance

The London Olympic Stadium was converted to a football ground and became West Ham United's home from 2016, renamed the London Stadium. The conversion is the most controversial in recent Olympic history — the running track remains, pushing supporters further from the pitch than at a purpose-built football ground, and the atmosphere suffers for it. Capacity is approximately 60,000.

## Rio 2016: Maracana reused

Rio's Olympics used the Maracana — already one of football's iconic grounds — as the main stadium, which was a practical rather than a legacy-building decision. The Maracana had been renovated for the 2014 World Cup and was operational. Post-Games, it returned to Flamengo and Fluminense fixtures.

## Paris 2024: Stade de France

The 2024 Paris Olympics used the Stade de France as its primary athletics and opening ceremony venue. At 81,338 capacity, built in 1998, it was a mature, operational venue that required no post-Games legacy planning — it will continue as France's national football and rugby stadium.
