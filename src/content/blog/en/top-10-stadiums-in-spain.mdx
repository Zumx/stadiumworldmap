Spanish football infrastructure is undergoing its most significant transformation in decades. Real Madrid's Bernabeu completed a comprehensive renovation in 2023. FC Barcelona's Camp Nou is mid-renovation with completion expected around 2026. Atletico Madrid moved into the Wanda Metropolitano in 2017. The result is a La Liga with several of the world's most ambitious football stadiums alongside historically rich grounds that define their clubs. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Santiago Bernabeu, Madrid

Capacity: 81,044 after the renovation completed in 2023. Real Madrid's home since 1947, the Bernabeu's renovation — designed by GMP Architekten and L35 Arquitectos with a cost of approximately 1.076 billion euros — added a retractable roof, a retractable pitch, and a 360-degree video facade to the exterior. The retractable pitch works similarly to Tottenham Hotspur Stadium's system, allowing the playing surface to roll outside for maintenance or replacement. The new exterior — a series of steel rods forming a shimmering curtain around the bowl — is visible from the Paseo de la Castellana and has become one of Madrid's distinctive architectural additions. Real Madrid are the Champions League's most successful club with 15 titles.

## 2. Spotify Camp Nou, Barcelona

Capacity: approximately 99,354 before the renovation that began in 2023 (target post-renovation capacity approximately 105,000). FC Barcelona's home since 1957, the Camp Nou is the largest stadium in Europe and one of the largest in the world. The renovation — designed by Populous and Nikken Sekkei — will add a new roof covering the previously open top tier, significantly improve concourse and facilities, and may raise Barcelona to the largest stadium capacity in Europe when complete. During the renovation, Barcelona have played home Champions League matches at the Estadi Olimpic Lluis Companys. The Camp Nou's scale — a bowl of four tiers visible from much of the city — is unmatched in European football.

## 3. Civitas Metropolitano (Wanda Metropolitano), Madrid

Capacity: 68,456. Atletico Madrid's home since 2017, built on the site of the old Estadio de la Comunidad de Madrid in Barajas, east of the city centre. Atletico played at the Calderon for decades before the move. The Metropolitano hosted the 2019 Champions League final (Liverpool 2 Tottenham 0) and operates one of the better food and commercial matchday experiences in Spanish football. Atletico's passionate supporter culture — Diego Simeone's tactical identity has sustained European competition for over a decade — fills the bowl consistently.

## 4. Mestalla, Valencia

Capacity: 55,000. Valencia CF's home since 1923, Mestalla is one of the oldest top-division grounds in Spain still in active use. The steep upper tier and enclosed bowl create excellent acoustics — Mestalla is consistently rated one of La Liga's most atmospheric grounds despite Valencia's difficult financial situation in recent years. A new stadium (the Nou Mestalla) has been under incomplete construction since 2007, stalled by financial difficulties. Until the new ground is completed, the old Mestalla continues.

## 5. San Mames, Bilbao

Capacity: 53,289. Athletic Club Bilbao's home, known as La Catedral (the Cathedral), opened in 2013 to replace the old San Mames that had stood on the same site since 1913. The new ground, designed by IDOM, is fully enclosed with a distinctive exterior of folded metal panels in Athletic's red and white colours. Athletic Club's unique policy of fielding only players born or trained in the Basque Country gives the club and its stadium a specifically regional identity unlike any other top-division ground in Europe. San Mames hosted Euro 2020 group stage matches.

## 6. Estadio Ramon Sanchez Pizjuan, Seville

Capacity: 43,883. Sevilla FC's home since 1958, Pizjuan hosted the 1986 World Cup (including the West Germany versus France semifinal, the first World Cup match decided on penalties), the 1982 European Cup final, and multiple Europa League finals. Sevilla are the Europa League's most successful club with seven titles. The compact, enclosed bowl makes it one of Andalusia's most atmospherically intense grounds.

## 7. Estadio Benito Villamarin, Seville

Capacity: 60,720. Real Betis' home, expanded to its current capacity in recent years as Betis' support has grown with sustained La Liga presence and Europa League football. Betis won their first Copa del Rey since 2005 in 2022 and the Villamarin was the location of significant celebrations. The Sevilla derby — Sevilla FC versus Real Betis — is one of La Liga's most intense local rivalries, and the Villamarin on a derby night is among Spanish football's more charged atmospheres.

## 8. Estadi Olimpic Lluis Companys, Barcelona

Capacity: approximately 55,000 (official; configured to approximately 50,000 for football). Barcelona's temporary home during the Camp Nou renovation, the Estadi Olimpic was built in 1927 and renovated for the 1992 Barcelona Olympics. It hosted the 1992 Olympic opening ceremony and athletics events. Named after Lluis Companys, the Catalan president executed by Francisco Franco's regime in 1940, it is one of the more politically charged naming choices in European football geography.

## 9. Reale Arena (Estadio Anoeta), San Sebastian

Capacity: 39,500. Real Sociedad's home in San Sebastian (Donostia), rebuilt and reopened in 2019 as the Reale Arena after the original 1993 Estadio Anoeta was comprehensively renovated. The enclosed bowl, tight to the pitch, represents a significant improvement on the old open structure. Real Sociedad have been consistent European competitors since the early 2020s under Imanol Alguacil, and the Reale Arena has hosted Europa League and Champions League group stage matches.

## 10. El Sadar, Pamplona

Capacity: 23,576. CA Osasuna's compact home in Pamplona, Navarra. The Estadio El Sadar is small by La Liga standards but creates an atmosphere beyond its size thanks to the club's loyal supporter base. Osasuna won the Copa del Rey runner-up position in 2023 and have established themselves as a consistent La Liga presence in recent seasons despite operating on one of the division's smaller budgets.

## The renovation era in Spanish football

Real Madrid's Bernabeu renovation and Barcelona's Camp Nou expansion represent the two most expensive stadium projects currently active in European club football. Together they will define the architectural face of the two most globally recognised football clubs for the next generation. The contrast — Bernabeu completed, Camp Nou in progress — means La Liga's two dominant clubs are at different stages of the same transformation for the 2024-25 and 2025-26 seasons.

## La Liga's stadium diversity

The ground list above illustrates La Liga's unusual range: from the 81,000-seat Bernabeu to Osasuna's 23,576-capacity El Sadar, from the ultra-modern Civitas Metropolitano to the century-old Mestalla. Spanish football does not have the uniform stadium modernisation that German or English football experienced after 2000 — the Primera Division contains very new and very old grounds in active use simultaneously. Athletic Club's San Mames completed its new ground in 2013 while Valencia's Mestalla renovation has been stalled for over fifteen years. Real Sociedad's Reale Arena is the most recently completed new ground at the mid-tier club level and is widely considered a successful model.

## The Copa del Rey and neutral-venue finals

Spain's domestic cup, the Copa del Rey, traditionally holds its final at a neutral venue — Estadio de la Cartuja in Seville has been the most consistent recent host. The Copa's knockout format (including first rounds between lower-division clubs and La Liga sides) produces occasional upset results that are celebrated more genuinely at the smaller grounds involved. Osasuna's 2023 Copa del Rey final run, which took a Pamplona club to the national final, was one of the competition's more emotionally resonant recent stories.

## Getting to Spanish stadiums

The Bernabeu, Civitas Metropolitano, and Wanda are all on Madrid's Metro network. The Reale Arena in San Sebastian requires a train from Bilbao or a bus from San Sebastian Amara station — it sits outside the city centre. San Mames is an eight-minute walk from Bilbao Abando station. All grounds are on the [map](/map) with their transport contexts visible.
