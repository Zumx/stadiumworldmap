A tifo is, at its simplest, a coordinated display produced by organised supporters in a stadium's standing or terrace section. At its most complex, it is a multi-thousand-piece visual production involving months of preparation, significant cost, and the precise coordination of every person in a section holding their specific element at the exact right moment. The ultras culture that produces these displays is one of the most debated aspects of modern football — celebrated for generating the atmospheres that broadcast football worldwide, criticised for the violence and pyrotechnics that sometimes accompany it.

## What ultras groups are

The ultras movement emerged in Italian football in the late 1960s, with Fossa dei Leoni (AC Milan) and Fedelissimi Granata (Torino) among the earliest groups. The model spread to other Latin European countries and then globally — today ultras groups exist on every continent, though the phenomenon retains its strongest expression in Italy, Germany, Turkey, the Balkans, and South America.

An ultras group is a formally or semi-formally organised supporters' association that takes responsibility for the atmosphere in a specific section of a ground, typically a curved end or a standing terrace. Groups have names, leadership structures, membership dues, and defined territories within the stadium. Membership provides access to the section, to the club of the tifo preparations, and to the social networks of organised support. The largest groups run dozens or hundreds of members in active roles on matchday and many more in a broader membership that funds their activities.

## Borussia Dortmund's Yellow Wall

The Sudtribune at Signal Iduna Park in Dortmund is the largest terrace in world football, holding 25,000 standing supporters. On matchday it becomes the Yellow Wall — a sea of yellow flags, scarves, and kits that has become one of football's most reproduced images. The noise produced by 25,000 standing supporters in an enclosed end amplifies to a level that visiting players regularly identify as the most intimidating environment in European club football.

Dortmund's terrace culture is organised by a network of fan clubs and ultras groups who coordinate flag distribution, choreography, and chanting. The Sudtribune has no single controlling ultras group in the Italian mould; instead, it operates on a broader organised-fan model specific to German football, where the club itself plays a more active role in supporter liaison. Signal Iduna Park's total capacity is 81,365 for domestic Bundesliga matches, dropping to approximately 66,000 for UEFA competitions where standing areas must be converted to seating.

## Milan's Curva Sud and Inter's Curva Nord

The San Siro hosts two of Italy's most historically significant ultras groups in close proximity. AC Milan's Curva Sud is home to groups including the Fossa dei Leoni (now dormant) and their successors, while Inter Milan's Curva Nord hosts several active groups. The two sections are at opposite ends of the ground and have produced some of the most elaborate tifo choreography in European football — full-stadium card mosaic displays, enormous banners spanning the entire terrace, choreographed flag waves timed to music.

Italian ultras culture carries the full range of the movement's contradictions. The organisations that produce the displays also have documented involvement in ticket touting, extortion of club management for free tickets, and, historically, political violence. Several major Italian clubs have had complicated formal relationships with their ultras groups, alternating between accommodation and confrontation. The Curva Sud at Milan is listed as one of European football's most influential supporter sections in terms of both atmosphere and the export of the tifo model to other countries.

## Ultras Tito Cucchiaroni and Genoa

Genoa CFC's supporter culture, anchored by the Gradinata Nord at the Stadio Luigi Ferraris, is one of Italy's oldest organised ultras traditions. Ultras Tito Cucchiaroni — named after an Argentine player who died in a car crash — is among the most historically cited Genoese fan organisations. Genoa's rivalry with Sampdoria, both clubs sharing Ferraris, produces derbies whose atmosphere is considered among the most intense in Serie A.

## Pyrotechnics: the regulatory conflict

Flares, smoke bombs, and pyrotechnics have been a feature of ultras culture since its origins. The visual effect — columns of coloured smoke rising from a packed terrace — is photogenic and atmospheric. The safety concerns are not abstract: stadiums have seen fires, supporters have been burned, and smoke has restricted visibility for stewards and players.

UEFA's regulations prohibit pyrotechnics explicitly, and clubs whose supporters use them are subject to fines and stadium bans. German clubs have faced the most publicised regulatory conflicts over this, with German ultras groups staging protests — including full-stadium walkouts in the first twelve minutes of matches in 2023 — against what they characterised as disproportionate regulation of fan culture. The DFL (Deutsche Fussball Liga) and DFB have not yet found a durable compromise.

## Safe-standing in the Premier League

The Premier League operated an all-seater policy at top-division grounds since the Taylor Report of 1990, a direct response to the Hillsborough disaster in which 97 Liverpool supporters died in a crush at Sheffield Wednesday's Hillsborough ground. For three decades, standing at Premier League grounds was formally prohibited, though rail seating and the practice of standing in seated areas created an informal grey zone.

From the 2022-23 season, the Premier League permitted clubs to install licensed safe-standing sections — rail seats that fold down for seating events but can be locked upright for standing. Multiple clubs, including Chelsea, Manchester City, Liverpool, Arsenal, and Tottenham, have installed sections. The capacity, configuration, and success of these sections vary, but the direction of travel is toward more standing provision in top English football for the first time in over thirty years.

## The global export of ultras style

Brazilian, Argentine, and Chilean supporter groups developed their own organised culture independently, with the barra brava tradition in South America predating much of the European ultras movement. Today, the aesthetic exchange flows in both directions — South American flags and hand-held flares appear in German grounds; European-style tifos appear in Brazilian derbies. ## Tifo production: logistics and cost

A full-stadium tifo display for a major European club match involves months of preparation. The design phase typically produces a concept that can be divided into sections no larger than one person can hold, lift, or wave. Printed or hand-painted sections are distributed to supporters in the relevant stand or terrace before the match — often in tightly rolled tubes placed on seats — with coordinated instructions on when to raise, wave, or reveal them. For the largest displays, sections of the design can span several hundred metres of terrace and require precise synchronisation between multiple groups.

The cost is substantial: a major card mosaic or banner display at Borussia Dortmund, Galatasaray, or Atletico Madrid can run into tens of thousands of euros, funded primarily through merchandise sales, membership fees, and direct donations from the ultras group's member base. Some clubs formally subsidise supporter group display budgets; others maintain a formal distance from the ultras while tolerating or even celebrating the results.

The tension between club commercial interests and ultras group autonomy is a persistent feature of European football politics. Clubs value the atmosphere, the image rights, and the broadcast-ready visuals that tifos produce. Ultras groups value their independence from club management and their right to express political or social views within the stadium. When those interests diverge — as they have at Bayern Munich, Juventus, and at several Turkish clubs — the results are protests, walkouts, and occasionally the suspension of tifo production entirely.
