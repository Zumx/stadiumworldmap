Ukraine's stadium infrastructure was transformed by Euro 2012, jointly hosted with Poland. Four host cities — Kyiv, Lviv, Donetsk and Kharkiv — received new or comprehensively renovated grounds. The Russian invasion since 2022 has interrupted normal football operations: matches in Ukraine are played behind closed doors or moved to safer cities, and several grounds have been damaged or destroyed. The list below covers the major Ukrainian stadiums with attention to their pre-war and current status. Every site is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Olimpiyskiy National Sports Complex, Kyiv

Capacity: 70,050. Ukraine's national stadium, originally opened in 1923 and reconstructed for Euro 2012 at a cost of approximately $586 million. The Euro 2012 reconstruction wrapped the existing bowl in a new fabric-coated tensile roof structure that floats over the stands without external supports — a striking architectural treatment. The stadium hosted the Euro 2012 final (Spain 4 Italy 0), the 2018 UEFA Champions League final (Real Madrid 3 Liverpool 1), and Ukraine national team matches. Since 2022, matches at Olimpiyskiy have been suspended.

## 2. Donbass Arena, Donetsk

Capacity: 52,187. Shakhtar Donetsk's home, opened in 2009 at a cost of approximately $400 million and designed by ArupSport. The first stadium in the former Soviet Union built to UEFA five-star standards from new. The building was struck by shelling in 2014 during the early phases of the Donbass conflict and has been unusable since. Shakhtar Donetsk relocated their home matches first to Lviv, then to Kharkiv, then to Kyiv (Olimpiyskiy until 2022, then various Polish grounds since then). The Donbass Arena remains structurally damaged and outside Ukrainian government control as of writing.

## 3. Arena Lviv

Capacity: 34,915. Lviv's Euro 2012 venue, opened in 2011 and designed by Albert Wimmer. The stadium hosted Euro 2012 group matches and has been used as a temporary home by Shakhtar Donetsk and other displaced clubs. Lviv's relative distance from the front line has made it a preferred venue for Ukrainian football activity during the war, although matches still take place behind closed doors due to security restrictions.

## 4. Metalist Oblast Sports Complex, Kharkiv

Capacity: 38,633. Metalist Kharkiv's historic home, reconstructed for Euro 2012. The 1925-founded ground was rebuilt with a new tensile roof structure spanning all four stands. Kharkiv hosted Euro 2012 group matches including the most-watched match in the tournament's group stage. Metalist Kharkiv folded in 2016, though a successor club using the same name operates in lower divisions. The proximity of Kharkiv to the front lines since 2022 has restricted football operations at Metalist.

## 5. Chornomorets Stadium, Odesa

Capacity: 34,164. Chornomorets Odesa's home on the Black Sea coast, comprehensively rebuilt and reopened in 2011 (independent of Euro 2012 infrastructure). The site overlooks the Black Sea and has historic resonance for Odesa football culture. Chornomorets play in Ukrainian first division football. Odesa's relative distance from the front lines (compared to Kharkiv and Donbass) has allowed some football activity to continue, though under significant restrictions.

## 6. Dnipro-Arena, Dnipro

Capacity: 31,003. SC Dnipro-1's home (and previously FC Dnipro), opened in 2008. The stadium represents the pre-Euro 2012 generation of Ukrainian stadium development. FC Dnipro reached the 2015 UEFA Europa League final (lost to Sevilla 3-2 in Warsaw) — one of the strongest performances by a Ukrainian club in European competition. SC Dnipro-1 are the current senior tenant.

## 7. Lobanovskyi Dynamo Stadium, Kyiv

Capacity: 16,873. Dynamo Kyiv's historic compact home in Kyiv city centre, renamed for the legendary manager Valeriy Lobanovskyi who led Dynamo to multiple Soviet and European titles. The original ground dates from 1934. With its small modern capacity, Dynamo also uses Olimpiyskiy for Champions League and high-demand domestic matches. The Lobanovskyi ground is among the closest top-flight venues in any European country to the urban centre.

## 8. Slavutych-Arena, Zaporizhzhia

Capacity: 11,981 (pre-war). The home of Metalurh Zaporizhzhia (now defunct) and later Zorya Luhansk. The 2008-opened stadium has been used as a relocation host for displaced clubs from the Donbass region. Zaporizhzhia's position close to the front lines since 2022 has made football operations difficult.

## 9. Vorskla Stadium, Poltava

Capacity: 24,795. Vorskla Poltava's home in central Ukraine, opened in 1951 and partially renovated multiple times. Vorskla play in Ukrainian Premier League football and the ground has been used as a relocation host for displaced clubs.

## 10. Avanhard Stadium, Lutsk

Capacity: 12,080. Volyn Lutsk's home in western Ukraine. The location in western Ukraine, far from the front lines, has made Lutsk one of the venues where competitive Ukrainian Premier League matches have continued during the war, although strict security restrictions apply.

## Euro 2012 and its legacy

Ukraine's joint hosting of Euro 2012 with Poland transformed the country's football infrastructure. The total investment in stadiums and supporting infrastructure exceeded $14 billion. The four Ukrainian host cities — Kyiv, Lviv, Donetsk and Kharkiv — each gained a tournament-standard venue, although the Donbass Arena's loss to the 2014 conflict was a major setback. The Euro 2012 model — improving infrastructure across multiple regional cities — succeeded in raising the baseline standard of Ukrainian football venues, but the political and military events since 2014 have constrained what that infrastructure can deliver.

## Football in Ukraine during the war

Since 2022, the Ukrainian Premier League has continued operations under modified arrangements: matches are played behind closed doors, often with air-raid shelter access procedures in place. Clubs displaced from occupied or front-line regions have relocated to safer venues. The Ukrainian football authorities have maintained competitive league operations as a deliberate statement of national continuity, though European matches involving Ukrainian clubs are now played outside Ukraine. The Ukrainian national team plays its home matches abroad.

## Visiting Ukrainian grounds

Travel to Ukraine for football tourism is not advisable while the conflict continues. The grounds described above are mapped at [/map](/map) for reference and historical understanding. Several have been damaged or destroyed during the war, including the Donbass Arena and other venues in occupied or contested regions. The full picture of Ukrainian stadium infrastructure after the war ends will not be known until peacetime conditions allow comprehensive assessment.
