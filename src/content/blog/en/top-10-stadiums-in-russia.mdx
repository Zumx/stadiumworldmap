Russian football's stadium landscape was reshaped by the 2018 FIFA World Cup, when twelve venues across eleven cities were built or renovated for the tournament. The infrastructure programme delivered some of the most architecturally ambitious football stadiums of the past decade, although the post-tournament legacy use has been uneven. The list below covers the major World Cup grounds and the historic Russian football venues that continue to define the domestic game. Every site is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Luzhniki Stadium, Moscow

Capacity: 81,000. Russia's national stadium, originally opened in 1956 as the Central Lenin Stadium and the country's largest sporting venue. Luzhniki was the centrepiece of the 1980 Summer Olympics and was reconstructed for the 2018 World Cup, when it hosted the opening match (Russia 5 Saudi Arabia 0) and the final (France 4 Croatia 2). The original outer facade and roofline were preserved during the renovation, but the interior bowl was rebuilt completely. The stadium hosts Russia national team matches and major cup finals.

## 2. Gazprom Arena, Saint Petersburg

Capacity: 67,800. Zenit Saint Petersburg's home, opened in 2017 after a notoriously prolonged construction process that took more than a decade and cost over a billion dollars. Designed by Japanese architect Kisho Kurokawa with a UFO-shaped retractable roof, the stadium sits on Krestovsky Island. The retractable pitch can be moved out of the building, and the roof closes fully — features that make it one of the most technically advanced stadiums in Europe. It hosted seven 2018 World Cup matches and the 2021 UEFA Champions League final.

## 3. Kazan Arena, Kazan

Capacity: 45,379. Rubin Kazan's home, opened in 2013 for the Summer Universiade and used as a 2018 World Cup venue. Designed by Populous with an exterior featuring the largest outdoor video screen in Europe (3,700 square metres) wrapped around the curved facade. The screen functions as a building-scale display visible from the surrounding Kazan landscape. The stadium hosts Tatarstan's primary football club and has been used as a temporary swimming venue at the 2015 World Aquatics Championships.

## 4. Otkritie Arena (Spartak Stadium), Moscow

Capacity: 45,360. Spartak Moscow's home, opened in 2014 after the club had played at borrowed grounds for decades. The first dedicated Spartak stadium in club history. The exterior facade carries an oversized diamond-pattern logo referencing Spartak's emblem, a design treatment unusual for a modern football stadium. The compact rectangular bowl with steep tribunes generates strong matchday acoustics. Spartak are Russia's most-supported club, with a national fanbase that fills the ground regularly.

## 5. VTB Arena (Dinamo Stadium), Moscow

Capacity: 26,319 (football configuration). Dinamo Moscow's home and one of the most architecturally distinctive stadium-arena hybrids in European football. The historic Dinamo Stadium was demolished and rebuilt as part of a complex containing both an outdoor football stadium and an indoor arena (for ice hockey and concerts) within the same structure. The football pitch and ice rink share the building but are completely separate venues. The 2019 reopening completed one of Moscow's longest-running stadium reconstruction projects.

## 6. Rostov Arena, Rostov-on-Don

Capacity: 45,000. FC Rostov's home, opened in 2018 as one of the World Cup tournament venues. The stadium occupies a previously underdeveloped site on the south bank of the Don River, with the design (Populous) referencing the curves of the river in the facade. Post-tournament, the stadium has been used regularly for Rostov's Premier League home matches but operates well below capacity for most fixtures.

## 7. Mordovia Arena, Saransk

Capacity: 41,685 (reduced post-tournament to approximately 28,000). The Mordovia Arena in the small city of Saransk is often cited as the World Cup 2018 venue with the most difficult legacy use case. The host city has no top-division football club and a population of approximately 315,000, making the original capacity unsustainable. The stadium has been partially decommissioned, with some sections removed to convert it to a more practical size for FC Mordovia and FC Tambov matches.

## 8. Volgograd Arena, Volgograd

Capacity: 45,568. Built for the 2018 World Cup on the site of the demolished Central Stadium near the Mamayev Kurgan memorial complex (commemorating the Battle of Stalingrad). The location near one of Russia's most historically resonant Second World War sites was a deliberate symbolic choice. Rotor Volgograd, the local club currently in Russia's second division, uses the stadium. The post-tournament use has been complicated by the club's lack of Premier League status.

## 9. Nizhny Novgorod Stadium

Capacity: 43,319. Built for the 2018 World Cup at the confluence of the Oka and Volga rivers, with the design (Populous) referencing the wind-carved patterns of the surrounding landscape. The exterior facade comprises white and blue ETFE panels arranged in vertical bands. FC Pari Nizhny Novgorod plays its home matches here. The stadium has been one of the more successful 2018 World Cup venues in terms of post-tournament programming, due partly to the city's larger population and existing football culture.

## 10. Samara Arena (Cosmos Arena)

Capacity: 44,807. Built for the 2018 World Cup on the eastern outskirts of Samara. The domed roof — a steel lattice structure — references Samara's role as a centre of Soviet aerospace engineering. The stadium hosts Krylia Sovetov Samara, a Premier League club, providing one of the more sustained post-tournament use cases among the new 2018 venues.

## The 2018 World Cup infrastructure legacy

The 2018 World Cup left Russia with twelve modernised or new stadiums across eleven cities. The largest venues — Luzhniki, Gazprom Arena, Kazan Arena, Otkritie — sit in major football cities and continue to host top-division clubs with regular attendance. The smaller-market venues — Saransk, Volgograd, Kaliningrad — have struggled with post-tournament use, and several have been partially decommissioned or are operating well below capacity. The total infrastructure investment exceeded $11 billion, making it one of the most expensive World Cup hosting programmes in history.

## The Russian Premier League

Russian Premier League football is currently disconnected from European club competition due to the post-2022 sporting sanctions. UEFA suspended Russian clubs from the Champions League and Europa League in February 2022, and the FIFA suspension applies to the national team. The domestic league continues — Zenit Saint Petersburg, CSKA Moscow, Spartak Moscow, Dinamo Moscow and Lokomotiv Moscow remain the historical heavyweights — but the absence of European matches has reduced the demand for the largest stadium capacities.

## Visiting Russian grounds

Practical travel to Russian football grounds has become significantly more complex since 2022. Direct flight access from Western Europe and North America is largely unavailable, and visa policies have tightened. For visitors with the means to travel, the post-World Cup stadium infrastructure remains in operation and matches are held to full Premier League schedule.
