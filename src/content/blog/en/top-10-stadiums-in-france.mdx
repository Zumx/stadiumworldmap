France's football infrastructure was transformed by the 1998 World Cup, which brought a new national stadium and prompted significant investment in regional grounds. The 2016 European Championship and 2024 Olympics further accelerated upgrades. The result is a country with several genuinely world-class venues and a strong second tier of modernised regional grounds. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Stade de France, Saint-Denis

Capacity: 81,338. France's national stadium, opened January 1998 in time for the World Cup, was built in the Saint-Denis suburb north of Paris. It hosted the 1998 World Cup final — France 3 Brazil 0, Zinedine Zidane's two headers — and has since hosted Champions League finals (2006, 2022), Rugby World Cup matches, Six Nations fixtures for the France rugby union team, and the 2024 Olympic opening ceremony. The bowl is large and well-configured, though the running track — required for its original Olympic athletics brief — creates distance between the front rows and the playing surface. The RER B and D rail connections make it one of Europe's best-served stadiums by public transport.

## 2. Parc des Princes, Paris

Capacity: 47,929. Paris Saint-Germain's home in the 16th arrondissement, Parc des Princes was built in 1972 to replace an earlier ground on the same site. The Roger Taillibert-designed bowl is low and enclosed, producing a concentration of noise that larger stadiums cannot match. PSG's transformation from a midsized French club to a Champions League major power under Qatari ownership since 2011 has given the ground regular European nights against the continent's best opposition. The Boulogne and Auteuil ends are the active supporter sections.

## 3. Orange Velodrome, Marseille

Capacity: 67,394 after renovation for Euro 2016. Olympique de Marseille's home, opened in 1937 and substantially rebuilt multiple times, the Velodrome is the most atmospheric club ground in France. Marseille's supporter culture is intense and the ground produces a noise level that visiting teams and officials consistently describe as one of Europe's most challenging environments. OM's 1993 Champions League win — the only French club victory in the tournament — is commemorated extensively around the ground. The 2016 renovation added a new roof that closes the bowl and dramatically improves acoustics.

## 4. Groupama Stadium, Lyon

Capacity: 59,186. Olympique Lyonnais' purpose-built home opened in 2016 in Decines-Charpieu, east of Lyon. Designed by Populous, it was the first major stadium in France built almost entirely with private funding. The all-seater bowl is modern, tight, and acoustically strong. Lyon have been one of France's most consistently successful clubs since the early 2000s, and the Groupama Stadium was built to a standard that matches their European ambitions. It hosted Euro 2016 group stage and knockout matches.

## 5. Stade Pierre-Mauroy, Lille

Capacity: 50,186 with the roof closed. LOSC Lille's home in Villeneuve d'Ascq, opened in 2012, is notable for being a retractable-roof stadium — one of the few in European football outside Scandinavia. The closed bowl allows concerts and events to operate in controlled conditions. Lille's 2011 Ligue 1 and Coupe de France double and their 2021 Ligue 1 title under Christophe Galtier were celebrated in a stadium that has since hosted Euro 2016 and Champions League group stage matches.

## 6. Stade Bollaert-Delelis, Lens

Capacity: approximately 38,223. RC Lens' home in the mining town of Lens has a reputation for producing one of the most intense atmospheres in French football, built on a mining community supporter culture that has sustained the club through periods in the lower divisions. Bollaert hosted the 1998 World Cup, including England's round of 16 loss to Argentina. The ground's compact oval design and tight stands create a concentration of noise that larger venues rarely achieve.

## 7. Allianz Riviera, Nice

Capacity: 35,624. OGC Nice's purpose-built stadium opened in 2013, replacing the old Stade du Ray. Designed by Wilmotte and Associates, the east-facing open side of the bowl frames a view of the surrounding hills on clear days. It hosted Euro 2016 group stage matches and serves as an alternative venue for the French national team. The beach proximity and Mediterranean setting give the stadium a context unlike any other in French football.

## 8. Matmut Atlantique, Bordeaux

Capacity: 42,115. Girondins de Bordeaux's purpose-built home, opened in 2015, designed by Herzog and de Meuron. The timber-clad exterior references the wine-producing context of the Bordeaux region. It hosted Euro 2016 group stage and quarterfinal matches. Bordeaux's subsequent financial difficulties and relegation have meant the stadium has operated well below its capacity potential in recent seasons, which has given it the slightly awkward status of a high-quality venue without a club commensurate with its infrastructure.

## 9. Stade Geoffroy-Guichard, Saint-Etienne

Capacity: 41,965. AS Saint-Etienne's home, known as Le Chaudron (the Cauldron), is consistently cited as one of France's most atmospheric club grounds. The enclosed bowl in an industrial city with a strong working-class football identity generates a noise level that belies the club's recent difficulties. Saint-Etienne were France's dominant club in the 1970s, reaching the European Cup final in 1976. Geoffroy-Guichard hosted 1998 World Cup and Euro 2016 matches. The green colour scheme of the ground — seats, facades, surrounds — is total.

## 10. Stade Yves-du-Manoir, Colombes

Capacity: approximately 14,000 (currently limited). The historical stadium at Colombes, in the western suburbs of Paris, hosted the 1924 Olympics athletics and football events, and served as France's main football venue for decades before the Parc des Princes and Stade de France were built. Extensively restored and pressed back into use for the Paris 2024 Olympic Games for hockey and field athletics events, Colombes represents the oldest surviving Olympic venue in active use in France.

## Euro 2016's stadium legacy

France's investment ahead of Euro 2016 — new grounds at Lyon and Nice, upgraded Velodrome, renovated Bollaert, Pierre-Mauroy, and Geoffroy-Guichard — produced a tournament with consistently high-quality venues. Unlike some host nations, France entered 2016 with strong club football demand for most of the new stadiums, which has sustained them in competitive use. The gap between Lyon's Groupama Stadium as a success and Matmut Atlantique in Bordeaux as a near-miss reflects the importance of matching stadium investment to club ambition.

## Ligue 1 and European competition

French football's competitive structure has been shaped by Paris Saint-Germain's Qatari-backed dominance since 2011. PSG have won the majority of Ligue 1 titles in the 2010s and 2020s, which has reduced domestic competition but sent significant transfer revenue into the market. The Parc des Princes has hosted UEFA knockout rounds against the continent's best clubs annually. Outside Paris, the Groupama Stadium in Lyon, the Velodrome in Marseille, and the Stade Pierre-Mauroy in Lille are the grounds most regularly associated with European competition. Lille's 2019 Europa League final run and Lyon's repeated Champions League quarterfinals in the 2000s and 2010s brought those grounds international profiles considerably beyond their domestic league standing.

## Getting to French stadiums

France's national rail network (SNCF) connects the major stadium cities well. The Stade de France is a direct RER journey from central Paris — Gare du Nord or Saint-Michel Notre-Dame — to Stade de France-Saint-Denis station, a ten-minute trip. The Velodrome in Marseille is accessible from Marseille Saint-Charles station by metro. The Groupama Stadium in Lyon requires the T7 tram from Part-Dieu station.
