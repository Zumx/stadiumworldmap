Slovak football has undergone significant stadium investment since the country's separation from Czechia in 1993. Bratislava's Tehelne Pole reconstruction, the renovation of regional grounds in Kosice and Nitra, and the broader modernisation of the Fortuna Liga have produced a stadium stock that is steadily improving without yet reaching central European standard. Every stadium below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Tehelne Pole, Bratislava

Capacity: 22,500. Slovakia's primary national stadium, rebuilt in 2019 on the site of the original 1940s ground. The new Tehelne Pole hosts the Slovakia national team and SK Slovan Bratislava — the country's most successful club, with Slovan having won the Slovak Super Liga repeatedly since the league's formation. The compact modern bowl is enclosed and represents the standard against which Slovakia's other grounds are measured.

## 2. Stadion Pasienky, Bratislava

Capacity: approximately 11,500. Bratislava's secondary stadium, used by Inter Bratislava and historically by Slovan during the construction of Tehelne Pole. The ground retains a Soviet-era character despite various renovations and remains a working second-tier and lower-division venue.

## 3. Stadion Pod Dubnom, Zilina

Capacity: 11,258. MSK Zilina's home in northwestern Slovakia. Zilina won three consecutive Slovak Super Liga titles (2010, 2012, 2017) and participated in UEFA Champions League group stages, with the ground hosting some notable European nights. The compact ground generates a good atmosphere.

## 4. Stadion Antona Malatinskeho, Trnava

Capacity: 18,448. Spartak Trnava's home in the historic town of Trnava, west of Bratislava. Spartak are one of Slovakia's most decorated clubs historically (multiple titles in the Czechoslovak era and recent Super Liga wins) and the renovated ground is one of Slovakia's best. The stadium has hosted Slovakia national team fixtures.

## 5. Stadion v Lokomotivy, Kosice

Capacity: approximately 10,000. The historic ground in Kosice, eastern Slovakia. FC Kosice were Slovakia's first club in the UEFA Champions League group stage (1997-98) and the city has produced consistent Slovak football despite its peripheral position. The ground has undergone repeated renovation phases.

## 6. Stadion MFK Ruzomberok

Capacity: 6,000. Ruzomberok's compact home in north-central Slovakia. MFK Ruzomberok won the Slovak Super Liga in 2006 and remain consistent in the top division. The ground is well-maintained for its size and reflects the regional Slovak football model.

## 7. Stadion Pod Cabokom, Senec

Capacity: 3,500. The municipal ground used by FC Senec. Senec is a small town near Bratislava and the ground reflects the modest infrastructure of Slovakia's smaller top-flight clubs.

## 8. Stadion Nitra

Capacity: 11,532. FC Nitra's home, recently renovated. Nitra are one of Slovakia's traditional clubs and the new ground brings the venue closer to modern standards.

## 9. Stadion Banska Bystrica

Capacity: 9,000. The central Slovak city of Banska Bystrica hosts MFK Banska Bystrica's home ground. The city's central position and reasonable population make it a logical Slovak second-tier football base.

## 10. Slovan Bratislava's training ground, Bratislava

Capacity: limited. The supporting infrastructure for Slovan Bratislava's youth academy and reserve team operations. Slovan's investment in player development under the Slovak corporate ownership has produced multiple Slovakia internationals through this system.

## The Fortuna Liga

Slovakia's top division — the Fortuna Liga — has 12 clubs and runs from July to May. SK Slovan Bratislava have been the dominant force since the late 2010s, with Spartak Trnava, MSK Zilina, and DAC Dunajska Streda as the main challengers. Slovakia produces a steady stream of players for the Czech Fortuna Liga, the Austrian Bundesliga, and the German Bundesliga, with talents like Marek Hamsik (Napoli, China, Sweden) and Milan Skriniar (Inter, PSG) representing the upper tier of the export.

## The Slovakia national team

Slovakia's only World Cup appearance as an independent nation came in 2010 (round of 16) and they have appeared at three European Championships — 2016, 2020, and 2024. The Euro 2024 round of 16 progression (lost to England in extra time after a late equaliser) showed the team's continued competitive level. Most home fixtures are played at Tehelne Pole.

## Visiting Slovak grounds

Bratislava is one hour by train from Vienna and two hours from Budapest, making Slovak fixtures an easy addition to a central European football trip. The Tehelne Pole is a short tram or bus ride from central Bratislava. Trnava, Zilina, and Kosice are reachable by Slovak rail (cestovna karta tickets work across the network).
