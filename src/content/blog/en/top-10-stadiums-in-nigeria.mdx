Nigeria is football-mad and the country's stadium landscape reflects two parallel realities: a small set of large national grounds capable of hosting Africa Cup of Nations matches, and a much larger set of provincial venues where Nigerian Premier Football League clubs play in front of regionally devoted crowds. The country has produced more African footballers in top European leagues than any other nation and the Super Eagles remain one of Africa's most consistently strong sides. ## 1. MKO Abiola National Stadium, Abuja

Capacity: 60,491. The Federal Republic's principal national stadium, opened in 2003 for the All-Africa Games and renamed in 2019 in honour of Moshood Kashimawo Olawale Abiola, the presumed winner of the annulled 1993 election. The bowl hosts Super Eagles matches and major Nigerian Football Federation events. The athletics track keeps front-row seats away from the pitch, but the venue's symbolic weight as Nigeria's national ground is unrivalled.

## 2. Godswill Akpabio International Stadium, Uyo

Capacity: 30,000. Often called the most beautiful stadium in West Africa, the Uyo ground opened in 2014 in Akwa Ibom State as a multi-use venue for football and athletics. The relatively compact bowl, clean concrete-and-steel construction, and modern facilities have made it the preferred Super Eagles home for several World Cup qualifying campaigns.

## 3. Teslim Balogun Stadium, Lagos

Capacity: 24,325. Lagos's principal football venue, located in Surulere, named after the Nigerian footballer Teslim Balogun. The stadium has hosted Super Eagles matches when Abuja was unavailable and is the home ground of Lagos-based clubs in the NPFL.

## 4. Nnamdi Azikiwe Stadium, Enugu

Capacity: 22,000. Named after Nigeria's first president, the Enugu stadium is the home of Rangers International, one of Nigeria's most historically significant clubs. The bowl has hosted Super Eagles fixtures and African Champions League matches.

## 5. Sani Abacha Stadium, Kano

Capacity: 25,000. Kano Pillars' home in northern Nigeria, named after a former military head of state. Kano Pillars are northern Nigeria's most popular club and the stadium regularly fills for derby matches against southern opposition.

## 6. Adamasingba Stadium, Ibadan

Capacity: 25,000. The Ibadan ground, also known as Lekan Salami Stadium, is home to Shooting Stars Sports Club — one of Nigerian football's most historic names, founded in 1976. The bowl has hosted CAF club competition matches.

## 7. Stephen Keshi Stadium, Asaba

Capacity: 22,000. Built in 2018 and renamed after the former Super Eagles captain and AFCON-winning coach Stephen Keshi, who died in 2016. The Delta State stadium has hosted Nigerian national team matches and continental club fixtures.

## 8. Lekan Salami Stadium, Ibadan (renovated)

Capacity: 15,000. Following its 2022 renovation, the venue (technically the same site as Adamasingba, though distinct in current branding) is used for Nigerian Premier Football League fixtures and hosted matches during 3SC's recent campaigns.

## 9. UJ Esuene Stadium, Calabar

Capacity: 16,000. Named after a former military governor, the Calabar venue has hosted Super Eagles matches and Calabar Rovers' home fixtures. The Cross River State setting has produced one of the more reliable Nigerian provincial football scenes.

## 10. Ahmadu Bello Stadium, Kaduna

Capacity: 25,000. Named after the first Premier of Northern Nigeria, the Kaduna stadium is a long-standing northern football venue and has hosted both Super Eagles fixtures and CAF club games involving Kaduna United.

## The Nigerian Premier Football League

The Nigerian Premier Football League has 20 clubs and runs a calendar shaped around the West African climate. The most historically successful clubs are Enyimba (multiple CAF Champions League titles), Rangers International, Shooting Stars, Kano Pillars, and Lobi Stars. Wage levels are low compared to North African leagues, which has driven the steady exodus of Nigerian talent to leagues outside Africa.

## The Super Eagles

The Super Eagles have won the Africa Cup of Nations three times (1980, 1994, 2013) and reached the World Cup six times. The team has produced generations of European-league stars — Jay-Jay Okocha, Nwankwo Kanu, John Obi Mikel, Victor Osimhen — whose careers traced through Italy, England, Spain, Turkey and beyond. The Godswill Akpabio Stadium in Uyo has become the home-game venue of choice when Abuja's MKO Abiola is unavailable. Both stadiums are marked on the [map](/map).
