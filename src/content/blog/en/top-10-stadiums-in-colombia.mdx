Colombian football is concentrated in three major cities — Bogotá, Medellín, and Cali — each with two rival clubs sharing the city's primary stadium. The Bogotá derby (Millonarios vs Santa Fe), the Paisa derby in Medellín (Atlético Nacional vs Independiente Medellín), and the Cali derby (América de Cali vs Deportivo Cali) define the country's club football. ## 1. Estadio Nemesio Camacho El Campín, Bogotá

Capacity: 36,343. Bogotá's main football ground at 2,640 metres altitude, opened in 1938 and the home of both Millonarios and Independiente Santa Fe. The altitude — exceeded among major South American grounds only by La Paz — is a significant home-field advantage. The Bogotá clásico between Millonarios and Santa Fe produces the matchday crowds and atmospheres for which the venue is celebrated.

## 2. Estadio Atanasio Girardot, Medellín

Capacity: 44,739. Medellín's main football ground in the El Estadio district, the home of Atlético Nacional and Independiente Medellín. The Paisa derby between the two clubs is among the most intense in South America. The ground hosted Atlético Nacional's 1989 and 2016 Copa Libertadores victories' decisive home legs. The bowl is steep, the acoustics excellent, and the ultras culture of the Los del Sur (Nacional) and Rexixtenxia Norte (DIM) shapes matchdays.

## 3. Estadio Pascual Guerrero, Cali

Capacity: 35,418. Cali's main football ground, opened in 1937 and the home of both América de Cali and Deportivo Cali (when not using their own Estadio Deportivo Cali). The Cali derby is one of South America's longest-running. The stadium was a venue for the 1986 and 2001 Copa América tournaments and continues to host major international fixtures.

## 4. Estadio Deportivo Cali (Estadio Palmaseca), Palmira

Capacity: 55,000. Deportivo Cali's own ground in Palmira, just outside Cali, opened in 2008. The bowl is one of the largest stadiums in Colombia and provides Deportivo with home matches outside the shared Pascual Guerrero. The 55,000 capacity gave the ground its position as the country's second-largest football venue when it opened.

## 5. Estadio Roberto Meléndez (Metropolitano de Barranquilla)

Capacity: 46,692. Barranquilla's stadium, the home of Junior in the Categoría Primera A and the primary venue for the Colombian national team. The ground's coastal Atlantic location and lower altitude — compared to the high-altitude grounds in Bogotá and Medellín — make it the preferred venue for Colombia's home fixtures when neutral ground for visiting teams is required.

## 6. Estadio General Santander, Cúcuta

Capacity: 42,000. The Cúcuta ground on the Venezuelan border. The stadium has hosted Colombian Categoría Primera A matches and Copa Libertadores fixtures. The cross-border setting and the Cúcuta Deportivo club's historical role in Colombian football give the venue continuing relevance despite the club's recent financial struggles.

## 7. Estadio Hernán Ramírez Villegas, Pereira

Capacity: 30,000. The Pereira ground in central Colombia's Coffee Region (Eje Cafetero). Deportivo Pereira's surprise Categoría Primera A title in 2022 brought the stadium back into the football spotlight after years on the periphery.

## 8. Estadio Olímpico Pascual Guerrero (athletics use), Cali

The dual-use configuration of Cali's Pascual Guerrero stadium, used for athletics and the 1971 Pan American Games among other events. Cali's status as a Colombian sports capital has been built partly on multi-purpose venues like this one.

## 9. Estadio Jaime Morón León, Cartagena

Capacity: 16,000. The Cartagena ground, the home of Real Cartagena. The Caribbean coastal setting and the historical significance of Cartagena as Colombia's colonial-era port distinguish the venue from interior Colombian grounds.

## 10. Estadio Alfonso López, Bucaramanga

Capacity: 28,000. Bucaramanga's ground in eastern Colombia, the home of Atlético Bucaramanga. The compact bowl and the recent investment in modernisation reflect Bucaramanga's emergence as a stable Categoría Primera A presence in the 2020s.

## The altitude factor and Colombian football

Colombian football is one of few national leagues defined by altitude. Bogotá's El Campín at 2,640 metres, Medellín's Atanasio Girardot at 1,500 metres, and Pasto's La Libertad at over 2,800 metres produce different physical demands on visiting players. The lower-altitude coastal grounds — Barranquilla, Cartagena — provide the neutral altitude conditions when international fixtures require them. The combination of altitude variation, six major derby clubs spread across three cities, and the country's continuing production of major players gives Colombia one of the most distinctive stadium geographies in South America.
