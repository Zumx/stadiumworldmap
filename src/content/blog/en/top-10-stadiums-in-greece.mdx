Greek stadium architecture sits on a strange continuum: the ancient stadium at Olympia is the etymological origin of the word, and the modern grounds of Athens and Thessaloniki carry both the weight of that lineage and the very Mediterranean ultras culture that defines Greek matchdays today. The Super League's intensity is concentrated in two cities — Athens and Thessaloniki — where derbies regularly produce some of the most volatile atmospheres in European football. ## 1. Athens Olympic Stadium (OAKA), Athens

Capacity: 69,618. The Spiros Louis Stadium at the Athens Olympic Sports Complex hosted the 2004 Olympic athletics programme and the football final, as well as the 2007 UEFA Champions League final. The 2002-04 renovation added Santiago Calatrava's distinctive double-arch roof structure, which became the defining image of the Athens Games. The bowl is large and the running track distances the front rows from the football pitch, which is why OAKA is now used primarily for international fixtures and one-off matches rather than weekly club football.

## 2. Karaiskakis Stadium, Piraeus

Capacity: 32,115. Olympiakos's home in the port city of Piraeus, rebuilt in 2003-04 for the Athens Olympics' football preliminary rounds. The new bowl was constructed on the site of the original 1895 ground (one of the oldest sports venues in the country). The compact, four-stand design produces excellent acoustics, and the Gate 7 ultras section generates the matchday atmosphere that has made Olympiakos the dominant Greek club of the 21st century. The view from the upper tier takes in the Aegean Sea.

## 3. Toumba Stadium, Thessaloniki

Capacity: 28,701. PAOK's home in the Toumba district of Thessaloniki since 1959. The ground is one of the most intimidating in European football: four steep stands close to the pitch, near-vertical sightlines from the upper rows, and a Gate 4 ultras section that produces sustained noise levels rare even by Mediterranean standards. PAOK's run to the 2017-18 Greek Super League title — their first in 33 years — produced some of Toumba's most celebrated nights.

## 4. AEK Arena – Agia Sophia, Athens

Capacity: 32,500. AEK Athens's modern home, opened in 2022 to replace the demolished old Nikos Goumas Stadium on the same site in Nea Filadelfeia. The new ground is named for Hagia Sophia in Constantinople — a reference to AEK's founding by Greek refugees from Asia Minor in 1924. The exterior features motifs that nod to that founding history. The bowl is steep, compact, and acoustically excellent, restoring AEK's home advantage after a long period of playing at OAKA.

## 5. Stadio Apostolos Nikolaidis, Athens

Capacity: 16,003. Panathinaikos's historic home at Leoforos Alexandras in central Athens. Built in 1922 — the oldest currently-used Greek football ground — the stadium is locally known simply as Leoforos. Panathinaikos played their home matches here for most of the 20th century before moving briefly to OAKA, then returning. The three-sided open-corner layout and the proximity of the upper tiers to the pitch produce an intense matchday atmosphere despite the relatively small capacity.

## 6. Kleanthis Vikelidis Stadium, Thessaloniki

Capacity: 28,000. Aris's home in Charilaou, Thessaloniki, named for the club's founder and one of the oldest continuously-used grounds in Greece. The stadium has hosted Aris matches since 1951 and has been progressively redeveloped without a full reconstruction. Aris's Super Greek presence has fluctuated, and the Super 3 ultras group is among the most active in the country.

## 7. Stadio Vassilis Konstantakopoulos, Crete

Capacity: 27,500. The home of OFI Crete in Heraklion. The ground hosted matches during Greece's Euro 2004 preparations and has been gradually modernised since. The Cretan setting and the volcanic atmosphere generated by the Gentile ultras have made it one of the more interesting venues for European clubs visiting in continental fixtures.

## 8. Pankritio Stadium, Heraklion

Capacity: 26,240. Built for the 2004 Athens Olympics' football competition and used by Crete clubs ever since. The compact modern bowl was a model for several other Greek municipal stadium upgrades. Pankritio has hosted Greek Cup matches and remains one of the country's better post-Olympic stadium legacies.

## 9. Asteras Tripolis Arena, Tripoli

Capacity: 7,608. The compact home of Asteras Tripolis in the central Peloponnese. The small capacity belies the ground's importance: Asteras have been a fixture of the Super Greek in the 21st century and their stadium provides an example of a small-town Greek venue whose intimacy generates atmospheres disproportionate to its size.

## 10. Stadio Georgios Karaiskakis, Old Phaliro

The original Karaiskakis Stadium on the same site as the modern Olympiakos ground (now demolished and rebuilt) was the venue of the 1896 Olympics velodrome and athletics events. The current stadium honours that lineage in its name. Visiting the site today means standing on ground that has hosted continuous athletic activity since the late 19th century — a continuity matched by few other places.

## Why Greek stadiums feel different

Greek matchdays carry an emotional intensity that surprises first-time visitors from northern European leagues. The pyrotechnics, the unified standing-and-singing of entire ends, and the proximity of the stands to the pitch produce experiences that combine Italian Serie A intensity with Balkan atmospheres. The ancient stadium at Olympia, the modern Karaiskakis at Piraeus, the volcanic Toumba in Thessaloniki — Greek venues span millennia but share an intensity that makes them collectively one of the most interesting national stadium portfolios in Europe.
