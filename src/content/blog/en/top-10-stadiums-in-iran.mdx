Iranian football is built around a small number of very large stadiums, with Tehran's Azadi at the centre. The Persian Gulf Pro League's biggest clubs — Persepolis, Esteghlal, Sepahan, Tractor and Foolad — draw crowds that fill the upper bowls of stadiums originally built for the 1974 Asian Games or for hosting national-team fixtures. Iran qualifies for the World Cup with regularity, and the atmosphere generated for AFC Champions League nights ranks among the most intense in Asia. Every ground listed below is on the [map](/map).

## 1. Azadi Stadium, Tehran

Capacity: 78,116. Iran's national stadium and the shared home of the country's two most popular clubs, Persepolis and Esteghlal. Built for the 1974 Asian Games and originally named Aryamehr, Azadi (the word means freedom) hosts the Tehran derby, a fixture that regularly produces complete sell-outs and the highest sustained noise levels recorded at any Asian club football match. The bowl is open at both ends, with a running track that pushes front-row seats back from the touchline, but the sheer volume of the crowd compensates.

## 2. Naghsh-e-Jahan Stadium, Isfahan

Capacity: 75,000. Home of Sepahan, the dominant club of central Iran and a regular Persian Gulf Pro League title contender. Construction stretched across more than two decades, with the stadium finally opening to its current capacity in 2003. The location on the outskirts of Isfahan is named after the historic square at the city's heart. Sepahan's home matches against the Tehran clubs draw crowds well above 60,000.

## 3. Yadegar-e-Emam Stadium, Tabriz

Capacity: 66,833. Home of Tractor SC, the most-supported club in northwestern Iran and one of the loudest grounds in the league. Tractor's supporters, drawn from Tabriz's Azerbaijani Turkic population, generate atmospheres that have made the stadium a feared away trip for visiting Tehran sides.

## 4. Pars Stadium, Shiraz

Capacity: 50,000. Opened in 2017 as the home of Fajr Sepasi and Bargh Shiraz, Pars Stadium replaced the smaller Hafezieh ground that had hosted football in Shiraz for decades. The modern concrete bowl is one of the few new builds in the post-2010 generation of Iranian stadiums.

## 5. Foolad Arena, Ahvaz

Capacity: 30,655. Foolad FC's purpose-built club ground in the southwestern city of Ahvaz, opened in 2017. The stadium is rare in Iran for being a football-specific ground without a running track, with front rows close to the touchline and a steeper rake than the older national-team venues.

## 6. Ghadir Stadium, Ahvaz

Capacity: 38,960. The older multi-purpose ground in Ahvaz, home to Esteghlal Khuzestan. The bowl is athletics-oriented but has hosted Persian Gulf Pro League matches and Iran national-team fixtures.

## 7. Takhti Stadium, Tehran

Capacity: 25,000. The older Tehran ground that predates Azadi, named after the Olympic wrestling champion Gholamreza Takhti. It served as Iran's primary national stadium in the 1950s and 1960s and remains in use for lower-division and youth-team football.

## 8. Imam Reza Stadium, Mashhad

Capacity: 27,700. Padideh FC's home in the religious city of Mashhad. The relatively recent build (opened 2017) gave eastern Iran a modern football ground after decades of reliance on older multi-purpose facilities.

## 9. Vatani Stadium, Qaem Shahr

Capacity: 15,000. Nassaji Mazandaran's home in the northern province of Mazandaran. Nassaji won the Hazfi Cup in 2022, the first major trophy for a club from outside the major-city axis.

## 10. Bahonar Stadium, Kerman

Capacity: 15,000. Mes Kerman's ground in the southeastern desert city of Kerman, a regular Persian Gulf Pro League and Azadegan League venue.

## Persian Gulf Pro League

The Persian Gulf Pro League runs 16 clubs over an August-to-May calendar. Persepolis and Esteghlal between them have won the majority of titles in the post-revolutionary era, with Sepahan and Tractor as the most consistent challengers from outside Tehran. The league exports players regularly to the Gulf and Turkish leagues, and the AFC Champions League produces several knockout-round Iranian appearances each cycle.

## Iran's atmosphere problem and its solution

The size of Iranian crowds — Azadi alone has hosted league attendances above 80,000 — is constrained primarily by the running tracks at the older stadiums, which push the front row back and reduce the acoustic intensity of the bowl. The Foolad Arena and Imam Reza Stadium are the first new builds to address this with football-specific geometry. Future Iranian stadiums are expected to follow the football-only template, although Azadi will likely remain in its current configuration for the foreseeable future.
